INTRODUCTION 
Pursuant to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission’s (“Commission”) October 27, 
2025 Notice Inviting Comments on the Secretary of Energy’s advance notice of proposed 
rulemaking (“ANOPR”)—issued under section 403 of the Department of Energy Reorganization 
Act on October 23, 2025—the Intervenor Center for Biological Diversity (“Center”) respectfully 
submits the following Reply Comments.1 The Center is a national, non-profit conservation 
 
1 See Oct. 27, 2025 Notice Inviting Comments, RM26-4; Nov. 7, 2025 Notice Granting Extension of Time, RM 26-4 
(setting Dec. 5, 2025 Reply deadline).

3 
 
organization with more than 1.8 million members and online activists committed to accelerating 
the renewable energy transition and protecting human health, the natural environment, and 
imperiled species from the worsening climate emergency, the extinction crisis, and 
environmental degradation. 
As discussed below, and as reflected in Opening Comments, the Commission’s 
consideration of the rapidly expanding energy needs of data centers must address both the 
challenges and opportunities associated with these facilities. With respect to challenges, the 
Commission must—consistent with its statutory obligations and as further detailed in the 
Addendum—fully account for the environmental implications of data-center growth, including 
risks to climate mitigation efforts and other significant environmental impacts, and require 
maximum transparency. With respect to opportunities, the Commission should examine how 
these facilities can contribute to grid reliability and efficiency through demand response, load 
flexibility, and maximized reliance on renewable energy resources. 
As regards more specific issues raised in the ANOPR, the Commission’s Notice, and 
Initial Comments, we have several comments. First, below we outline measures the Commission 
should adopt when evaluating any policy for expedited interconnection of large loads, to ensure 
that consumers are not required to subsidize data centers or related transmission and 
infrastructure costs that should appropriately be borne by the private corporations that benefit 
from such investments—and to prevent the creation of stranded assets. Second, we urge the 
Commission to ensure that these regulations capture the broad range of data centers by defining 
such facilities as those with a load starting at 20 MW, or a lower threshold, and extending to all 
higher loads, so that the variability across these operations is appropriately addressed. Third, we 
emphasize the need for a robust consultative process that meaningfully incorporates

Finally, we underscore the importance of deliberation in this rulemaking process. The

essential that this process remain thorough, transparent, and unhurried. 
In sum, the Commission must ensure that the interconnection of the substantial loads 
associated with these facilities is managed in a way that is fair to consumers, fully consistent

impacts of this rapidly expanding industry. 
DISCUSSION 
I. 
Threshold Issues The Commission Should Consider In Addressing The Matters 
Raised In This Proceeding. 
 
A. The Commission Should Consider The Environmental Implications Of Any 
Agency Rule That Encourages The Growth Of Data Centers And Other Large 
Load Facilities. 
 
As reflected in the comments already in the record, the growth of data centers for 
Artificial Intelligence (“AI”) and other purposes, as well as of cryptomining facilities, are key 
drivers of the DOE’s effort to expedite the interconnection of large loads to the country’s 
transmission system. Before turning to the specific issues raised in the ANOPR, Commission’s 
Notice, and the Initial Comments, it is vital to address the risks data centers and cryptomining 
facilities pose to the climate and the environment.2  
1. Data Centers And Cryptomining Threaten The Climate.

Data enters, AI, and cryptomining facilities demand enormous amounts of energy. The load 
growth these facilities are projected to bring is driving more fossil fuel generation, taking the 
nation in the completely wrong direction to address the climate emergency. The Commission has 
an obligation to take this strong connection between these facilities and fossil fuels into 
consideration as it considers appropriate regulatory structures. 
a. 
Data Centers, AI, and Crypto-Facilities Demand Seismic-Levels of 
Energy. 
 
As discussed in Opening Comments, data centers are highly energy- and water-intensive. 
These facilities host vast computing infrastructure—including server racks, networking 
equipment, backup power systems, physical security features, and advanced cooling 
technologies—to process massive amounts of data and support services like artificial 
intelligence, cryptocurrency mining, photo sharing, and video streaming.  
Cloud Service Providers, largely owned and operated by major technology corporations, 
currently control approximately 67 percent of global cloud infrastructure.3 This dominance, 
coupled with Big Tech’s strategic prioritization of the AI and cryptocurrency sectors, is fueling a 
rapid proliferation of conventional and AI-specific data centers, as well as cryptocurrency 
mining operations, across the United States. 
As of March 2025, the U.S. housed 5,426 recorded data centers.4 In 2023, these facilities 
consumed 176 terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity, or 4.4 percent of the country's total electricity 
use—more than the total energy consumption of Ohio in 2022.5 The energy demands of AI

announced their collaboration to build four new natural gas power plants, totaling more than 5 
gigawatts (GW), to provide energy to data centers supporting AI and GenAI operations in the 
ERCOT and PJM markets.12

undermining national efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

from paying any fees to the utility for delivering power. Constellation Energy leaned into this 
model to get out of paying federal and state-regulated delivery charges.91  
As for ratepayers, co-location has the consequence of precluding them from receiving 
lower delivery rates that they might otherwise have paid when a new energy-intensive customer 
becomes a utility ratepayer and pays its share of the utility’s cost of service. In the end, 
residential ratepayers could end up paying higher costs as those costs are no longer distributed 
across a greater number of customers. 
The data center boom is a big gamble. Contracts may fall through, and other less-energy 
intensive processing models for AI could become more mainstream. Moreover, load growth 
projections are still uncertain.92 Therefore, utilities’ quick moves to accommodate projected load 
growth comes with real consequences for residential customers who will shoulder the 
overwhelming costs of the nearly 20 GW of gas and transmission infrastructure utilities are 
building that may not ultimately be needed.93  The Commission has an obligation to protect 
consumers from these risks.

C. The Commission Should Engage in Tribal Consultation Before Fast‑Tracking

The Center concurs with Opening Comments discussing how the Commission must 
engage in meaningful consultation with federally recognized Tribes in considering reforms to 
expedite interconnection for large loads.98 Such consultation is not merely a policy preference 
but a requirement: Executive Order 13175 directs federal agencies to engage Tribes whenever 
proposed regulations have tribal implications. The Commission must therefore consult Tribes 
before fast-tracking data center or other large-load interconnections to uphold tribal sovereignty, 
address environmental justice concerns, and mitigate legal and operational risks. 
Tribal communities are among the most energy-vulnerable populations in the United 
States, with households facing energy burdens roughly 28% above the national average and an 
estimated 17,000 homes without electricity, affecting at least 54,000 people.99 Large energy-
intensive facilities like data centers and cryptomines can strain grid infrastructure, require new 
transmission lines, and increase fossil-fuel generation, creating disproportionate environmental 
and public health impacts on tribal lands. Embedding tribal consultation into interconnection 
reforms ensures respect for Tribal sovereignty, advances environmental justice, and equitably 
distributes the benefits and burdens of grid expansion.

D. The Rulemaking Process Should Be Deliberate, Not Rushed. 
Finally, it is imperative that the Commission prioritize thoughtful deliberation over 
meeting the DOE-requested deadline of April 30, 2026 for finalizing reforms to expedite large-
load interconnection.100 These rules will affect a wide-range of stakeholders—including utilities, 
ratepayers, Tribal nations, and environmental justice communities. The important social, 
economic, technical and public health implications of these regulations require careful 
consideration. Rushing the rulemaking process risks incomplete or poorly reasoned rules and can 
undermine public confidence. Moreover, perceived conflicts of interest or undue influence, even

sufficient time for notice, comment, and consultation is vital to ensuring meaningful 
participation, transparency, and fairness, and to developing enduring regulations that serve the
