Comments of Alliant Energy 
A. The Minimum Interconnection Size for Large Loads Should be Increased to at 
Least 100 MW  
 
The ANOPR proposes that any new rule apply to new gross or net loads greater than 20 
MW consistent with the Commission’s pro forma Large Generator Interconnection Procedures 
(“LGIP”) and Large Generator Interconnection Agreement (“LGIA”).5  Alliant Energy strongly 
recommends that the Commission increase the minimum applicability threshold from 20 MW to

at least 100 MW.  While aligning with the generator interconnection threshold has some appeal, 
the nature and scale of the loads driving this ANOPR, primarily hyperscale data centers, are 
fundamentally different from the smaller loads that would be captured by a 20 MW threshold.  
As such, the Commission should reconsider setting the threshold at 20 MW.  Such a low 
threshold would unnecessarily draw numerous smaller, less impactful projects into the 
interconnection process, creating backlogs, congestion, and delays that undermine the very 
objectives of the ANOPR.  By establishing a threshold of at least 100 MW, the Commission can 
ensure that interconnection resources are focused where they deliver the greatest system benefits.  
In addition, a 100 MW threshold enables the Commission, transmission providers, and 
operators to focus limited study resources on the largest and most complex loads with the 
greatest system impact, while exempting smaller industrial and commercial customers from 
unnecessary requirements.  This threshold reflects the scale of modern data centers and other 
large facilities driving these reforms and ensures that rules are tailored to their unique 
characteristics.  By concentrating on projects where system contributions are most significant, 
the Commission can create an efficient, fair, and forward-looking interconnection framework 
that supports timely integration and effective load management across the grid. 
B. The Study of Load and Generation Including Netting Should Account for 
Reliability, Affordability, and Customer Impacts 
 
The ANOPR proposes that, to the extent practicable, load and hybrid facilities should be 
studied together with generating facilities.6  The Alliant Energy Operating Companies are 
transmission dependent utilities (“TDU”) that do not directly own or operate transmission assets, 
which provides a unique perspective on transmission development.  While supporting necessary 
transmission build-out to maintain reliability and meet system needs remains a priority, TDUs

approach these decisions without bias towards transmission solutions and with a commitment to 
minimizing costs where possible.  Efficient siting of loads and generating facilities is essential, 
as the location of a large load near or at the same point of interconnection as a new generating 
facility can significantly reduce network upgrades.  However, it is critical for the Commission to 
recognize that simply assuming generation and load offset each other is insufficient and could 
jeopardize reliability, affordability, and customer interests.  Any study process the Commission 
implements should accurately account for the operational realities of combined load and 
generation at a single point of interconnection, ensuring reliability and cost-effectiveness for all 
customers.  
Reliability Considerations 
Generation and load interconnection studies serve different purposes.  Generally 
speaking, generation interconnection studies address power injection to confirm that the point of 
interconnection and surrounding system can support new generation.  In contrast, load 
interconnections focus on the ability to meet demand without destabilizing the grid and services 
for others.  When generation and load are netted, distinct study requirements and operational 
characteristics must be addressed.  For example, large AI data centers, with their size and 
variable demand, require careful analysis.  Misalignment between load and generation capacity 
factors at a shared interconnection point can lead to injections or withdrawals from the Bulk 
Electric System (“BES”), requiring full review of interconnection and network upgrades, 
including transmission service when load exceeds generation at hybrid facilities.  Study 
procedures and related agreements should incorporate relay and control schemes to limit net 
injections or withdrawals from the BES, ensuring reliability and system integrity.

No generation resource can deliver guaranteed, continuous output, so in hybrid 
configurations load and generation will not always align.  A full system impact study and 
necessary upgrades are required to account for the generator’s maximum injection when load is 
absent and to ensure the load is served when generation is unavailable.  Coordinating in-service 
and commissioning timelines for both components adds complexity, making it essential to study 
scenarios where one becomes operational without the other.  The Commission should require 
study processes and agreements to include clear operational controls and relay schemes that limit 
harmful net injections or withdrawals, thereby protecting reliability and maintaining system 
integrity. 
Affordability and Customer Impacts 
To protect customers from bearing costs caused by load at a hybrid facility using grid 
services when not self-generating, or when the anticipated load does not materialize, it is 
essential to determine the system upgrades required.  This determination should be based on a 
study of the potential impacts if full load or maximum generation were to import from or export 
to the grid.  Without such analysis and corresponding network upgrades, risks related to energy 
market costs, congestion management, and resource adequacy could be unfairly shifted to other 
customers. 
The Commission should adopt policies that require robust study protocols and clear 
modeling standards when evaluating combined load and generation at a single interconnection 
point.  These measures will help maintain reliability, protect affordability, and enable efficient 
siting without unintended consequences.  By addressing these risks proactively, the Commission 
can establish a framework that supports innovation while safeguarding system integrity and 
preserving progress already made.

Netting Proposal 
Alliant Energy cautions against the netting proposal in the ANOPR.  Netting assumes that 
load and generation at the same point of interconnection can offset one another for transmission 
planning purposes, but this oversimplification ignores critical operational realities.  Load and 
generation do not behave in a perfectly correlated manner—generation can be intermittent or 
unavailable, while load remains firm and must be served at all times.  Treating these resources as 
interchangeable risks underestimating system needs, which could compromise reliability and 
increase costs for all customers. 
The netting proposal places independent system operators (“ISOs”) and regional 
transmission operators (“RTOs”) in the untenable position of evaluating retail access rules 
governed by state-level policies, an area clearly outside their jurisdiction.  Rather than relying on 
flawed assumptions, the most effective way to meet growing demand from large loads like data 
centers is to accelerate the generation interconnection process.  MISO’s Expedited Resource 
Addition Study (“ERAS”) process illustrates how this can work in practice.7  ERAS prioritizes  a 
limited number of critical resource interconnections and studies them on an expedited timeline, 
enabling projects to advance quickly while ensuring both load and generation are accurately 
represented in planning models.   
The Alliant Energy Operating Companies currently have seven projects in the ERAS 
process, including two in the first study cycle.  Our experience shows that ERAS delivers exactly 
what is needed: timely studies that allow development activity to proceed and ensure system 
reliability.  Expedited interconnection processes like ERAS—not netting—are the right solution 
to integrate large loads efficiently and maintain a robust, reliable grid.

C. Use of Study Deposits and Readiness Requirements to Prevent Speculative 
Projects from Causing Delays in the Interconnection of Large Loads 
 
The ANOPR proposes applying standardized study deposits, readiness requirements, and 
withdrawals penalties to large loads, similar to those for generating facilities, and seeks 
comments on whether existing requirements should be adopted.8  Alliant Energy supports 
policies that encourage readiness for large load interconnections, including reasonable measures 
such as study deposits and readiness requirements, to prevent speculative projects from creating 
delays or backlogs.  These requirements, however, should be tailored to the unique 
characteristics of large loads rather than simply mirroring generator policies, ensuring that the 
process remains focused on projects prepared to advance. 
Study Deposits 
Alliant Energy supports implementing study deposits requirements for large load 
interconnection studies, as no such framework currently exists in MISO.  To assist the 
Commission in developing this framework, the study deposit structure established under Order 
No. 20239 for generator interconnections can serve as a useful guide.  While this framework 
provides a strong starting point, flexibility should be granted to transmission providers, and 
ISOs/RTOs to determine the appropriate study deposit size for large loads.  Applying a study 
deposit requirement can help ensure that only viable, commercially ready projects enter the study 
process, identify non-viable projects earlier, and shorten timelines, benefiting existing and future 
large load customers.  Upfront deposits can also provide greater certainty for interconnection

goals without undermining proven mechanisms that already facilitate timely interconnections.

D. Hybrid Facilities Issues for Large Load Interconnection Studies and 
Transmission Service Cost Allocation 
 
The ANOPR proposes that hybrid facilities be studied based on their net injection or 
withdrawal rights, that transmission service be based on withdrawal rights, and that ancillary 
services be based on peak demand without consideration of co-located generation.  While these 
concepts aim to address hybrid configurations, they raise critical reliability and cost allocation 
concerns that must be resolved in the final rule.  
First, when load and generation share the same point of interconnection, operational 
safeguards are essential to maintain grid reliability and prevent unintended impacts on system 
operations.  Key requirements include bidirectional flow control to block unintended injections 
or withdrawals, separate metering, and protection settings that isolate faults without affecting the 
transmission system.  Compliance with NERC planning criteria should be non-negotiable, and 
additional criteria should be established by the relevant ISO/RTO and transmission owner.  
Proper studies and identification of necessary network upgrades are fundamental to ensuring 
reliability and fairness, and hybrid loads must pay for the benefits provided by the BES.  
Second, the ANOPR also proposes inconsistent cost allocation—netting for transmission 
service while applying gross peak demand for ancillary services.  This approach lacks 
justification and creates unnecessary complexity.  Both transmission and ancillary services 
reflect the load’s reliance on the grid; therefore, the Commission should adopt a gross load 
approach for both, allowing exceptions only if supported by the utility and approved by the state 
regulator.  This method aligns with Commission precedent under the MISO Open Access 
Transmission Tariff (“OATT”), which follows the Order No. 888,11 pro forma OATT, the entire

Ancillary services are critical to maintain system reliability by supporting frequency

E. Curtailable Large Loads Should Receive Expedited Study Treatment and 
Flexibility in Curtailment Agreements  
 
The ANOPR’s proposal to expedite interconnection studies for large loads that agree to 
curtailment is a sound principle, as curtailable loads can enhance system reliability and 
affordability.  However, curtailable large loads should not be required to participate in a 
mandatory formal demand response program as it would unnecessarily hinder and discourage 
innovative solutions.  Large loads, particularly data centers, often have unique operational 
characteristics and contractual arrangements that do not align with standardized demand 
response frameworks.  Alliant Energy recommends that the Commission allow for flexible, 
bilateral agreements between large load customers and transmission providers.  Such agreements 
can define key curtailment terms, including amount, notice period, and duration, tailored to the 
customer’s capabilities and grid needs.  This approach promotes collaboration, accelerates 
readiness, and avoids imposing rigid program requirements that could create barriers to 
participation.  Alliant Energy recommends that the final rule clarify that eligibility for expedited 
study treatment can be based on these agreements, ensuring flexibility while advancing system-
wide benefits. 
F. Study Considerations for the Use of Existing Generation to Serve New Load 
The ANOPR proposes requiring an existing generating facility seeking partial suspension 
to serve a new co-located load to undergo a system support resource (“SSR”) or reliability must 
run (“RMR”) -type study.12  Alliant Energy supports a SSR or RMR-type study because it 
ensures potential reliability impacts and cost shifts are evaluated.  A study process provides the

G. Transition Rules for Implementing a New Large Load Interconnection Process 
The Commission should establish a clear transition point for implementing new processes 
under this rule.  At a minimum, large loads that have begun the formal interconnection process 
with the relevant transmission owner or provider by the rule’s effective date should continue 
without having to backtrack to meet new requirements.  Secretary Wright’s stated goal is to 
ensure large loads can “connect to the transmission system in a timely, orderly, and non-
discriminatory manner.”13  Alliant Energy Operating Companies have continued to achieve these 
goals in collaboration with ITC Midwest and ATC under current MISO rules.  Changing course 
at this stage would cause significant delays, drive up costs, and create uncertainty for projects 
already underway.  Large load interconnections often involve substantial planning, engineering, 
and contractual commitments.  Forcing these projects to restart under new rules could disrupt 
timelines, jeopardize customer commitments, and undermine confidence in the interconnection 
process.  However, maintaining continuity ensures that investments made under existing 
procedures are preserved, while still allowing new reforms to apply prospectively without 
penalizing projects that have acted in good faith under current rules.

H. Additional Considerations for the Large Load Interconnection Rulemaking 
Many states and load serving entities have already taken and continue to take significant 
steps to address the interconnection of large loads, and the Commission’s eventual final rule 
should not derail or counteract this progress.  To accomplish this, the Commission should 
continue its long-standing precedent, most recently reiterated in Order No. 2023,14 to evaluate 
obligations that result from any final rule in this proceeding under the independent entity 
variation standard for ISOs/RTOs and the consistent with or superior to standard for non-
ISO/RTO transmission providers.  Adopting these standards will facilitate a much more 
streamlined compliance and implementation process while hopefully avoiding overly 
prescriptive directives on this rapidly evolving issue. 
In the ANOPR, large loads that share a point of interconnection with new or existing 
generation facilities are termed “hybrid facilities.”15  The Commission should avoid using this 
term in the final rule, as it may create confusion with existing hybrid definitions in tariffs and 
other contexts.  Additionally, generation and load sharing an interconnection point can involve 
varied contractual arrangements, which could lead to inconsistent outcomes under a standardized 
process or conflict with state regulatory frameworks.  Instead, the Commission should describe
