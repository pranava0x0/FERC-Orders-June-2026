II. 
SUMMARY OF LEAN ENERGY US 
The Local Energy Aggregation Network (LEAN) Energy US is the national nonprofit 
organization representing nearly 20 million residential, commercial, and industrial electricity 
customers served by Community Choice Aggregation (CCA) programs in California, Illinois, 
Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Ohio, and Rhode Island.  In states that 
have restructured energy markets and enabled CCA under state law, these community-driven 
models empower local governments to purchase and develop power on behalf of their customers.  
While these programs are diverse in form and structure, they play a critical role in allowing 
thousands of local governments to take control of their energy procurement while collaborating 
with utilities.  CCAs protect consumers, drive innovation, provide competitive choices to 
customers, create jobs, and stimulate economic development nationwide. 
Many CCAs are prepared to serve data centers with reliable, and affordable energy or partner 
with them to provide additional energy solutions.  LEAN Energy US has an interest in FERC’s 
proceeding because it has the potential to establish rules and/or tariffs that determine how large 
loads are incentivized to structure their configurations, how they behave during the 
interconnection process, as well as how they interact with the grid long-term.  A beneficial 
approach that carefully considers the appropriate role of federal standards could result in data 
centers reducing system costs, adding flexibility and capacity, and enhancing the overall grid, 
while missing the full scope of this opportunity could result in additional grid strain, increasing 
costs, and reliability impacts.  CCAs stand ready to participate as partners and solutions 
providers, with our primary interest in maintaining our member communities’ local autonomy 
and low costs.

a. FERC Should Carefully Consider its Jurisdiction Over Load Interconnection 
to Provide Clarity for Active State Regulatory Actions 
As noted by many commenters,2 there is already significant progress being made at the 
state level to ensure data centers come online in a timely manner while “providing critical checks 
for resource adequacy, reliability, and affordability.” 3  Many CCAs across the U.S. are engaged 
in those state processes and agree with these commenters that FERC should be careful not to 
usurp traditional roles in this proceeding.  For example, forecasting occurs at the state and local 
levels, so federal standards, if any are established, need to consider how to best and most 
accurately inform state/local forecasting processes.  Like utilities, CCAs’ ability to timely 
procure cost-effective resources for their customers is underpinned by reliable and accurate 
forecasting.  
LEAN Energy US agrees that FERC should avoid simply asserting jurisdiction over large 
load interconnection in ways that could upend these traditional roles and relationships.  In fact, 
many commenters point out that significant benefits from FERC’s intervention here—such as 
improving the timeliness and accuracy of load forecasting and predictable interconnection 
processes—can be accomplished without the Commission asserting jurisdiction over load 
interconnection.4  LEAN Energy US agrees and encourages an approach that focuses on the

b. CCAs Can Provide Data Centers with Energy and Capacity Including 
Flexibility Outside the Fence-line of Their Facilities 
 
The potential for data centers to operate flexibly is a focus of both the ANOPR and 
commenters.  While data centers are improving their ability to adjust operations due to grid 
conditions, Microsoft points out that they currently have limited curtailment capability and notes 
that incentives will drive innovation.5   Data centers that co-locate storage or generating assets 
and have the ability to shift load in response to market or grid operating condition signals may be 
well-positioned to work with their local CCA to integrate their hybrid resources into a flexible 
load program that can benefit not only the data center as a customer, but all customers served by 
the CCA, by reducing peak demand through automated load reduction.  Further, commenters 
including EmeraldAI, Tesla, Rewiring America, and Voltus point out that flexibility need not be 
inherent to the facility itself.6  With their localized profiles, CCAs are well-positioned to serve 
the needs of data centers with a portfolio of reliable and affordable power options, including 
existing load flexibility programs that can scale and leverage local customer assets to meet peak 
demand.  However, these flexibilities can and should be incentivized in ways other than faster 
interconnection.  Flexibility can be considered a competitive product, and should be available

c. FERC Should Consider the Full Suite of Actions that Could Shift Costs to 
Existing Consumers 
Many commenters noted the importance of protecting existing customers from cost-
shifting.7  LEAN Energy US shares these concerns, as CCAs exist to provide consumers with 
competitive options that fit their energy needs and preferences.  As such, we have an interest in 
ensuring that markets are fairly designed to avoid passing the costs of one class of customer to 
another.  In particular, we wish to emphasize the comments of the Ohio Consumers’ Counsel in 
noting that the reliability studies required by Principle #10 are insufficient to protect consumers 
from the financial impacts of existing generators retiring from the market to instead serve more 
lucrative large load customers.8  FERC should consider alternative mechanisms to ensure it 
retains visibility over the full spectrum of actions that have the effect of raising consumer costs 
and ensure it adopts policies to mitigate such actions.
