REPLY COMMENTS OF VERRUS LLC 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Verrus LLC (“Verrus”) respectfully submits these Reply Comments to the Federal Energy 
Regulatory Commission (“Commission”) regarding the Advance Notice of Proposed 
Rulemaking (“ANOPR”) on the Interconnection of Large Loads to the Interstate Transmission 
System. 
As detailed in our initial comments, Verrus is a developer, owner, and operator of a 
first-of-its-kind fleet of purpose-built, flexible hyperscale data centers. Verrus brings a unique 
perspective to this proceeding because our facilities are designed from the ground up to function 
not merely as consumers of power, but as interactive grid assets. Unlike standard industrial loads 
that require firm, unvarying service, or traditional demand response participants that offer 
voluntary, behavioral reductions and that might vary from year to year, Verrus facilities integrate 
utility-scale battery storage and advanced load-management controls able to respond 
automatically to control area operator dispatch instructions similar to a central station generator 
to modulate demand in real-time. This “asset-backed” flexibility allows us to support grid 
reliability year in and year out for the duration of its interconnection and avoid network 
constraints in ways that traditional large loads and data centers historically cannot. 
Verrus continues to strongly support the Commission’s objectives in this proceeding. The

record developed in response to the ANOPR confirms that the current “one-size-fits-all”

Existing rules generally fail to distinguish between loads that strain the grid and loads that

infrastructure development. As the global economy increasingly depends on energy-intensive 
digital infrastructure, the United States’ ability to rapidly integrate these large loads is a key 
determinant of its economic and national security competitiveness. We cannot afford to leave 
existing grid capacity idle while critical industries and national objectives wait in line. 
To remedy this discrimination and unlock the speed-to-market required for the United 
States to maintain its technological leadership, Verrus urges the Commission to advance a Notice 
of Proposed Rulemaking (“NOPR”) that adopts four foundational reforms: 
1.​ Define “Flexible Load”: Establish a clear, national definition for asset-backed loads that 
provide long-term committed, verifiable, dispatchable  flexibility.

II. THE NEED FOR REFORM: ENDING DISCRIMINATION AND 
CROSS-SUBSIDIZATION 
The record demonstrates that the status quo—a patchwork of largely unfiled state and 
utility practices—is failing to maximize the efficiency of the transmission grid. And even where 
load interconnection rules are filed, by utilizing a single customer class and study process that 
fails to distinguish between flexible and inflexible loads, transmission providers are effectively 
requiring flexible resources to cross-subsidize their inflexible peers. This “one-size-fits-all” 
treatment is a classic form of undue discrimination: treating non-similarly situated customers as 
if they were the same. 
3 

--- PAGE 4 ---
A. Current Practices Force Cross-Subsidization and Stranded Investment 
As Verrus noted in its initial comments, flexible loads like ours can operate almost 
entirely within the existing headroom of the transmission system. By committing to curtail 
demand during peak constraint periods, we will, in most cases, avoid the need for new network 
upgrades. However, even in those cases where upgrades are not necessary, current 
interconnection procedures typically trigger network upgrade requirements and assign costs 
based on a load’s maximum theoretical coincident demand, ignoring its ability to curtail. 
This failure has two pernicious effects. First, it forces flexible loads to pay for 
transmission capacity they do not strictly need—effectively subsidizing the network upgrades 
required by inflexible loads that cannot modulate their consumption. Second, and perhaps more 
damaging to the public interest, it slows the deployment of highly beneficial flexible loads while 
also charging ratepayers and/or the specific customer for unnecessary infrastructure. When 
utilities ignore the curtailment capabilities and commitments of large loads, they build upgrades 
that could have been avoided and make customers wait in the process. This fails to maximize the 
utilization of current infrastructure, resulting in higher relative transmission rates for everyone. 
The converse is also true: connecting large flexible loads that utilize existing “headroom” 
spreads the fixed costs of the grid over a larger base, creating downward pressure on rates for all 
customers. By failing to prioritize these efficient interconnections, the current framework denies 
ratepayers the economic benefits of a more highly utilized transmission system. 
Regardless of how broadly the Commission draws the scope of its final rule, it must, at a 
minimum, ensure that it remediates this specific discrimination. A market structure that fails to 
value flexibility is a market that chooses higher costs and slower deployment.

B. Cost Allocation Must Recognize Avoided Upgrades 
The ANOPR proposes to assign 100% of network upgrade costs to new large loads.  If the 
Commission chooses to adopt this principle, it must ensure that a study process is in place that 
enables large loads to avoid those upgrades through firm, asset-backed commitments to curtail. 
Verrus strongly opposes proposals that would assign network upgrade costs directly to a large 
load customer if that customer has made commitments to exercise operational flexibility to avoid 
triggering the upgrade. Assigning the full cost of a network upgrade to a flexible load that has 
committed to curtail—and thus does not cause the need for the upgrade—would be unjust and 
unreasonable. 
The Commission has multiple pathways to ensure fairness, and should not consider 100% 
cost assignment to be the only option. It could create a separate “Flexible Large Load” 
interconnection class with distinct processes and transmission service cost structures, or establish 
separate classes of transmission service (e.g., conditional firm service) that large loads can select. 
Verrus urges the Commission to ensure that any conforming tariff structure: (1) allows flexible 
loads to avoid network upgrade cost assignment or allocation by committing to curtailment; and 
(2) assigns cost responsibility equitably, ensuring that flexible loads do not bear the burden of 
infrastructure required solely for inflexible demand.

IV. THE “FAST TRACK” IS JUSTIFIED BY EFFICIENCY AND ENGINEERING, NOT 
PREFERENCE 
Some commenters have characterized the ANOPR’s proposal for expedited studies as 
unfair. TAPS, for instance, argues that creating an expedited process for curtailable loads “raises 
serious concerns about undue discrimination,”  and ISO-NE cautions against broad expedited

processes.  These arguments, however, misunderstand the rationale for a “Fast Track.”

A. Faster Study is Efficiency, Not Favoritism 
Distinct study “lanes” are warranted because flexible loads can get online using the 
existing grid, which allows them to begin operating faster with less stranded cost risk to 
ratepayers. As the CAISO correctly notes in its comments, “it is generally easier to study and 
interconnect curtailable facilities.”  When a load commits to curtailing during system constraints, 
9
the scope of the required interconnection study narrows significantly. There is no need to study 
deep system reinforcements or complex thermal overloads if the load contractually and 
physically agrees to reduce load below levels that would trigger such upgrades  during those 
conditions. 
Therefore, an expedited timeline is not discriminatory “queue jumping” as some 
commenters have suggested—it simply reflects the reduced time required to study a simpler 
project and the efficiency gains realized when faster projects are allowed to move at a faster

B. Streamlining Flexible Loads Clears the Queue for Everyone 
Establishing a fast lane for flexible loads benefits the entire interconnection queue. By 
quickly processing loads that do not require network upgrades, transmission providers can clear 
the backlog and focus their scarce engineering resources on the complex projects that actually 
require deep study. 
Precedent for this approach is emerging. For example, Southwest Power Pool’s (“SPP”) 
recent High Impact Large Load Generation Assessment (“HILLGA”) proposal recognizes that 
integrating the study of load with electrically proximate generation/resources is more efficient 
than standalone regulation. As DTE Electric noted in its comments, this integrated approach can 
“identify opportunities for co-location that reduce the need for network upgrades . . . and 
accelerate the timeline for bringing both load and generation online.”

C. The Strategic Cost of “Slow” 
Finally, the Commission must recognize that speed is a strategic imperative. As noted in 
our introduction, the United States is in a race to deploy the digital infrastructure necessary for 
economic and technological leadership and to maintain national security. When a technical 
pathway exists to interconnect large loads quickly—because they do not require network 
upgrades—requiring them to proceed at the pace of the slowest, most complex projects is a 
policy error the country cannot afford. Where “fast” is obtainable through engineering, choosing 
“slow” through administrative inertia should not be an option. 
V. CONCLUSION 
The Commission has a critical opportunity to modernize the regulatory framework to 
reflect the realities of the modern grid. Flexible, asset-backed loads provide measurable public 
benefits: they stabilize the grid, defer costly infrastructure expansion, and unlock greater use of 
existing transmission capacity. 
Verrus respectfully requests that the Commission proceed with a NOPR that: 
1.​ Defines “Flexible Load” in a way that includes asset-backed resources capable of material 
(but not necessarily 100%) curtailment; 
2.​ Establishes a Fast-Track interconnection process for these loads based on their reduced 
system impact; 
3.​ Recognizes Flexibility as a reliability resource in planning and modeling; and 
4.​ Aligns Cost Allocation to ensure that flexible loads are not forced to subsidize the
