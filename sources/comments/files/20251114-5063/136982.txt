Dear Chairman Rosner and Commissioners:
VEIR Inc. (“VEIR”) appreciates the opportunity to submit comments to the Commission’s Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (“ANOPR”) in Docket No. RM26-4-000, issued to the DOE’s October 23, 2025 letter pursuant to Section 403 of the Department of Energy Organization Act, directing FERC to initiate rulemaking on the interconnection of large loads. 

As a U.S.-based company developing and manufacturing superconducting power cables for high-capacity, compact transmission and distribution applications; VEIR’s technology aligns closely with the policy goals underlying this rulemaking: to unlock large new loads (including hyperscale data centers and industrial electrification), improve interconnection speed, and reduce grid expansion bottlenecks.

1. Acknowledgement of Policy Intent
We commend DOE and FERC for recognizing that the growth of large loads (particularly in AI data centers, semiconductor fabrication facilities, and heavy manufacturing) requires a prompt, nondiscriminatory, and systematic transmission interconnection framework. The ANOPR (Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking) identifies loads above 20 MW seeking direct access to the transmission system deserve comparable procedural clarity to generator interconnections. VEIR supports the Commission initiating a rulemaking to develop a transparent, predictable, and technology-neutral framework for these connections, recognizing that timely action is essential for U.S. economic growth, competitiveness and grid resilience. 

2. Technology Imperative: Superconducting Systems as Enabling Infrastructure
In the context of this rulemaking, VEIR encourages FERC to consider how its future interconnection framework can be structured to recognize and include innovative cable technologies that substantially shift capacity, speed, and footprint:
•	Superconducting cables deliver multiple gigawatts of throughput in minimal physical corridors and allow for rapid deployment underground or at substation-campus connections-- features aligned with large-load interconnection needs in dense zones (e.g., data-center clusters, industrial zones, and urban areas).
•	The large-load interconnection regime can include both procedural mechanisms and technology-neutral performance standards (or incentives) that reward deployment of high-capacity, space-efficient systems. For example, expedited study tracks might apply for projects proposing such high-density conductors.
•	VEIR invites FERC to consider in the ANOPR stage: (a) whether study cost-allocation frameworks should recognize reduced ROW and environmental impact associated with innovative cable technology; (b) whether pilot or demonstration status of such technologies merits expedited interconnection treatment; and (c) how manufacturing scale-up of such systems may reduce long-lead supply-chain delays that currently hamper large-load attachments.

3. Recommendations for Implementation
VEIR is encouraged by the principles outlined in the ANOPR and particularly supports Principles 8 and 9, which highlight the value of allowing large load developers to build or fund their own network upgrades. We believe this flexibility can accelerate deployment and create space for innovative technical approaches to interconnection and co-location of load and generation. 
We also support exploring standardized data requirements and predictable study timelines for large-load projects, drawing on lessons from recent generator interconnection reforms.
Specifically, we recommend:
•	We ask FERC to consider creating a “low-impact interconnection study track” for projects that demonstrably reduce system or siting complexity—such as those using compact, underground, or non-emitting delivery technologies.
o	For example, advanced conductor systems like VEIR’s can deliver high-capacity connections within existing rights-of-way, avoid new overhead corridors, and minimize network reconfiguration needs. These characteristics reduce environmental and siting risks that often delay interconnection studies, allowing such projects to proceed through a more efficient review process.
•	Ensure cost-allocation frameworks acknowledge when interconnection upgrades are mitigated or avoided entirely by advanced conductors while allowing crediting for non-traditional upgrade paths.
•	Allow large loads (or load developers) to own or build portions of the interconnection infrastructure (including superconducting feeders) under engineer-and-construct options equal to those available to generators. This is consistent with DOE’s principle allowing self-build. 
•	Encourage DOE and FERC to coordinate with manufacturing and supply-chain support programs (e.g., 48C/45X tax credits, Advanced Manufacturing Office) so that large-load interconnection reform is paired with domestic cable manufacturing scale-up.

4. VEIR Commitment and Domestic Leadership
VEIR is expanding domestic manufacturing capacity for superconducting cable assemblies, terminations, and their associated cryogenic and support systems; thereby creating skilled U.S. jobs and strengthening the nation’s advanced energy manufacturing base. While VEIR sources high-temperature superconducting (HTS) tape from established suppliers, the company integrates these critical materials within the United States into fully engineered transmission and distribution systems. This approach shortens supply-chain lead times, reduces dependence on overseas fabrication, and accelerates the deployment of next-generation grid infrastructure that supports national reliability and resilience goals.
By integrating advanced conductors into the large-load interconnection framework, FERC and DOE can catalyze infrastructure build-out that meets the National goals for AI, data-center load growth, industrial onshoring, and grid resilience.

5. Conclusion
In closing, VEIR strongly supports the objectives of this rulemaking: to provide prompt, non-discriminatory access for large loads to the interstate transmission system, and to align interconnection processes with 21st-century load demands. We encourage FERC, in issuing its NOPR, to incorporate mechanisms that actively incentivize or accelerate deployment of enabling solutions like superconducting cable systems. Such alignment will maximize the ROI of the regulatory reform, de-risk large-load infrastructure, and ensure that U.S. grid evolution keeps pace with strategic economic growth. We look forward to engaging with DOE and FERC throughout this process and offering technical support to the rulemaking.
