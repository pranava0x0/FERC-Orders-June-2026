Initial Comments of L. Lynne Kiesling, Ph.D.
November 21, 2025
Executive Summary

This comment evaluates the Department of Energy’s Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) regarding large-load interconnection through the lens of transaction-cost economics, regulatory economics, and technological change. The rapid growth of AI data center load presents a critical coordination challenge due to the temporal mismatch between rapid data center deployment (18–24 months) and slow grid infrastructure expansion (7–10 years).
To address this pacing mismatch and ensure nondiscriminatory access, the Commission should adopt a modular, principles-based framework grounded in three economic propositions:
Flexibility as a System Value: Modern data centers possess significant latent flexibility through workload shifting, UPS-based frequency response, and hybrid on-site generation. Current interconnection rules treat these loads as static and inflexible, ignoring capabilities that could significantly reduce system stress and upgrade costs.
Reducing Transaction Costs: Legacy interconnection rules create high asset specificity and hold-up risks, driving developers toward inefficient bypass strategies. Co-location and hybrid configurations are rational economic responses to these high transaction costs ("make vs. buy" decisions) and should be facilitated rather than penalized by extending generator-style interconnection regimes to loads.
Foundational Cost Allocation Reform: The ANOPR’s suggestion of 100% participant funding for upgrades relies on flawed embedded-cost methodologies that ignore marginal impacts. Efficient cost recovery should instead follow Ramsey-Boiteux principles, allocating fixed costs inversely to elasticity. Because large loads are highly elastic and can relocate or self-supply, assigning them full upgrade costs encourages inefficient grid defection.
Recommendations: The Commission should establish a federal framework that lowers barriers to flexible resources, modernizes market participation rules for technologies like thermal energy storage (TES), and reforms cost allocation to reflect marginal impacts. This approach will encourage the integration of grid-supportive flexibility, reducing the need for costly infrastructure while ensuring reliability and competitive neutrality.
