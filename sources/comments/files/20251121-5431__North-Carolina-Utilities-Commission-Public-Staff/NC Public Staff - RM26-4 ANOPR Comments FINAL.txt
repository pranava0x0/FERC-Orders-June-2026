II. 
THE COMMISSION’S AUTHORITY 
A. 
Jurisdiction 
Interconnection of load into the transmission system might, in a technical sense, be within 
FERC’s jurisdiction; however, this is an area in which most, if not all, of the authority has been 
reserved to the states by Congress. Accordingly, asserting such jurisdiction should not be 
undertaken lightly, without sufficient time for thorough study and without exceedingly strong 
grounds. Even though section 201(a) of the Federal Power Act (“FPA”) grants FERC authority 
over the transmission of electricity in interstate commerce and the wholesale sale of energy, this 
authority is expressly limited: “such Federal regulation, however, to extend only to those matters 
which are not subject to regulation by the States.”3 Thus, federal authority to regulate interstate

transmission and wholesale sales of electricity does not convey or imply authority over related 
areas that are subject to regulation by the states, specifically energy generation and the terms, rates, 
and conditions of retail sales of electricity. As the Supreme Court has held, it is not a sufficient 
basis for federal jurisdiction merely for a rule to have a direct effect on wholesale electricity rates;4 
in addition, a jurisdictionally sound federal rule may not regulate retail electric sales.5 The ANOPR 
recognizes these jurisdictional lines.6 In the matter of interconnecting load, that leaves such a 
narrow path for lawful action as to be of little practical use. A Commission rule prescribing the 
methods, timing, and transmission pricing for interconnecting load to the transmission system 
could not, at the end of the day, provide any right for that load to be actually served as a retail 
customer, a matter unquestionably reserved to the states alone.7  
The primary impediment to the electric industry serving large loads is having sufficient 
reliable energy. Because federal jurisdiction does not extend to facilities used for the generation of 
electric energy,8 the rules envisioned by the ANOPR cannot reach the heart of the problem they 
wish to address. A transmission study of a load that cannot be served is of no value.

B. No Showing of Unjust, Unreasonable, or Unduly Discriminatory Rates 
The Commission may initiate complaints or rulemakings under FPA section 206 to remedy 
rates or practices that violate section 205 of the FPA by being unjust, unreasonable, or unduly 
discriminatory or preferential.9 However, to proceed under section 206, the Commission must have 
substantial record evidence that the existing rate is unjust and unreasonable and that the 
replacement tariff is just and reasonable.10 However, the ANOPR does not enumerate any such 
practices.  
The ANOPR suggests that the Commission’s landmark Order No. 888, issued pursuant to 
FPA sections 205 and 205, and which required transmission owners and operators to file tariffs 
providing open access to the transmission system, establishes the legal basis for applying similar 
open access rules to large load interconnections.11 However, they are fundamentally different. In 
Order No. 888, the Commission found that open access rules were needed to remedy undue 
discrimination by monopoly-owned transmission providers that had the ability to block electricity 
from being sold in interstate commerce.12 The Commission noted that the cost of the electricity 
being brought to market by new generating facilities was substantially lower than the embedded 
costs of the incumbent utilities’ generation.13 In contrast, rather than having an incentive to

III. 
COMMONSENSE REGULATION 
If the Commission determines that it has jurisdiction and authority to issue rules governing 
transmission-connected load, the North Carolina Public Staff urges it to do so judiciously.  
First, the Commission must ensure that any new rules do not reverse the hard-fought 
progress on generation interconnection. As noted, the primary and driving need for the bulk electric 
system reliability at this time is more generating resources. The ANOPR recommends that large 
load interconnections be studied with generation to the extent practicable.18 Even with reasonable 
study processes in place to minimize speculative projects entering the load queue, adding a new 
layer to the study process will inevitably cause restudies, delays, and inaccurate studies when 
projects inevitably either drop out of the queue or seek material changes to their load profiles. In 
its comments, Duke Energy Corporation has detailed some of the numerous complex details that 
would need to be addressed to ensure that the interconnection of large loads does not slow the 
interconnection of generation resources.19  
The North Carolina Public Staff recommends that if the Commission promulgates a rule 
on large load interconnection, that it limit its applicability to loads in excess of 300 MW. This 
would reduce the number of load customers requiring study and limit the projects studied to those 
most likely to have significant impacts on the bulk electric system. Further, it would reduce

As noted above, whether and on what terms and at what rates a retail customer can be served is a 
matter for state regulation.  
Bundled retail rates that include transmission should be preserved. When the Commission 
issued Order No. 888, it took no action to require these rates to be unbundled, in part to avoid 
difficult jurisdictional issues.21 This system has worked well for years and allows the North 
Carolina Public Staff and the North Carolina Utilities Commission greater transparency into 
transmission costs. At a time when electric rates are rising, with transmission costs rising faster 
than overall rates, statutory consumer advocates need to retain this tool to ensure they can fully 
perform their obligation to review and audit monopoly utility costs.

Third, the North Carolina Public Staff fully agrees with the fourteenth principle of the 
ANOPR that all NERC reliability standards must be met. As the Commission is no doubt aware, 
NERC is nearing the end of the work plan for its Large Loads Task Force.22 NERC has published 
for comment preliminary draft guidelines on managing large loads, which includes provisions on 
interconnection process.23 While NERC is assiduously studying reliability issues associated with 
serving large loads and preparing to issue guidelines, issuing final rules prior to those guidelines 
being finalized would be counterproductive.
