COMMENTS  
OF OLD DOMINION ELECTRIC COOPERATIVE 
 
Old Dominion Electric Cooperative (“ODEC”) appreciates the opportunity to provide 
comments responsive to the Notice Inviting Comments issued in the above-referenced proceeding 
on October 27, 2025, pertaining to the Secretary of Energy’s October 23, 2025, proposed advance 
notice of proposed rulemaking1 (“Proposed ANOPR”) made pursuant to section 403 of the 
Department of Energy Organization Act. 
This proceeding offers the Commission a unique opportunity to provide clarity and establish 
a durable framework for the interconnection of large loads to the transmission system. ODEC 
recognizes the national imperative to integrate emerging, energy-intensive facilities, such as data 
centers and advanced manufacturing facilities, onto the grid in a manner that supports reliability 
and economic growth. However, this pursuit must be achieved without shifting unjust or 
unreasonable costs onto incumbent native load customers, particularly residential and small 
commercial ratepayers. The Commission should use this opportunity to take measures to ensure 
that the costs associated with accommodating these extraordinary new demands are borne by the 
new large loads driving them, consistent with cost-causation principles.

I. 
Comments on the 14 Principles Included in the ANOPR 
 
The Secretary of Energy’s letter releasing the Proposed ANOPR for consideration and final 
action by the Commission emphasized the urgent need for timely, orderly, and non-discriminatory 
interconnection of large loads to the transmission system. In pursuit of these goals, the Proposed 
ANOPR includes 14 principles to inform the Commission's rulemaking procedures. ODEC’s 
comments on each principle, presented in the order outlined in the Proposed ANOPR, are set forth 
below. 
PRINCIPLE 1: “[T]he Commission’s jurisdiction should be limited to interconnections 
directly to transmission facilities, consistent with FERC’s seven-factor test.” 
 
ODEC supports this principle, provided that the Commission clarifies in the Final Rule that 
the determination whether interconnection facilities are transmission or local distribution must be 
made on a case-by-case, functional basis consistent with the Commission’s seven-factor test, as 
opposed to a bright-line rule such as voltage-based or how the facilities may be characterized for 
other purposes. The Proposed ANOPR cites to Cal. Pac. Elec. Co., 133 FERC ¶ 61,018, at P 45 
(2010) (“California Pacific”). In that case, the Commission advised that the primary function of a 
facility determines whether the facility is under the Commission’s jurisdiction.2 The Commission 
also noted that “the term ‘distribution’ is often confused with ‘local distribution’” and that while 
“local distribution” is a legal term under the Federal Power Act and the Commission does not have 
jurisdiction over “local distribution”, “distribution” is a vague term that usually refers to lower-
 
2 Id. at P 45.

voltage lines that are not networked and carry power in only one direction.3 As the Commission 
has observed, some lower-voltage facilities are “local distribution” facilities that are not under the 
Commission’s jurisdiction, but some lower voltage facilities are used for jurisdictional service and 
are subject to the Commission’s jurisdiction.4  
ODEC does not support a brightline rule that the Commission’s jurisdiction should be limited 
to interconnections directly to only those facilities that have been labeled or characterized as 
“transmission facilities.” While we agree with the need to distinguish between the Commission’s 
jurisdiction and the state/retail jurisdiction, ODEC is concerned that adopting or permitting a 
bright-line rule could be shortsighted. Such a limitation would overlook the downstream realities 
of integrating large new loads, particularly in electric cooperative service territories where 
interconnection of the new large-load facilities often occurs with lower-voltage distribution 
facilities between the large load and transmission facilities. While these distribution facilities may 
resemble transmission facilities due to their high-voltage capabilities, they are not part of the 
transmission system. Ignoring these complexities risks creating a gap in the Commission’s rules 
for large-load interconnections that will impede the Commission’s exercise of its jurisdiction while 
respecting the limits of its authority. Instead, as the Commission has struck this balance in Order 
No. 888 through the use of the seven-factor functional analysis, the Commission should adopt the 
same approach for large-load interconnections.

For example, ODEC is primarily a transmission-dependent wholesale electric utility. All 
eleven of our member distribution cooperatives depend on at least one third-party Transmission 
Owner (“TO”) to provide the transmission network that moves bulk power from generation sources 
to their distribution systems. For our standard connections serving residential and small 
commercial customers, the TO provides the connection point where our cooperative distribution 
members tie into the transmission system at voltages typically 115 kV or higher. The distribution 
cooperative owns the distribution substation, including the transformers with high-side voltage at 
transmission levels. This same approach is used when ODEC’s member cooperatives connect large 
loads. In these cases, the TO constructs a multi-terminal transmission switchyard, and the 
distribution cooperative builds a nearby distribution station with transformers and connects to the 
TO’s switchyard. Depending on the developer, either the distribution cooperative or the large load 
customer installs distribution-voltage circuits to distribution transformers outside the building, 
then runs lower-voltage cables to the customer’s switchgear inside the building. The distribution 
utility does not treat these substations as “dedicated facilities” and retains the option to serve other 
customers from them. For example, if the local sewer utility required a pumping station with a 
high-volume electrical load, it would be served from the same substation. 
As a result, limiting the Commission’s jurisdiction only to interconnections directly to 
“transmission facilities” would be difficult to interpret. The Commission should clarify that any 
such rule must be based upon the functional use analysis per the seven-factor test. 
PRINCIPLE 2: “. . . the reforms should only apply to new, large loads greater than 20 
MW, and for hybrid facilities with greater than 20 MW.” 
 
ODEC does not support the proposed 20 MW threshold for defining “large loads” and 
recommends that the Commission not follow the pro forma Large Generator Interconnection 
Agreement and Procedures in this regard. In our view, 20 MW is too low and would unnecessarily 

--- PAGE 5 ---
 
Page 5 of 14 
capture projects that do not present the same planning and reliability challenges as truly large 
loads. If the threshold is too low, it could lead to a greater number of load interconnections being 
processed under the large-load interconnection rules than is feasible or necessary. We believe a 
threshold of 50 MW is more appropriate and ensures that reforms focus on projects with a material 
impact on the bulk power system. A 50 MW load will almost certainly require the construction of 
a new substation facility, which represents a significant investment and planning effort at both the 
transmission and distribution levels. By contrast, a 20 MW load could theoretically be 
accommodated at an existing substation if sufficient capacity is available, meaning it does not 
necessarily trigger major infrastructure changes or regional reliability concerns. We also note that 
PJM’s initial proposal in its Critical Issue Fast Path on Large Load Additions set a threshold of 50 
MW or more to trigger additional limitations for new large load requests. To the extent individual 
transmission providers determine that there is a need for separate rules for interconnection for 
loads less than 50 MW, the Commission can review such proposals on a case-by-case basis. 
PRINCIPLE 3: “[L]oad and hybrid facilities should be studied together with generating 
facilities.” 
 
ODEC does not support studying load and hybrid facilities together with generation facilities. 
ODEC and the other PJM TOs jointly filed Comments in EL25-49-0005 stating that loads and 
resources should be metered separately, even if they are located on the same or adjacent parcels. 
Accurate metering is essential because transmission cost allocation, load forecasting, and market 
participation all depend on precise data. The Proposed ANOPR notes that joint studies would allow 
for efficient siting and minimize the need for costly network upgrades. However, efficient siting 
and accurate assessments are not mutually exclusive. Instead, there are compelling reasons to study

these facilities separately. Generators experience both planned and unplanned outages; thus, the 
transmission facilities must be evaluated with only the load operational, not the generation. 
Additionally, the generator and the load may retire at different times, so each must be able to stand 
alone in future planning scenarios. Transmission providers can assess planned and existing 
transmission facilities and network upgrades in these separate analyses, in order to mitigate against 
unnecessary network upgrades. 
It is also important to recognize that not all generators are equivalent. For example, a solar 
facility located next to an around-the-clock load does not offset demand after sunset. Moreover, 
the relationship between load and generation is not one-to-one. To accurately assess this match, 
factors such as Effective Load Carrying Capability (“ELCC”), ancillary services, fuel type, and 
storage must be considered. These complexities reinforce the need for separate, detailed studies 
rather than treating hybrid/co-located generation and load as a single entity to expedite the study 
process. 
While ODEC supports loads bringing their own generation, the general principle is that 
specific operational requirements should be considered, given the complexity of the daily grid 
interactions of these combined facilities. 
PRINCIPLE 4: “[L]oad and hybrid facilities should be subject to standardized study 
deposits, readiness requirements, and withdrawal penalties.” 
 
ODEC generally supports this principle but strongly recommends creating a large load queue, 
along with load forecasts and operating protocols, to help administer these requirements. In PJM, 
the Regional Transmission Organization already has standards that require sufficient transmission 
and distribution facilities to be in place before interconnecting the new load; thus, ODEC views it 
as a very reasonable extension to require that sufficient generation supply be available as well. We

rely on and support the comments submitted by the National Rural Electric Cooperative 
Association to address the jurisdictional nuances associated with this position. 
PRINCIPLE 5: “[H]ybrid facilities should be studied based on the amount of injection 
and/or withdrawal rights requested.” 
 
ODEC does not support studying hybrid facilities based on the injection/withdrawal rights 
requested because we believe it will cause issues stemming from generator outages and the 
retirement of load or generating facilities, as the transmission system will still need to be built to 
accommodate either the load or the generating facility operating independently in the future. The 
ambiguity regarding whether either the load or the generators may continue operating at full 
capacity when the other becomes unavailable is concerning, as it creates uncertainty for reliability, 
cost allocation, and market participation. 
During the PJM study process, we have observed that many projects initially proposed as 
solar with storage ultimately become solar-only projects, constituting a material modification. If 
projects are studied as hybrid facilities with combined load and generation, the concept of material 
modification should apply to both components. Any significant change to either the load or the 
generation should require the project to refile, ensuring that system studies remain accurate and 
reliable. 
PRINCIPLE 6: “[A]ny hybrid interconnection should be required to install system 
protection facilities necessary to prevent unauthorized injections or withdrawals that 
exceed the respective rights.” 
 
ODEC supports requiring hybrid interconnections to install system protection facilities. 
ODEC’s distribution cooperative members are already installing similar system-protection devices 
on storage facilities. 
Additional consideration should be given to the following complexity associated with system 
protection facilities. Most current system protection facilities trigger a complete disconnection 

--- PAGE 8 ---
 
Page 8 of 14 
from the grid during certain fault conditions. This type of protection scheme could result in a 
significant loss of load or a generation event. The design of interconnection facilities adds further 
complexity, such as large load campuses with multiple distribution substations serving facilities 
with 200–300 MW of load per substation to meet regional planning requirements and NERC loss-
of-load reporting guidelines (EOP-004-4). When generation is located at these sites, it may need 
to be connected separately because the load can be served by multiple transmission lines. This 
raises a critical concern: a generator outage should not cause a trip of loads served by transmission 
lines that are not electrically connected to that generator. Clear standards are needed to prevent 
cascading disconnections and ensure reliability in these complex configurations. The Final Rule 
should direct transmission providers or NERC to address these concerns and propose standards, as 
necessary.  
PRINCIPLE 7: “[T]he interconnection study of large loads that agree to be curtailable and 
hybrid facilities that agree to be curtailable and dispatchable should be expedited.” 
 
ODEC supports the encouragement of voluntary mechanisms to expedite interconnection 
studies, particularly for large loads that agree to be curtailable and for hybrid facilities that agree 
to be curtailable and dispatchable. However, these expedited processes must not compromise 
system reliability or allow standards to fall below acceptable levels. Any accelerated study 
approach should still conform to existing procedures and technical requirements, while permitting 
adjustments to study timelines to achieve faster results without sacrificing reliability. ODEC is 
skeptical that this could be achieved in 60 days. If the Final Rules adopts a requirement for 
expedited interconnection studies for large loads and hybrid facilities that agree to be curtailable 
and dispatchable, respectively, the Commission should leave to each transmission provider to 
determine the feasible time period for the expedited process, taking into account reliability and 
resources, as well as other generation and/or load interconnection queue processes.

PRINCIPLE 8: “[L]oad and hybrid facilities should be responsible for all network 
upgrades that they are assigned through the interconnection studies.” 
 
ODEC strongly supports allocating 100 percent of the interconnection costs to the large load 
and hybrid facilities assigned to them through the interconnection studies. This cost allocation 
principle should apply consistently to both PJM Supplemental Projects and Baseline RTEP 
projects. Additionally, ODEC neither supports nor opposes offsetting these costs through a 
crediting mechanism. FERC has long recognized a crediting mechanism for interconnecting 
customers bearing the upfront cost of network upgrades.6 However, the network upgrades 
necessary to reliably interconnect these new large loads are unprecedented. ODEC would support 
the Commission revisiting the applicability of the “but for” test from Order No. 2003 regarding 
the network upgrades needed to serve new large loads.7 The core rationale of the crediting 
mechanism contemplated by this principle and Order 2003 may be found not to apply to new 
interconnecting large loads and hybrid facilities, as any system reliability benefits are far 
outweighed by the direct benefit to the interconnecting customer. 
PRINCIPLE 9: “To the extent the interconnection customer is not the transmission owner, 
the interconnection customer shall be given the same or equivalent option to build as 
provided to generator interconnection customers.” 
 
ODEC supports an option for customers to interconnect and build their own interconnection 
facilities if it is a hybrid facility with a generation nameplate greater than the load amount (i.e., net 
injection to the grid only) and it is located on the same parcel with a single interconnection point, 
and as long as the load and generation are metered separately. However, we must also emphasize 
that the obligation to serve is the right of the local distribution utility. ODEC does not support

PRINCIPLE 10: “[A]n existing generating facility that seeks to enter a partial suspension 
to serve a new load at the same location must go through a system support 
resource/reliability must run type study.” 
 
If large loads seek to expedite interconnect by bringing their own generation, then using an 
existing generator by placing it in partial suspension does not solve the resource adequacy issue 
and therefore should not qualify as the large load bringing new generation. While ODEC does 
support an option for customers to interconnect and build their own generation if it is located on 
the same parcel with a single interconnection point, the generation should be “new.” This should 
not include any existing generators that deactivate all or part of their capacity to execute more 
lucrative large-load bilateral supply contracts with large loads that possess unlimited financial 
resources. The definition of new resources should be resources that have not previously injected 
energy into the grid. Some merchant generation companies have publicly stated that uprate costs 
exceeding a certain dollar threshold would trigger the entire resource being declared “new”. ODEC 
does not support this concept. It is simply a scheme that allows merchant generation companies to 
negotiate lucrative supply contracts with wealthy new large loads at the expense of everyday 
consumers. 
Additionally, any transmission costs determined by a deactivation study that would be done, 
should partial suspension be allowed, need to be directly charged to the large load.  
PRINCIPLE 11: “[U]tilities serving large loads, including those at hybrid facilities, should 
be responsible for transmission service based on their withdrawal rights.” 
 
ODEC does not support limiting utilities serving large loads' transmission service obligation 
based on their withdrawal rights. We believe the premise of this principle is impractical given the 

--- PAGE 11 ---
 
Page 11 of 14 
realities of operating and maintaining generation facilities. Generators will experience planned and 
unplanned outages, which are not likely contemplated in the facilities' withdrawal rights. The load 
will then request special treatment to allow it to operate during these outages. While the 
transmission system may be able to support some or all of the load, the large load will benefit from 
the transmission system without adequately contributing to its upkeep and maintenance over the 
life of the project. This principle will shift the cost to other ratepayers that should be borne by the 
large load. 
Additionally, the large load will not have gone through the studies required to determine if 
transmission upgrades were needed. Finally, the generator will probably not operate 24 hours per 
day, although the load may operate 24 hours per day. This mismatch in timing will require large 
loads to be studied and will result in transmission upgrades that entail maintenance and operating 
costs, which may not be covered by the withdrawal rights granted for the hybrid facility. 
The Commission should ensure that large loads pay their fair share of costs. Facilities served 
by dedicated generation and hybrid facilities should be responsible for contributing to the upkeep 
and maintenance of the transmission system based on peak demand, without consideration of any 
co-located generation facilities. To achieve this, large loads and generation resources must be 
metered separately so accurate data is available to allocate transmission costs appropriately. 
PRINCIPLE 12: “[U]tilities serving large loads, including those at hybrid facilities, should 
be responsible for ancillary services based on peak demand, without consideration of any 
co-located generation.” 
 
ODEC supports large loads being responsible for providing ancillary services based on peak 
demand. Even though large loads are generally considered flat, they are not constant. Their demand 
fluctuates with the time of day and seasonal temperature changes, creating a need for ancillary 
services, such as regulation and reserves, to maintain system balance. Building a generator to serve 

--- PAGE 12 ---
 
Page 12 of 14 
a large load must address the entire challenge. Generators are rated by fuel type, storage, and 
performance history, and in PJM, they receive an ELCC value that is lower than the nameplate 
capacity of the resource. To match a large load’s energy requirements, the load demand should be 
compared to the generator’s ELCC value, not its nameplate rating. But that comparison doesn’t 
include the ancillary services, which remain essential. The same resource constraints that exist for 
energy and capacity also apply to ancillary services. New generators should provide enough 
capacity to contribute to ancillary service pools, and the only way to ensure proper compensation 
and cost allocation is to meter the generator and the large load separately. 
PRINCIPLE 13: “[Need for a transition] plan to implement proposed reforms.” 
 
The build cycle for large loads and generation is not aligned. Large loads can be ready in 
roughly half the time it takes to bring generation online, and this does not account for transmission 
upgrade timelines. While loads can physically interconnect at any time, capacity may not be 
available, and adding a significant load without additional resources risks system disruption due 
to frequency and voltage variations that can trigger cascading failures. It will also drive up energy 
prices as older, higher-cost generators are dispatched, impacting all consumers. Any transition plan 
must address resource adequacy for both system stability and consumer costs. If large loads are 
allowed to interconnect without bringing their own capacity resources or before the system has 
adequate capacity, they must accept curtailment obligations to protect reliability. Existing 
consumers who have paid for the current generation should be shielded from cost impacts through 
hedging or other mechanisms, without regulatory barriers. 
PRINCIPLE 14: “[U]tilities serving large loads must meet all applicable NERC reliability 
standards and OATT provisions.” 
 
ODEC supports requiring utilities serving large loads to meet all applicable NERC reliability 
standards and OATT provisions. Utilities and large loads must comply with all applicable

Page 13 of 14 
reliability standards, and to achieve this, these loads should be registered with NERC. Furthermore, 
existing standards should be enhanced to address the additional operational complexities that large 
loads introduce to the grid. 
II. Conclusion 
 
 
WHEREFORE, for the foregoing reasons, ODEC urges the Commission to adopt an 
approach that aligns cost responsibility with cost causation, protects incumbent customers, and 
advances reliability as its foremost obligation.
