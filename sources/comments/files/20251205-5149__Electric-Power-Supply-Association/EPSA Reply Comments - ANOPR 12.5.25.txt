B. Attempts to Pivot Large Load Interconnection Concerns to Support 
Utility Self-Build of Generation Must be Dismissed 
 
Regrettably, this proceeding is being utilized as an opportunity to press 
unwarranted attacks on our nation’s competitive power markets in an attempt to hijack 
this critical emergent concern to dismantle competitive mechanisms and allow 
monopolistic behavior to flourish further. Ignoring years of market price signals that 
justified generation retirements, transmission owners – particularly in PJM – now argue 
this crisis can be resolved by state-led processes that would allow these transmission 
owners to build and rate base new generation.8 
While it is clear that new generation of all stripes is needed to meet growing 
demand from large loads as well as other power customers, this does not call for the 
degradation of the organized ISO/RTO markets whose purpose was outlined more than 
25 years ago in Order No. 2000, implementing wholesale competition to lead to least 
cost reliability outcomes.9 Fundamentally, monopoly ownership stifles competition, 
increases consumer costs, distorts markets, and unfairly shifts financial risk from 
investors to ratepayers. Allowing transmission owners to return to the business of 
building and operating rate-based generation after Order No. 2000 was established to 
resolve massive cost overruns and poor operating performance under the previous 
regime should not be part of any solution. Data from PJM drives this point home as, 
despite recent increases in capacity market prices, entities under the Fixed Resource

C. Concerns Raised Regarding FPA Section 212(h) are Not Germane to the 
ANOPR’s Goals 
 
A small number of commenters raise concerns that FPA Section 212(h) limits the 
Commission’s authority over transmission in states that have not required unbundling of 
retail transmission.15 That statutory language addresses retail wheeling and includes 
the following language: “Nothing in this subsection shall affect any authority of any State 
or local government under State law concerning the transmission of electric energy 
directly to an ultimate consumer.”16 Commenters extrapolate this language to present a 
limitation to the Commission’s authority over large load interconnection to the 
transmission system.  
These arguments incorrectly conflate the wheeling/delivery and interconnection 
components of transmission service. As discussed in the EPSA initial comments, FERC 
has found interconnection service to be a transmission service and has recognized that 
it is separate and distinct from wheeling/delivery. In Order No. 2003, for instance, the 
Commission made clear that “an Interconnection Customer need not enter into an 
agreement for the delivery component of transmission service to interconnect with a 
Transmission Providers' Transmission System,” and further that “Interconnection 
Service or an interconnection by itself does not confer any delivery rights from the 
Generating facility to any points of delivery.”17

While FPA Section 212 does impact orders requiring interconnection or wheeling, 
the Commission’s authority to require those types of interconnections arises under 
separate statutory provisions – FPA Sections 210 and 211. Section 210 relates to 
FERC’s “[i]nterconnection authority,” authorizing the Commission to order the “physical 
connection” of an applicant’s facilities with those of an “electric utility.” Section 211 
relates to FERC’s “[w]heeling authority,” authorizing the Commission to order a 
“transmitting utility” to provide transmission delivery service to an applicant. The 
Commission has consistently made clear that the two are separate, and that restrictions 
on its powers to order wheeling do not apply where an applicant has only sought 
interconnection service.18 
The arguments raising section 212(h) concerns attempt to establish a retail 
“piece” of the connection which then grants transmission owners a blanket lever for 
control over the interconnection – a scenario which, based on the ANOPR’s reasoning 
and the additional legal support included in EPSA’s initial comments, runs contrary to 
the statutory intent and the goal of the ANOPR itself.  
This reading also glosses over distinctions that may exist in various large load 
and generation configurations. There are existing scenarios, for instance, in which the 
large load customer owns the 230 kV lines that connect a generator to a data center. 
This could bode for voltage specificity in a proposed rule but certainly demonstrates that
