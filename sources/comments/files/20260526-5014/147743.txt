

Lauren Hopkins, Clearfield, PA.
I am a residential ratepayer in Clearfield County, Pennsylvania served by Penelec. I am writing because my electric bill is proof of the unfair and systematic transfer of costs from large commercial electricity consumers to residential ratepayers.

Penelec's residential Price to Compare has increased from 4.981 cents per kWh in March 2021 to 11.747 cents per kWh in December 2025. An additional 11.88% increase will go into effect June 1, 2026. My bill specifically has gone from $167 a month in May 2021 to $349 this month. That's an additional $2,184 a year I have to pay, and doesn't even take into consideration the rate increase coming next month.

The PJM independent market monitor has attributed 63% of the 833% capacity auction price increase between the 2024-2025 and 2025-2026 delivery years to data center electricity demand. This translates to $9.3 billion in costs being paid by 67 million ratepayers across the 13 PJM states. The Union of Concerned Scientists identified an additional $4.3 billion in transmission interconnection costs previously obscured in utility filings, connecting private data centers directly to the high-voltage transmission system.

The data centers driving these costs are concentrated in Northern Virginia and Maryland. Any jobs, tax revenue, and economic activity created by those facilities remain in Virginia and Maryland, but the costs are socialized onto me and the other millions of ratepayers in PJM states.

As a residential customer in rural central Pennsylvania, I receive no economic benefit from data centers in Virginia or Maryland. What I have received is $2,184 in additional yearly costs that will continue to increase. I should not have to pay my money to subsidize costs that large corporations are responsible for.

What I am asking FERC to do:

First, require that large loads, specifically data centers and other high-density commercial facilities, bear the full cost of transmission infrastructure required to interconnect them to the interstate transmission system, rather than socializing those costs across existing ratepayer classes.

Second, require that PJM's capacity auction cost allocation methodology distinguish between infrastructure serving broad public load and infrastructure serving concentrated private commercial loads. The current framework treats these identically. They are not identical.

Third, require utilities to disclose on residential bills the portion of supply and transmission charges attributable to large load interconnection costs. Ratepayers currently have no visibility into why their bills are rising. Transparency is a prerequisite for the kind of informed public participation that legitimate regulatory proceedings require.

Fourth, act before the Shapiro price cap expires in 2030. The cap has provided temporary protection for Pennsylvania ratepayers. Without action, costs will increase even more.

The Federal Power Act requires that rates be just and reasonable. Requiring residential ratepayers in rural Pennsylvania to subsidize the electricity infrastructure of Fortune 500 technology companies generating no local economic benefit is neither just nor reasonable. I respectfully urge the Commission to act.

Respectfully submitted, 
Lauren Hopkins
