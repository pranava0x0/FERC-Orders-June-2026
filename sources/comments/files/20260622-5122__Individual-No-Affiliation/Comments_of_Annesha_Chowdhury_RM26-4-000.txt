I file these comments in my individual capacity as a technologist working directly on the problem 
this proceeding addresses: how to integrate large new loads, including AI data centers, onto a 
constrained  transmission  system  in  a  manner  that  is  timely,  orderly,  reliable,  and  non-
discriminatory.  My  comments  focus  on  a  specific,  implementable  opportunity  that  the 
Commission's record has not yet fully developed: the use of AI-assisted dynamic line rating and 
physics-constrained  study  automation  to  accelerate  large-load  interconnection  without 
compromising reliability.
II. Summary of Recommendations
1. The Commission should recognize AI-assisted dynamic transmission line rating, built on the 
ambient-adjusted and dynamic rating foundation of Order No. 881, as a near-term tool for 
surfacing latent transmission headroom that can accommodate large loads without waiting for new 
line construction.
2. Any acceleration of interconnection or System Impact Study timelines toward the proposed 60-
day target should be paired with a requirement that automated or AI-assisted study tools be 
validated against deterministic, physics-based engineering models, so that speed does not come at 
the cost of reliability.

3. The Commission should encourage standardized telemetry and data-exchange practices that 
make AI-assisted rating and study acceleration auditable and reproducible across transmission 
providers.
4. Flexible and curtailable large loads should be evaluated using real-time, consequence-aware 
analytics, which can quantify the reliability value of their flexibility and support the faster study 
track the ANOPR contemplates.
III. Discussion
A. AI-assisted dynamic line rating can surface existing headroom and reduce the need to 
defer large loads pending new construction.
A significant share of the interconnection delay facing large loads is not a physical absence of 
capacity but an inability to see and use capacity that already exists. Transmission lines are 
conventionally rated conservatively using static assumptions. Order No. 881's move toward 
ambient-adjusted ratings established that ratings can and should reflect actual conditions. Building 
on that foundation, AI methods that fuse real-time weather data with the IEEE 738 heat-balance 
model can identify dynamic headroom on existing lines, which can meaningfully exceed static 
ratings under favorable conditions. Where that latent headroom can be safely used, large loads can 
be integrated faster and at lower cost than approaches that assume new construction is always 
required. I recommend that the Commission expressly acknowledge AI-assisted dynamic line 
rating as an eligible tool in the large-load integration toolkit.
B. Faster study timelines must be coupled with physics-based validation to protect reliability.
The  ANOPR  asks  whether  interconnection  studies  could  be  completed  within  60  days. 
Compressing study timelines is achievable with software and machine learning, but pure data-
driven models can produce results that are statistically plausible yet physically invalid, which in 
grid operations can translate into reliability risk. The sound path is a hybrid, physics-constrained 
approach: machine-learning components accelerate data ingestion and scenario generation, while a 
deterministic engineering layer, such as power-flow and thermal models, validates every result 
against physical law before it is relied upon. I recommend that, to the extent the Commission 
encourages automated or AI-assisted study acceleration, it also direct that such tools incorporate a 
physics-based validation layer and remain auditable, so that faster studies remain trustworthy 
studies.
C. Flexibility and curtailment commitments should be evaluated with real-time, consequence-
aware analytics.

The ANOPR contemplates a faster track for large loads that agree to be flexible and curtail usage. 
Determining how much reliability value a given flexibility commitment actually provides is itself 
an analytical problem well suited to real-time modeling. Consequence-aware analytics can 
quantify, for a specific location and system state, the headroom that a curtailable load frees up and 
the reliability margin its flexibility preserves. Embedding this kind of analysis in the faster study 
track would let the Commission and transmission providers extend expedited treatment on a 
principled, measurable basis rather than a categorical one.
D. Standardized, auditable data practices will make these tools usable across providers.
The benefits described above depend on consistent access to the underlying telemetry, including 
line conditions, weather inputs, and topology data. I recommend that the Commission encourage 
standardized data-exchange and documentation practices for any AI-assisted rating or study tools, 
so that their inputs and outputs are reproducible and auditable by transmission providers, 
regulators, and affected stakeholders. Standardization also lowers the barrier for smaller providers 
to adopt these methods.
IV. Conclusion
The need to integrate large loads, including AI data centers, in a timely and orderly manner is 
urgent, and tools to do so reliably are largely available today. AI-assisted dynamic line rating and 
physics-constrained study automation can help the Commission meet its timeliness goals while 
preserving the reliability and non-discrimination principles at the center of this proceeding. I 
appreciate the opportunity to comment and am available to provide further technical detail at the 
Commission's request.
