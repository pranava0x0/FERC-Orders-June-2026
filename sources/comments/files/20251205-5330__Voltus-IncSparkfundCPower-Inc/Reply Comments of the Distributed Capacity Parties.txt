INDUSTRY AND THOUGHT LEADERS SUPPORT EXPEDITED INTERCONNECTION 
FOR LOADS PAIRING WITH ADJACENT FLEXIBILITY 
As the Distributed Capacity Parties noted in initial comments, the ANOPR explicitly and 
laudably recognizes the value of load flexibility and “hybrid” facility dispatchability in Principle 
Seven, suggesting loads bringing these attributes should be prioritized for interconnection. The 
Distributed Capacity Parties wrote that the new capacity need not be physically located at the 
same facility, but “can be distributed across the geographic area within which the new load will 
impact system constraints, provided that the system operator has the requisite level of control to 
integrate the facility into operations and system planning.”5 
A variety of parties agree. In the context of new generation brought by specific large 
loads, Exelon notes that “[s]uch resources may share a point of interconnection or be in close 
(electrical) proximity to one another . . . and may warrant accelerated treatment to connect to the

Rewiring America writes,  
FERC should expand its expedited interconnection considerations beyond large 
load curtailability and co-located generation dispatchability to include 
appropriately located off-site distributed energy resources that can deliver 
curtailment or dispatch services under contract. These contracted resources should 
be evaluated as part of the interconnection package when they provide generation 
and/or transmission relief that offsets the impacts of the large load request. In this 
context, what matters is equivalent, verifiable performance in mitigating 
constraints within the same constrained transmission pocket or local grid area, not 
whether the asset is physically inside or outside the facility fence line.8  
 
Rewiring America expands the analysis to ANOPR Principle Three:

Base Power encourages the Commission to recognize that resource adequacy is 
not unilaterally bound by the physical location of the asset providing it. Resources 
capable of modulating their consumption or injection of power in response to 
system needs on demand, whether located at the load site or elsewhere on the 
system, offer functionally identical value to reliability and grid stability where 
modeled and verified in the course of typical, day-to-day grid operating 
conditions. The defining feature of resource adequacy is dispatchability, not 
geography. A resource that can increase or decrease its output or load in response 
to an operator signal provides the same system benefit whether it is co-located 
with the facility it serves or operates under a contractual arrangement elsewhere. 
Therefore, constraining generation in service of supporting new load 
interconnection dispatchability to a narrow class of assets would unnecessarily 
exclude a significant portion of capacity already supporting grid operation.11 
 
As these comments by innovative energy storage companies demonstrate, unlocking the 
full potential capacity that can be provided by advanced technology developers requires 
recognizing that appropriately located off-site resources provide network benefits on par with 
those provided by on-site resources, and should be studied and interconnected with equivalent 
priority. 
Emerald AI, a leading enabler of “on-site” flexibility of AI data center compute, 
nonetheless includes adjacent or offsite flexibility in its four-category classification of data 
center flexibility.12 Emerald AI notes the final category can “complement and augment the total 
flexibility a facility can offer. Such adjacent flexibility refers to the ability to coordinate with 
nearby or aggregated resources (e.g., a local VPP) via a software interface, so that the net effect 
reduces withdrawals on constrained parts of the grid.”13

Tesla highlights the value of distributed capacity, “urg[ing] the recognition of DERs and 
VPPs as meaningful grid service tools that can support faster interconnection of large loads to the 
transmission system. VPPs should be considered for their potential to alleviate transmission 
network constraints and capacity needs associated with the interconnection of large loads.”14 
DERs and VPPs are by definition not, at least in entirety, physically located on the site of new 
large loads seeking interconnection; Tesla’s position emphasizes the need for the expansive and 
comprehensive consideration of new off-site capacity whose network locations ease relevant grid 
constraints, with the instant proceeding offering an unprecedented opportunity to “unlock a new 
and massive category of untapped capacity to solve the growing pressure on the transmission 
system.”15  
With respect to curtailment resources specifically, the Joint Flexibility Parties add 
another dimension to the discussion, noting that “[n]ot all large loads will have the appetite to be 
curtailable, but all large loads will almost certainly be interested in having access to an expedited 
interconnection process. If another load or loads that are sited within the same stressed grid area 
can provide the necessary flexibility, then the large load should be considered for an expedited 
interconnection upon showing that it has entered into arrangements for such flexibility.”16 
The preceding excerpts highlight only a subset of the numerous stakeholder comments

expedited large load interconnection through contractual relationships. Advanced Energy 
United (“AEU”) offers an appropriate summary of the general industry position on the value of

of the flexibility to be at or near the site of the new large load, but some or all of it

Recent research provides additional support for the value provided by adjacent and 
nearby capacity resources to expedite large load interconnection. Enchanted Rock’s comments

priority, and interconnection process for large load customers, as well as load-supply hybrid

supply: “[c]olocated or adjacent supply may be owned and operated by the large load customer

CONCLUSION 
This ANOPR proceeding demonstrates broad industry and nationwide support to 
accelerate the ability of our electric grid to integrate the large loads that will secure America’s AI 
dominance. To enlist all available resources in this mission, the Distributed Capacity Parties ask 
FERC to develop rules that allow off-site resources that are capable of addressing constraints to 
be considered in joint interconnection studies and the expediting of large load interconnection 
pursuant to ANOPR Principles Three, Five, and Seven. Such an outcome is consistent with the 
emerging industry consensus, as detailed above.
