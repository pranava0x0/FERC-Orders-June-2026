EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
On April 16, 2026, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission issued an Order Regarding Intent
to Act in Docket No. RM26-4-000, committing to take action by the end of June 2026 on the
interconnection of large electrical loads to the interstate transmission system. The proceeding,
initiated by the Department of Energy under Section 403 of the DOE Organization Act in October
2025, has generated over 3,500 pages of public comments from approximately 175 unique
organizations. Commission staff have reviewed this record and coordinated across federal
agencies to prepare for what FERC has described as action that will be "quick, efficient, and
legally durable."
This brief identifies a structural gap in that record. The overwhelming majority of filings address
three sets of concerns: jurisdictional authority between federal and state regulators, cost allocation
for network upgrades, and interconnection study timelines. These are necessary questions. They
are not sufficient ones. No commenter in the docket has substantively addressed the
community-infrastructure dimensions of large-load interconnection: workforce housing, school
capacity, healthcare access, water system adequacy, transportation infrastructure, and the
long-term viability of host communities that absorb facilities consuming as much electrical capacity
as mid-sized cities.
This gap is most acute in Wyoming, where proposed data center developments would — if fully
built — more than double the state's existing electrical generation capacity, yet Wyoming's Public
Service Commission did not file comments in the docket. This brief presents a framework for
evaluating the community-development dimensions of large-load interconnection and argues that
durable federal rulemaking must account for the human infrastructure that makes host
communities viable over multi-decade facility lifecycles.
I. REGULATORY BACKGROUND AND PROCEDURAL POSTURE
On October 23, 2025, Secretary of Energy Chris Wright directed FERC to consider an Advance Notice
of Proposed Rulemaking on large-load interconnection, invoking a rarely used authority under Section
403 of the DOE Organization Act. The ANOPR proposes reforms to standardize how electrical loads
exceeding 20 megawatts — including data centers, advanced manufacturing facilities, and hybrid
generation-load configurations — connect to the interstate transmission system. The Secretary's
directive requested final action by April 30, 2026.
FERC established the proceeding as Docket No. RM26-4-000, issuing a Notice Inviting Comments on
October 27, 2025. Following requests for extension, the initial comment period closed November 21,
2025, with reply comments due December 5, 2025. The Commission received filings from utilities, grid
operators, data center developers, industrial consumers, state regulatory commissions, consumer

The rulemaking represents an assertion of federal jurisdiction that FERC has historically not exercised.
While FERC has maintained standardized interconnection procedures for generators since Order No.
888 and subsequent orders (2003, 845, 2023), load interconnection — how end-use customers
connect to receive power — has remained under state jurisdiction. The ANOPR advances the position
that large-load interconnections are analogous to generator interconnections and fall within FERC's
authority over interstate transmission.
FERC did not meet the Secretary's April 30 deadline. On April 16, 2026, the Commission issued an
Order Regarding Intent to Act, committing to action by the end of June 2026. The order catalogs interim
steps already taken: a December 2025 order directing PJM Interconnection to implement co-location
tariff provisions, a January 2026 approval of Southwest Power Pool's High Impact Large Load initiative,
and action on multiple individual tariff filings. The Commission emphasized that its forthcoming action
will establish standards for processing large-load interconnection requests and that generic rulemaking
should not discourage utilities from filing independently under Federal Power Act Section 205.
II. THE JURISDICTIONAL FAULT LINE
The dominant tension in the docket record is the contest between federal and state regulatory authority.
The National Association of Regulatory Utility Commissioners filed initial and reply comments opposing
FERC's proposed assertion of jurisdiction over load interconnection. In its reply comments, NARUC
reported that every one of the nineteen state public utility commissions that individually filed comments
either opposed or expressed significant caution regarding the proposed expansion of federal authority.
The state commissions' position is grounded in the Federal Power Act's jurisdictional framework, which
reserves to states the authority over retail rates, distribution systems, and end-use customer service.
NARUC's Board of Directors adopted a formal resolution in November 2025 urging FERC to preserve
state retail regulatory authority and ensure that large-load interconnections do not compromise grid
reliability or impose undue costs on existing ratepayers. Individual state commissions — including the
Organization of PJM States, the Missouri Public Service Commission, and the Kansas Corporation
Commission — filed comments emphasizing that durable solutions may require joint state-federal
regulation rather than unilateral federal assertion of control.
On the other side, data center developers and technology companies favor a stronger federal role to
reduce friction, shorten timelines, and manage transmission system impacts that extend beyond state
boundaries. The Data Center Coalition expressed support for a standardized process. Microsoft stated
that timely access to electric power supplies remains the biggest obstacle to deploying advanced
computing technology in the United States.
The legal durability of any FERC action faces scrutiny under the major questions doctrine articulated in
West Virginia v. EPA and the elimination of Chevron deference under Loper Bright Enterprises v.
Raimondo. Several commenters noted that FERC's own precedent highlights the jurisdictional

III. WYOMING'S REGULATORY SILENCE
Among the state commissions that did not file comments in the docket is the Wyoming Public Service
Commission. This absence is significant in proportion to the scale of large-load development proposed
within Wyoming's borders.
Wyoming's existing electrical generation capacity stands at approximately 10,200 megawatts. Current
in-state demand from homes, businesses, mines, refineries, and manufacturers consumes roughly 40
percent of that capacity, with the balance exported. Against this baseline, proposed data center
developments in Wyoming include:

The first phase of Project Jade alone — at 1,800 megawatts — would represent more than five times
the electrical consumption of all approximately 238,000 homes in Wyoming. If all proposed
developments reached full build-out, the aggregate demand would exceed the state's total existing
generation capacity. No other state in the docket faces a comparable ratio of proposed large-load
demand to existing generation baseline.
Several of these developments pursue what industry participants describe as "islanding" —
constructing private power generation facilities that operate without grid interconnection. The Wyoming
PSC holds regulatory authority over such generation facilities, but as Wyoming Public Media reported
in January 2026, regulated utilities have expressed frustration that they lack regulatory power over the
broader picture, particularly regarding load forecasting in a future where islanded data centers may
eventually seek grid integration or depart entirely.

recommendations on the regulatory approach. Deputy Chairman Chris Petrie of the Wyoming PSC has

IV. THE COMMUNITY-DEVELOPMENT GAP IN THE DOCKET RECORD
A review of the principal comment categories in the docket reveals a record organized almost entirely
around three axes: jurisdictional authority (federal vs. state), economic mechanisms (cost allocation,
participant funding, credit-back structures), and process engineering (study timelines, queue
management, threshold definitions). These are technical questions addressed by technically qualified
parties. The Halcyon analysis of the 175 initial comment filings identified six core topic areas; none of
them address community infrastructure.
The ANOPR itself proposes fourteen guiding principles. The later principles address transition planning,
reliability standards, consumer protection, and the treatment of existing agreements. Yet the framework
does not require — or even invite — analysis of what happens to the physical communities where large
loads concentrate. A 1,800-megawatt data center campus is not merely an electrical load on a
transmission system. It is an employer, a water consumer, a traffic generator, a housing-market force,
and a tax-base participant in a specific county, school district, and health-services region.
The FERC proceeding is not designed to regulate community planning — and this brief does not argue

The community-development dimension is not adjacent to the rulemaking. It is upstream of it.
V. A COMMUNITY-DEVELOPMENT STANDARD FOR LARGE-LOAD HOST
COMMUNITIES
Maven Solutions has developed a comprehensive evaluation framework — the Polar-Grade
Community-Development Standard — designed to assess and score the readiness of host
communities to absorb large-load facilities across ten integrated dimensions. The framework draws on

historical precedent from master-planned community development, including the design principles
applied by George Mitchell in the development of The Woodlands, Texas, and integrates them with the
specific infrastructure demands of hyperscale data center operations.
The ten dimensions of the standard are:
1. Electrical Infrastructure & Grid Capacity. Transmission access, generation adequacy,
redundancy, and upgrade pathways.
2. Water Systems & Cooling Infrastructure. Supply sourcing, consumption modeling,
wastewater treatment, and aquifer impact.
3. Workforce Housing. Availability, affordability, and pipeline capacity for construction and
permanent operations workforces.
4. Education & Schools. K-12 capacity, enrollment forecasting, and capital funding mechanisms
for school districts absorbing population growth.
5. Healthcare Access. Hospital capacity, emergency services, behavioral health infrastructure,
and provider recruitment.
6. Transportation & Logistics. Road capacity, freight routing, construction traffic management,
and long-term commuter infrastructure.
7. Environmental & Land Use. Air quality permitting, wildlife corridor preservation, agricultural
land conversion, and noise/light mitigation.
8. Economic Design & Tax Base. Revenue distribution, tax incentive structures, community
benefit agreements, and long-term fiscal sustainability.
9. Regulatory & Governance. State commission capacity, local permitting frameworks,
inter-agency coordination, and public participation mechanisms.
10. Community Resilience & Lifecycle Planning. Facility decommissioning provisions,
economic diversification, stranded-asset risk, and multi-decade community viability.
Each dimension is designed to be evaluated independently and scored against defined readiness
criteria, producing a composite community-readiness assessment that can inform interconnection
planning, site selection, regulatory review, and community benefit negotiations. The standard does not
prescribe outcomes; it provides a structured evaluation methodology that surfaces infrastructure gaps
before they become operational constraints or sources of community opposition.
VI. IMPLICATIONS FOR THE RULEMAKING AND STAKEHOLDERS
The FERC proceeding in RM26-4-000 will continue generating filings, compliance deadlines, and
related state-level proceedings beyond the June 2026 action. Several law firms monitoring the docket

have noted that the Commission's forthcoming order could affect jurisdictional authority,
interconnection processes, treatment of co-located load and generation, and cost allocation principles.
If FERC issues a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking — which multiple analysts consider the most likely
form of action given the jurisdictional complexity — a formal comment period of 60 to 90 days will
follow, during which stakeholders can submit detailed responses to proposed rules.
Concurrently, the North American Electric Reliability Corporation has filed an accelerated large-load
action plan with FERC, targeting revised registry criteria and reliability standards by December 31,
2026. NERC's March 2026 whitepaper on gaps in existing practices for emerging large loads, along
with its planned Level 3 Alert, represents a parallel track that will establish reliability requirements
independent of the interconnection-process reforms in RM26-4-000.
For data center developers, the implication is that federal rules are being written now — across multiple
proceedings — that will define the regulatory environment for the next decade. Developers who engage
early in shaping those rules will operate within a framework they helped design. Those who do not will
comply with one they did not.
For state regulators, the implication is that absence from the federal record is not neutrality. Wyoming's
PSC, overseeing what may be the single most disproportionately affected state in the nation, has not
placed its perspective, its concerns, or its existing regulatory tools on the federal record. The
Commission's large-load tariff framework — developed through the Black Hills Energy / Microsoft
relationship dating to 2015 and recognized with a 2018 Smart Energy Decisions Innovation Award — is
an operational precedent that the federal rulemaking could learn from. That precedent is currently
invisible to the FERC proceeding.
For host communities, the implication is the one this brief is designed to surface: federal
interconnection rules that do not account for community-infrastructure readiness will produce facilities
that are grid-legal but community-fragile. The community-development standard proposed here is
offered as a tool for ensuring that the regulatory frameworks being built in 2026 account for the full set
of infrastructure systems that make large-load facilities viable — not merely the electrical ones.
