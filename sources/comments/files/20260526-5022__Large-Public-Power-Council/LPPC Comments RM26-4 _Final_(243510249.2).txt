Dear Chairman Swett and Commissioners Rosner, See, Chang, and LaCerte: 
The Large Public Power Council (LPPC) represents 29 of the largest, not-for-profit electric 
providers in the country. Our members are municipal and state-owned utilities serving over 30 
million Americans across 23 states and territories with 80 gigawatts of generation and 
nearly 50,000 miles of transmission. We share the Administration's commitment to energy 
dominance and specifically support improving the speed, efficiency, fairness, and predictability 
of large load interconnections. LPPC member utilities are actively partnering with large load 
customers, including data centers, semiconductor manufacturers, hydrogen producers, and 
advanced manufacturers, to integrate significant new electric demand into the grid in a 
responsible, customer-protective manner. 
In response to the Secretary of Energy's Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) 
and the Commission's invitation for comments in Docket No. RM26-4-000, LPPC underscored 
that its member utilities have long employed public power tariffs, special contracts, and bilateral 
service agreements to integrate large loads while protecting all customers from undue cost-
shifting. We write now to supplement the record in this docket with a current snapshot of publicly 
announced and aggregate large load projects being actively developed within LPPC member 
service territories, as well as a summary of the tariff and contractual frameworks those utilities 
are using to ensure large load customers pay their fair share. This material builds on the 
supplemental comments filed by the Edison Electric Institute in this Docket on March 12, 2026, 
and the National Association of Regulatory Utility Commissioners on April 13, 2026, cataloguing 
extensive state-regulated retail tariffs and agreements through which large loads are currently 
served and will be interconnected in the next several years.      
Based on data collected from LPPC members in early 2026, the highlights of this snapshot are 
these: 
• 
LPPC member utilities collectively report more than 50 gigawatts of large load 
requests across committed projects and speculative, early-stage inquiries, 
spanning data centers, manufacturing, and other industrial facilities. These requests 
contribute more than $140 Billion in capital investments.

• 
LPPC-member utilities currently serve over 18% of the nation’s data centers and are 
projected to serve more than a third of new data centers anticipated over the next 
five years.  
• 
Select member utilities with publicly citable projects represent billions of additional 
dollars in customer investment and thousands of megawatts of new connected load 
being developed in partnership with public power utilities. 
• 
LPPC member utilities are employing a variety of customer-protective mechanisms, 
including dedicated substations, upfront infrastructure funding requirements, take-or-pay 
provisions, minimum billing demand charges, exit fees, and bring-your-own-power 
arrangements, to ensure large load growth does not burden existing ratepayers. 
• 
Both approved tariffs and contract-based structures are in active use across multiple 
states, demonstrating that states and utilities have developed effective frameworks 
before any federal mandate. 
We continue to underscore the importance of crafting federal policies that build upon, rather 
than disrupt, this progress. LPPC member utilities look forward to continuing to work with the 
Commission, state regulators, and large load customers to advance the nation's economic and 
national security interests.

COMMON ELEMENTS OF LARGE LOAD TARIFFS AND CONTRACTS 
LPPC member utilities have developed several common protective elements. The table below 
summarizes key provisions and how they protect everyday customers. 
Tariff Element
How It Protects Everyday Customers
Eligibility / MW Threshold
• Defines the type of large load customer that qualifies, such as a 
minimum power demand (ranging from 5 MW to 75+ MW across 
LPPC members). 
• Ensures unique terms apply only to the largest customers and 
creates transparency around costs to serve them.
Customer-Funded 
Infrastructure 
• Requires the large load customer to pay upfront for dedicated 
substations, distribution upgrades, transmission lines, and other 
required facilities. 
• Ensures grid upgrades needed for large loads do not burden 
existing ratepayers.
Bring-Your-Own-Power / 
Generation Requirements 
• Requires large load customers to procure or contract for their own 
generation supply. 
• Insulates other customers from capacity cost impacts and ensures 
the customer is accountable for its own energy needs.
Minimum Billing Demand / 
Take-or-Pay 
• Bills the customer at a minimum demand level (e.g., fixed 
percentage of expected peak demand), even if actual usage falls 
short. 
• Ensures large load customers pay for the infrastructure built to 
serve them, regardless of fluctuating usage.

--- PAGE 8 ---
 
 
 
Tariff Element
How It Protects Everyday Customers
Contract Term and Exit 
Fees 
• Commits the large load customer to a minimum service term and 
assesses fees for early termination. 
• Reduces the risk of stranded assets that would otherwise be borne 
by all customers.
Financial Assurance / 
Creditworthiness 
• Requires the customer to post collateral or demonstrate 
creditworthiness prior to construction. 
• Ensures the large load customer can cover its costs, including bills 
and any exit fees.
Dedicated Substation / 
Separate Rate Class 
• Requires construction of a dedicated substation or creates a 
separate customer class for large loads. 
• Isolates cost accounting for large load service, making it easier to 
ensure fair cost allocation.
