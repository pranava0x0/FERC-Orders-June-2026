INITIAL COMMENTS OF THE DIGITAL ENERGY COUNCIL
I. Introduction
The Digital Energy Council (“DEC”) appreciates the opportunity to provide comments in
response to the Department of Energy’s October 23, 2025 Advanced Notice of Proposed
Rulemaking directing the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (“FERC” or Commission) to
consider reforms for the timely and orderly interconnection of large loads.
Energy is the foundation of proof of work, artificial intelligence, and advanced computing.
Without abundant, reliable, and affordable power, digital infrastructure cannot scale. Bitcoin
miners have already proven that large electrical loads can act as controllable, grid-responsive
resources. Across multiple markets, mining facilities have curtailed load within seconds during
periods of peak demand, providing measurable reliability support while maintaining stable
operations. These examples demonstrate that flexible load is already operating as a system asset,
not a theoretical concept.
As digital infrastructure demand grows, the existing interconnection framework built for
generation is not equipped to manage large transmission-level loads. Developers face multi-year
queues, inconsistent study processes, and unclear cost responsibilities that slow deployment and
create uncertainty. This proceeding is an opportunity to modernize how the grid integrates new
types of customers that can both consume and support power when needed. DEC supports the
Department’s initiative and encourages the Commission to establish a transparent, performance-
based framework that recognizes flexibility as a grid resource, accelerates access to transmission,
and ensures reliability and fairness for all users.
II. Recommendations
1. Clarify Interconnection Procedures for Flexible Large Loads
Current interconnection procedures treat all load as static, even though many modern facilities
can quickly adjust their demand and support grid stability. This limits how transmission
providers model and evaluate flexible demand during interconnection studies and creates

uncertainty for developers. The Commission should direct jurisdictional transmission providers
to include clear procedures within their Open Access Transmission Tariffs (OATTs) for
evaluating large, transmission-level customers capable of providing real-time telemetry and
verifiable curtailment. Recognizing flexible demand within existing OATT frameworks would
not create new service categories but would clarify how transmission providers study, model,
and coordinate with these facilities. Doing so would improve queue efficiency, reduce
uncertainty in planning, and attract private investment in grid-responsive infrastructure that
strengthens reliability.
2. Enable Expedited Interconnection for Flexible Loads
Current procedures make no distinction between firm, inflexible demand and loads that can
provide measurable system benefits. The Commission should direct transmission providers to
establish a voluntary, incentive-based fast-track process for customers that commit to verifiable
flexibility agreements. Transmission providers should post transparent study timelines, milestone
schedules, and progress updates for these projects to ensure accountability and predictability.
The Commission should also examine performance expectations for timely completion of studies
and require corrective or remedial actions when deadlines are missed without justification. Clear
schedules, voluntary participation, and consistent oversight would improve transparency, give
developers confidence in project timelines, and accelerate the interconnection of flexible loads
that support grid reliability and balance.
3. Integrate Generation and Load Interconnection Studies
The Commission should require transmission providers to establish a single, integrated
interconnection process under their OATTs for projects that combine generation and load at the
same point of interconnection. Current procedures force developers to complete separate studies
for generation and load, even when they are part of a single co-located facility. This duplication
increases costs, creates conflicting assumptions, and slows project development. A unified
process would allow transmission providers to evaluate combined impacts of generation and load
within a single study cycle.
4. Extend “Option-to-Build” Rights to Large and Hybrid Load Customers
Even when projects have completed interconnection studies and are ready to move forward,
network upgrades often stall due to limited utility construction capacity or competing priorities.
Many developers are willing and able to finance and construct approved upgrades directly but
lack a mechanism to do so. The Commission should extend the “option-to-build” authority,
currently available to generator interconnection customers to qualifying large-load and hybrid
facilities, subject to Commission oversight and cost-crediting provisions.

5. Modernize Cost-Responsibility and Tariff Treatment
Tariff and cost-allocation frameworks often treat all large customers alike, without considering
differences in flexibility, co-location, or system impact. This can unfairly burden facilities that
actually reduce grid stress. The Commission should affirm DOE’s principle that large loads and
hybrid facilities remain responsible for their assigned upgrade costs but clarify that those
demonstrating verifiable curtailment or on-site generation may qualify for phased or partial cost
responsibility. Aligning cost responsibility with actual system impact ensures fair treatment,
promotes investment in flexible technologies, and encourages efficient infrastructure
development.
6. Advance Flexible-Load Integration Through FERC-Led Stakeholder Processes
While this rulemaking focuses on interconnection, the benefits of flexible load extend into
wholesale market design and regional system operations. The absence of consistent frameworks
across RTOs and ISOs creates uncertainty for developers and operators. The Commission should
leverage its existing technical conference and regional stakeholder processes along with its
existing partnerships with the National Association of Regulatory Utility Commissioners
(NARUC) to evaluate how flexible large loads can better participate in reliability and market
operations. These forums can help identify best practices from early-adopter regions, such as
ERCOT, and translate them into national guidance. This effort would promote uniform
treatment, ensure consistent recognition of flexibility across markets, and strengthen the
reliability of the transmission system nationwide.
III. Conclusion
The United States is entering a period of rapid load growth driven by data centers, artificial
intelligence, digital-asset mining, and advanced manufacturing. This growth is reshaping the grid
and creating an opportunity to modernize how large customers connect and operate. If managed
effectively, these new loads can strengthen reliability, attract investment, and enhance national
competitiveness.
By clarifying interconnection pathways for flexible large loads, integrating generation-and-load
studies, creating voluntary expedited service options, and extending option-to-build authority,
the Commission can establish a more predictable and transparent framework for new demand.
Clear timelines, consistent accountability, and recognition of flexibility as a system resource will
reduce delays and improve coordination across markets.
Beyond this rulemaking, the Commission can use its existing authorities and convening power,
through technical conferences, coordination with RTOs and ISOs, and engagement with
NARUC, to promote best practices and expand the adoption of flexible load nationwide.

The Digital Energy Council is a non-profit advocacy organization founded in 2023 to advance
policies at the intersection of energy and digital infrastructure. DEC was created on the
understanding that energy is the foundation of the digital economy, powering blockchain, bitcoin
mining, artificial intelligence, and advanced computing. The Council brings together industry
leaders and policymakers to strengthen grid reliability, expand flexible load participation, and
ensure digital infrastructure growth is supported by abundant and affordable power.
