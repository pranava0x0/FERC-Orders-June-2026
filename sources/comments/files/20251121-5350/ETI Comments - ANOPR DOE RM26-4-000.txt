COMMENTS 
Below, ETI (A) explains why price formation efforts should be renewed in earnest, 
particularly in light of poor market outcomes arising from out-of-market interventions such as 
often-occurring unintended suppression of wholesale prices from demand response activations; 
(B) discusses the need for clear market signals to incentivize investments needed to address load 
growth; (C) discusses how competitive market prices can help improve reliability and 
affordability; and (D) explores recommendations for next steps.         
A. The Case for Renewed Price Formation Efforts  
Since 2014, the Commission has had a number of proceedings to address price formation 
issues in the RTO-ISO markets.  Despite these efforts, price suppressing events remain a persistent 
problem in these markets––even as recently as with the PJM uplift that arose out of the January 
2025 winter storm in the Northeast––thus undermining the signals that the prices should convey. 
In addition, the summer of 2025 saw increasing reliance on demand response initiatives in PJM 
Interconnection L.L.C. (“PJM”), which while beneficial for reliability and efficient resource 
allocation in the short run, have had the unintended consequence of suppressing wholesale market 
prices; and moreover, overuse of these programs in the presence of relatively normal spot market

prices will lead demand response providers to unsubscribe, defeating the programs’ reliability 
enhancing purpose. These price formation problems impact all manner of electrical infrastructure 
investment, including interconnection with transmission, and it is important for the Commission 
and the DOE to renew and reinvigorate the efforts to reform price formation.     
1. History of FERC’s Price Formation Efforts   
Starting in 2014, the Commission recognized the need for the reform of energy market 
pricing for the various jurisdictional RTO-ISOs.  While these have been generally positive 
developments, the need for further reform of the RTO-ISO pricing mechanisms remains, and 
should be renewed in light of continual pricing distortions which will hamper efforts to efficiently 
allocate transmission interconnections.   
In 2014, the Commission began to recognize issues related to the formation of market 
prices for energy and ancillary services for the various jurisdictional Regional Transmission 
Organizations (“RTO”) and Independent System Operators (“ISO”), and noted that such prices 
were not reflecting the marginal cost of production, actual operating costs, or operating limits.  
Accordingly, the Commission was concerned with the out-of-market actions (e.g., uplift payments, 
operator actions to secure reliability) that had been used by the RTO-ISOs that were driving such 
a divergence of energy and ancillary service prices from market fundamentals.7  Accordingly, the 
Commission ordered the RTO/ISOs to file reports addressing five areas of price formation: 
(1) pricing of fast-start resources; (2) commitments to manage multiple contingencies; (3) look-
ahead modeling; (4) uplift allocation; and (5) transparency.8

By 2016, the Commission recognized the need for pricing reform since the RTO-ISO 
energy pricing mechanisms were failing “to reflect the value of providing a given service, thereby 
distorting price signals and failing to provide appropriate signals for resources to respond to the 
actual operating needs of the market.”9  To address these distortions, the Commission issued Order 
825.  RTO-ISOs were ordered to settle energy transactions in relation to the relevant timing for 
when energy is dispatched and intertie transactions are scheduled.10  Further, the Commission 
ordered the RTO-ISOs to trigger shortage pricing for intervals whereby a shortage of energy or 
operating reserves has been indicated for the pricing of a resource during a given interval.11 When 
such shortages are resolved, not by a supply resource, but by the activation of retail or LDC-level 
demand response, it is no less critical that shortage pricing be triggered in order for wholesale 
prices to reflect system conditions.

resources can undermine price signals leading to unjust and unreasonable rates, and that there was 
an overall lack of transparency associated with uplift allocation and payment.12  As such, with 
Order 844, the Commission ordered the RTO-ISOs to improve uplift transparency with reports to 
address uplift by specific resources and zones, among other reports.13  For entities that serve load, 
uplift payments are particularly pernicious as these entities rely on forward power purchases to

2. ISO-RTO Prices Remain Subject to Out-of-Market Distortions  
Other than addressing the various RTO-ISO compliance filings responding to these price 
formation orders, the Commission’s price formation efforts have stalled, and material issues 
remain, including situations that penalize behavior that could improve coherent price formation.  
These material issues include: (a) persistent use of uplift allocations and other after-market 
adjustments; (b) wholesale market price distortions from the lack of demand response 
transparency; and (c) underfunding of congestion derivative instruments.   
Each of these out-of-market situations have led to poor price formation, thereby resulting 
in inefficient dispatch and investment signals, further increased reliance on non-market solutions, 
and reduced transparency and investor confidence.  Accordingly, the DOE should call on FERC 
to reopen and update the price formation proceedings, incorporating lessons from recent market 
events. 
a. Uplift Allocation and Other After-Market Adjustments Remain a 
Problem 
Most recently with the January 2025 winter storm, PJM assessed uplift costs to market 
participants, including with out-of-market make-whole payments and lost opportunity cost credits.  
As ETI explained: 
“[R]ecent extreme winter weather has once again highlighted critical flaws 
in PJM’s market structure, resulting in over $400 million in uplift costs in 
2025 year-to-date—expenses that cannot be hedged by load-serving entities 
through forward energy purchases and which are often passed directly to 
consumers. These out-of-market costs signal a fundamental flaw in how the 
market is designed; a problem that has been known to PJM and FERC for 
some time.  It is imperative that PJM, PJM stakeholders, FERC and the 

--- PAGE 7 ---

current administration prioritize reforms that ensure these costs are reflected 
in energy prices rather than hidden in unhedgable charges.”14        
 
This sort of price suppression during a scarcity event prompted by a winter storm makes it difficult 
for investors to reasonably assess the true economic value and feasibility of electricity projects.   
 
Similar investment uncertainty arose when a vast amount of generation did not perform for 
the PJM capacity market during Winter Storm Elliott in December 2022.  Under the PJM tariff, 
penalties were assessed for allegedly non-performing resources, while bonuses were due to over-
performing resources.  Ultimately, FERC mediated a settlement agreement between the various 
generators and PJM, whereby PJM agreed to reduce the assessed penalty amount by 31.7%, which 
also reduced the bonus by the same amount.15  Such after-the-fact adjustments make it difficult for 
investors to assess the potential viability of electricity projects.   
b. Demand Response Programs Not Factored into Wholesale Market 
Price Formation  
State initiated demand response programs can be efficient mechanisms for operational 
flexibility and allocation of electricity usage within a region.16  The problem is that some demand 
response programs occur at the local distribution level and are not integrated into the RTO-ISO 
price formation process.17  Since these demand response programs are not integrated into the 
wholesale price formation process, the RTO-ISOs do not have visibility into the demand (load) 
that takes place in these areas.  For example, a 2022 study estimates that approximately 5% of peak 
load from the Baltimore Gas & Electric region was not accounted for in the load estimates for the

B. Load Growth and the Need for Market-Based Investment Signals 
DOE’s projections show record levels of demand growth from data centers, artificial 
intelligence computing, and manufacturing.35  Meeting this growing load will require  significant 
new generation, transmission, and flexible resources—all of which depend on clear and durable 
RTO-ISO price signals.  Indeed, competitive markets have proven to be able to facilitate cleaner, 
more reliable power at a more cost effective price for consumers.36  As demonstrated above, 
RTO/ISO market designs that fail to properly reflect scarcity, congestion, and risk appropriately, 
have the unfortunate consequence of deterring capital investment.

Accordingly, ETI recommends that the DOE and the Commission work with the RTO-
ISOs to strengthen locational pricing mechanisms to more properly reflect system conditions.  
Current prices of forward markets tell market participants when to store and/or consume 
commodities, and help investors determine the relative values of time and place for the feasibility, 
location, and development of infrastructure projects.  Accordingly, promoting forward markets 
that provide transparent price signals facilitates generation and transmission investment.  An 
independent analysis by London Economics International (“LEI”) estimated that forward price 
markets as derived from congestion derivatives like FTRs save consumers in a market like PJM 
up to $1.2 billion annually by enhancing liquidity, transparency, and hedging in the forward 
market.37 Another LEI analysis for the Midcontinent Independent System Operator, Inc. (“MISO”) 
showed that the Stage 2 Auction Revenue Rights (“ARRs”) would not be fully funded without the 
price liquidity provided by financial participants in that FTR market.38 These findings demonstrate 
the value of sound forward market prices to consumers and to overall market efficiency.  
It would be useful to consider expansion of congestion derivatives markets (e.g., Financial 
Transmission Rights, CRRs) as essential hedging tools that provide term forward price signals to 
underpin long-term investment. This is particularly critical as additional solar and wind generation 
is integrated into the grid.  In many markets, investor-owned utilities and other market participants 
have requested congestion derivatives that distinguish between solar and non-solar time-of-use to 
assist in hedging physical assets.39

C. Competitive Market Prices Can Help Strengthen Reliability and Affordability of 
Energy    
 
Markets remain the most effective means of balancing reliability and affordability—but 
only if prices reflect true system conditions.  DOE and the Commission should work with the 
RTO-ISOs to provide for better price formation to accurately reflect system conditions and market 
fundamentals and facilitate markets for congestion hedging, which can reduce long-run costs by 
incentivizing efficient resource siting and flexible capacity. 
As demonstrated above, administrative interventions such as uplift payments, which are 
not hedgeable through forward contracts, may seem politically convenient, but raise consumer 
costs directly as a pass through in some circumstances and indirectly by raising the risk premia for 
serving full-requirements load and deterring market-based investment. Coordinated market design 
improvements across RTOs/ISOs are critical to maintaining consistent reliability frameworks.  
There is precedent for market designs helping with structural changes in energy markets, such as

D. DOE and FERC Coordination and Next Steps 
ETI commends the DOE for using its statutory authority to initiate this ANOPR, and ETI  
recommends that DOE and the Commission consider the reforming of RTO-ISO price formation 
to be added as a core principle to this interconnection reform.   
Further, ETI recommends that the Commission reopen its price formation inquiry to enact 
needed RTO-ISO pricing reforms, and absent action initiated by the Commission, for the DOE to 
formally exercise its statutory authority requesting that the Commission to do so.42  In reopening 
this inquiry, the DOE and the Commission should convene a joint technical conference on price 
formation, scarcity pricing, forward curve development, and integration of AI-driven load 
forecasting. 
Additionally, ETI recommends that the DOE use its authorities to prioritize market-based 
reforms as part of DOE’s grid modernization and transmission funding programs. These markets 
have provided significant value to consumers over the past two decades and it is critical that we 
utilize these tools to maintain affordability as we go through this transition. These market 
structures are designed to shift risk away from consumers and to entities that can manage that risk. 
When we have non-market-based solutions, the consumer ultimately pays a higher price.  
Accordingly, ETI stands ready to collaborate with DOE and FERC staff and to provide 
solutions to the examples of poor price formation outcomes across the RTOs/ISOs as discussed 
above.
