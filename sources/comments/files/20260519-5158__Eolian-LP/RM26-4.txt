SUPPLEMENTAL COMMENTS OF EOLIAN L.P.  
ON THE ADVANCE NOTICE OF PROPOSED RULEMAKING 
 
Eolian L.P. (Eolian) respectfully submits comments in response to the Advance Notice of 
Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) on Ensuring the Timely and Orderly Interconnection of Large 
Loads.1 On April 14, 2026, the Commission issued an Order Regarding Intent to Act in this 
proceeding.2 We agree with the Commission that, “‘[i]n light of the unprecedented current and 
expected growth of large loads’ such as data centers, there is an urgent need for thoughtful 
reforms to ensure that large loads are ‘able to connect to the transmission system in a timely, 
orderly, and non-discriminatory manner.’”3 The Commission’s Order Regarding Intent to Act 
states that the Commission will “address the problems discussed in the ANOPR in a manner that 
is quick, efficient, and legally durable,” potentially suggesting a faster procedural path than a 
rulemaking, such as action through a set of Orders to Show Cause.  
Eolian is an independent developer of both load and generation, with a broad portfolio of 
projects spanning many transmission provider territories. For the past decade, Eolian has been 
working to develop supply resources and load in a coordinated and efficient manner, including 
through co-location and at adjacent (that is, electrically proximate) sites. Drawing on that 
experience, Part I.B of these supplemental comments provide the Commission with specific

To achieve its goals of ensuring that large loads are able to connect in a timely, orderly, 
and efficient manner, the Commission must reform transmission provider tariffs across the 
country. In our experience, we have not encountered any transmission provider that currently has 
a process which (i) adequately integrates or coordinates the interconnection of supply resources 
and load, (ii) provides developers of supply resources such as Eolian with adequate incentive to 
locate generation and storage assets in a manner that opens additional system headroom for load 
interconnection, or (iii) offers the full range of transmission service options necessary to allow 
supply and load to coordinate to optimally use the existing system and reduce transmission 
infrastructure needs. The lack of tariff provisions to afford all three elements results in unjust and 
unreasonable or unduly discriminatory rates by exacerbating the reliability and affordability 
impacts of large load growth, while impeding speed-to-power for new large loads. The lack of 
coordination between load and generation interconnection processes, as well as the lack of 
transmission services to meet the needs and capabilities of configurations where generation is 
adjacent or located at the same point of interconnection as load, prevent new customers from 
accessing the electricity system and leads to an over-built transmission system by forcing the 
construction of unnecessary network upgrades.

I. 
COMMENTS 
A. 
The Commission must require nationwide reforms to enable coordinated 
interconnection of generation and load and provide transmission services that 
match the needs of load using a broad range of technologies and supply 
arrangements 
Eolian has focused on developing supply resources adjacent to large load customers to 
facilitate more timely service to the large load customer while reducing infrastructure needs (and 
their associated costs). In addition, Eolian has identified sites where the addition of generation or 
storage opens system transmission capacity to add new load without the need for additional 
network infrastructure. In Eolian’s experience, locating supply at these strategic locations—near 
the site of an incoming large load customer or at places where a new large load customer could 
connect—holds the potential to significantly reduce the amount of network upgrades required to 
integrate new load. Because long network upgrade construction timelines and lack of adequate 
supply are increasingly causing substantial delays for new load interconnection requests, 
developing generation or storage at these locations could allow large customers to connect to the 
system much faster. But existing rules do not adequately encourage generation and storage 
developers to pursue these strategies. Any action in this proceeding must fix this design failure, 
providing an incentive to locate efficiently.  
Eolian’s unique experience as a developer of both supply and load has revealed the lack 
of coordination in load and generation interconnection processes across the country, as well as a 
lack of consistent standards for how load is reflected in generation interconnection studies. 
Because generation and load are studied independently, load is often studied in a manner that 
ignores the presence of generation that could minimize system impacts, and vice versa. 
Transmission providers have refused Eolian’s requests to model supply and load together, even 
where the relevant assets are both Eolian’s own projects and their development is specifically

coordinated. The lack of integration in load and supply interconnection processes may result in 
both types of projects triggering significant network upgrades that may not be needed if the two 
projects were studied together, or studied in a manner that accounted for one another. As 
described below in Section I.B, Eolian has seen this lack of integration cause transmission 
providers to identify and assign the costs of network upgrades that would not be needed under 
coordinated load and generation interconnection studies, and cause cancellation or reduced 
commercial scale of both supply and load projects that would have been viable had transmission 
providers coordinated interconnection studies.  
While a single integrated study process would be the most efficient path for considering 
interconnection of load and generation together,4 the Commission can require load and 
generation study coordination even if it decides against asserting jurisdiction over load 
interconnection by requiring transmission providers to coordinate in generation interconnection 
study processes with the relevant transmission owners or electric distribution companies 
conducting load interconnection studies. Such coordination should include the opportunity for 
generation developers to nominate load(s) to be studied together with the generation. Further, to 
prevent undue discrimination and guard against inefficient system buildout, the Commission 
should require consistent standards for when and how load is included in generator 
interconnection studies.   
In addition, transmission providers do not allow load to avoid the need for network 
upgrades by offering non-firm network transmission services to loads willing and able to limit 
withdrawals from the broader transmission system.

As an increasing number of large load customers have sought to interconnect to the 
system, the challenges stemming from lack of generation and load study integration and the lack 
of differentiated transmission services matching the needs of co-located or adjacent customers 
have become more acute. However, these grid integration challenges pre-date the growth of 
demand from large load customers. In Eolian’s experience, inefficient grid planning outcomes 
are occurring even in areas with comparatively fewer large load customers. Every single area in 
which Eolian develops projects would benefit from reform.  
Eolian’s prior Comments and Reply Comments in this proceeding outline the key 
elements of reform needed to facilitate timely interconnection of new large load customers,5 
including:  
• The Commission must require transmission providers to provide for a generator 
interconnection process that allows generator and load interconnection requests to 
be studied together. While a single joint process would be the ideal, at minimum 
there must be an option for a generator to identify load to be included in its study.  
• The Commission should require transmission providers to adopt transparent and 
consistent standards for when and how new load additions are reflected in 
generation interconnection studies.  
• The relevant interconnection process must provide a path for permanent 
interconnection service. Temporary frameworks that hinge on subsequent 
processing through a standard queue create commercial risks that are likely to 
inhibit widespread use of these interconnection pathways.

• The Commission must require transmission providers to offer transmission 
services that match the ability of loads to use the capabilities of adjacent 
generation, including non-firm and reduced service options. Such service must 
allow the load to take service when the grid is unconstrained and the supply 
resource is not available (including when a battery resource is charging) while 
electing to curtail when the grid is constrained.  
• The Commission must ensure that these service options can be linked to load 
interconnection. To the extent the Commission takes jurisdiction over load 
interconnection processes, it should require transmission providers to expedite 
interconnection of large loads that are able to limit withdrawals from the system 
during grid constraints, including by relying on co-located or adjacent supply 
resources. Regardless of whether the Commission asserts jurisdiction over large 
load interconnections, the Commission must act to ensure that transmission 
service options and associated transmission study processes (at the local or 
regional level) enable and are not a barrier to states’ efforts to manage the 
interconnection of large loads. Specifically, transmission service options and 
associated operations and planning frameworks must recognize and account for 
the commitments of flexible loads to limit their withdrawals from the 
transmission system (including loads that achieve such flexibility through reliance 
on co-located or adjacent supply resources).    
These reform recommendations stand regardless of the procedural path the Commission takes, 
whether the Commission acts via rulemaking, through a series of show cause orders, or via 
another path.

Significantly, however, the Commission must require additional reforms beyond recently 
approved initiatives that some regions have enacted to ensure timely and affordable integration 
of large loads. While recent reforms in Southwest Power Pool (SPP) and PJM Interconnection, 
LLC (PJM) are positive steps, these reforms fall short of what is needed to fully address the large 
load interconnection challenges identified in the ANOPR. In SPP, while we support development 
of Conditional High Impact Large Load Service (CHILLS) and the High Impact Large Load 
Generation Assessment (HILLGA) process, these reforms are not adequate to facilitate 
interconnection of most new large load customers because of the operational risks and time-
limited nature of the service provided for the load and the generation.6 During the service terms 
for load under CHILLS and for generation under HILLGA, both are subject to unquantifiable 
curtailment risk. Furthermore, because HILLGA and CHILLS are not permanent service options, 
interconnection of both load and generation via this process remains subject to network upgrades 
that may be subsequently identified in the load and generation’s permanent interconnection study 
processes. And such permanent interconnection study process, which occurs under SPP’s 
Consolidated Planning Process, may be years after the facilities are operational. This creates 
potentially unworkable commercial risks for both load and generation that will inhibit use of 
these processes, as compared to what would be enabled via tariff mechanisms that allow for non-
time limited (i.e., permanent) non-firm transmission service, and non-time limited (i.e., 
permanent) interconnection rights for generation directly supporting that load.  
Likewise in PJM, the Commission must build on the Co-Location Order7—which, 
conditional on effective PJM compliance and implementation, is an important and significant 
step toward enabling faster and more affordable service—to maximize impact. First, the

B. 
Eolian’s experience developing projects demonstrates the need for these 
reforms 
Eolian develops energy generation, storage, and load assets throughout the United States. 
It has originated more than 30 GW of operating generation capacity, and more than 3.5 GW of 
commercialized battery energy storage capacity across 10 different utilities and power markets. 
Eolian also has more than 2 GW of data center and large industrial load applications in process in 
locations across the country. The company’s experiences illustrate lessons regarding the need for 
reform, including the scope of changes necessary to meet the speed-to-power challenge.  
To further support the Commission’s development of a record on the need for reform, 
Eolian provides three project development examples that illustrate the benefits of a more 
integrated and coordinated process for considering energy supply and load additions to the 
system. Each of these examples demonstrates how the current framework of uncoordinated study 
and undifferentiated transmission service options leads to study results that trigger infrastructure 
that could be avoided through a more sophisticated approach, which in turn delays speed to

power, increases costs for customers, and can result in reliability impacts where supply additions 
lag behind load growth. They also demonstrate how the bluntness of the current framework 
removes generators’ incentives to site facilities where they could avoid the need for otherwise 
unnecessary grid infrastructure, let alone to maximize the creation of additional headroom, 
thereby driving costs higher than needed to maintain reliable service and slowing down load

path to an offtake agreement. The Commission in this proceeding must cut through this 
misalignment, incenting and recognizing the benefits of efficient and coordinated load and 
generation siting.  
1. 
Lack of coordination between load and generation interconnection 
studies caused cancellation of the Matta Grid battery energy storage project 
in Virginia 
Eolian’s proposed Matta Grid battery energy storage project, a 200 MW / 800 MWh 
battery energy storage project, entered the PJM queue in 2020 and was assigned to PJM’s 
Transition Cycle 1. The project was directly adjacent to Amazon’s proposed 770 MW 
Mattameade Data Center and proposed to interconnect to the same 230 kV line. Although the 
data center and related substation were long-planned, the large load and associated infrastructure 
were not in the PJM base case for the Phase II study of Transition Cycle 1. In Eolian’s 
experience developing the project, PJM’s rules provide individual utilities with discretion 
regarding what infrastructure needed for load interconnection is input into PJM’s study models 
that form the basis of the generator interconnection studies. In this case, Dominion’s discretion to 

--- PAGE 10 ---
 
10 
omit infrastructure and load from its inputs into PJM’s models caused the data center substation 
to be entirely omitted from PJM’s generator interconnection studies for Transition Cycle 1. 
This omission proved fatal to the Matta Grid project. PJM’s Phase II study results 
assigned $158 million in network upgrade costs to Matta Grid, largely driven by a grid constraint 
25 miles from both the Matta Grid project and the proposed Amazon data center. A Timmons 
Group study that Eolian commissioned8 demonstrated that, had PJM included even a very 
conservative 150 MW of the 770 MW of Amazon load in its Phase II study for Transition Cycle 
1, the study results would have shown over $100 million less in system reinforcements assigned 
to the Matta Grid project.9 The failure of the generator interconnection study process to reflect 
reasonably expected conditions on the grid caused the cancellation of supply that is greatly 
needed in PJM and that could have reliably served adjacent load growth without extensive 
network upgrades.  
Eolian’s efforts to develop other projects have revealed that this is not an isolated 
experience. For example, in the American Electric Power (AEP) service territory, Eolian has 
observed that AEP appears not to submit transmission lines or substations into PJM models until 
the infrastructure is fully energized. Given the lead time inherent to developing generation and 
storage projects, this makes little sense. It provides for supply resources to be studied using 
models that will be years out of date when considering the proposed commercial operations date 
of the relevant infrastructure. Infrastructure can be omitted even when it is under construction, 
and sure to be operating by the time the relevant generation or storage projects come online. In 
many cases, such as with Matta, the omission of relevant load and related infrastructure will 
cause proposed generation to trigger more network upgrades. Thus, through their decisions

2. 
An uncoordinated approach to load and generation interconnection 
has triggered significant network upgrades at projects in the Eagle Mountain

Eolian has experienced similarly uncoordinated study processes outside of Regional

corridor that includes over 1000 MW of gas, 1200 MW of solar, and 1200 MW of solar. The

hyperscalers, independent data center developers, and Eolian itself via its joints venture—in an 
area surrounding the Mercer substation that could become the next “Data Center Alley” of the 
West.

The report assigned the cluster significantly more than $100 million in costs, including for 
remotely located upgrades.11 PacifiCorp’s failure to include the proposed load has persisted even

II. 
CONCLUSION 
Eolian urges the Commission to take bold, nationwide action to correct the inefficiencies 
illustrated above in both RTO and non-RTO regions that threaten affordability and resource 
adequacy. Inability to efficiently connect large load customers and adequate supply to support 
them threatens the economy of United States, and its leadership on artificial intelligence. The 
Commission can make significant strides in meeting this challenge by requiring transmission 
providers to integrate load and supply interconnection processes, incentivize efficient generation 
and storage assets siting to open additional system headroom for load interconnection, and offer 
the full range of transmission service options necessary to allow supply and load coordination to 
optimally use the existing system and reduce transmission infrastructure needs.

by 2027.  The location of the data center is adjacent on the south side of the AG1-412 queued

Conclusions 
When developers are siting new generation interconnection projects, many are strategically 
located near data centers to directly serve increasing load demands. However, if PJM is not 
considering the increased load within the interconnection process, new generators are at risk of 
extremely high upgrade costs that could be avoided when the data centers come online. 
Unfortunately, projects like AG1-412 were strategically located, but received overstated yet 
avoidable network upgrade costs.  
The PJM interconnection process has shifted from a serial process to a cycle process in order to 
efficiently study a larger number of requests simultaneously. Timmons Group commends the 
effort that PJM and the stakeholders went through to finalize the cycle process; however, there 
may be developmental challenges with the process as it is implemented in full for the first time 
with Transition Cycle 1. It is widely known that Northern Virginia is experiencing some of the 
highest data center growth in the world, requiring additional capacity to serve the load. If 
generation interconnection projects continue to receive unprecedented costs for upgrades that 
could be alleviated through appropriate load modeling, it will be nearly impossible to bring new 
projects in service in time to serve the increased load.

1.0 SCOPE OF THE STUDY  
Cluster Area 5 (CA5) is generally described as the Clover area and includes the following 
Interconnection Requests: C1-08, C1-09, C1-16, C1-34, C1-50, C1-51, C1-52, and C1-54. 
 
Consistent with Attachment W, Section 3.4.2 and Section 51.4 of PacifiCorp’s (“Transmission 
Provider”) Open Access Transmission Tariff (“OATT”), this interconnection Transition Cluster 
Study (“Cluster Study”) evaluated the impact of the proposed interconnections on the reliability 
of the Transmission System. The Cluster Study considered the Base Case as well as all generating 
facilities (and with respect to (iii) below, any identified Network Upgrades associated with such 
higher queued interconnection) that, on the date the Cluster Request Window closes: 
 
(i) 
are existing and directly interconnected to the Transmission System; 
(ii) are existing and interconnected to Affected Systems and may have an impact on the 
Interconnection Request; 
(iii) have a pending higher queued or higher clustered interconnection request to interconnect 
to the transmission system; and 
(iv) have executed an LGIA or requested that an unexecuted LGIA be filed with FERC. 
 
The Cluster Study consisted of power flow, stability, and short circuit analyses.  
 
This Cluster Study report provides the following information: 
• identification of any circuit breaker short circuit capability limits exceeded as a result of the 
interconnection; 
• identification of any thermal overload or voltage limit violations resulting from the 
interconnection; 
• identification of any instability or inadequately damped response to system disturbances 
resulting from the interconnection and 
• description and non-binding, good faith estimated cost of facilities required to interconnect the 
Generating Facilities to the Transmission System and to address the identified short circuit, 
instability, and power flow issues.

3.0 GENERATING FACILITY REQUIREMENTS 
The following requirements are applicable to all Interconnection Requests.  The Transmission 
Provider will identify any site-specific generating facility requirements in addition to the 
following in this report and in facilities studies.  Certain Interconnection Requests requesting 
service at a voltage level traditionally defined as distribution may be subject to the transmission 
interconnection request requirements listed below should the Transmission Provider make that 
determination. 
3.1 
Transmission Voltage Interconnection Requests 
All interconnecting synchronous and non-synchronous generators are required to design their 
Generating Facilities with reactive power capabilities necessary to operate within the full power 
factor range of 0.95 leading to 0.95 lagging.  This power factor range shall be dynamic and can be 
met using a combination of the inherent dynamic reactive power capability of the generator or 
inverter, dynamic reactive power devices and static reactive power devices to make up for losses.   
 
For synchronous generators, the power factor requirement is to be measured at the POI. For non-
synchronous generators, the power factor requirement is to be measured at the high-side of the 
generator substation. 
 
The Generating Facility must provide dynamic reactive power to the system in support of both 
voltage scheduling and contingency events that require transient voltage support, and must be able 
to provide reactive capability over the full range of real power output. 
 
If the Generating Facility is not capable of providing positive reactive support (i.e., supplying 
reactive power to the system) immediately following the removal of a fault or other transient low 
voltage perturbations, the facility must be required to add dynamic voltage support equipment.

These additional dynamic reactive devices shall have correct protection settings such that the 
devices will remain on line and active during and immediately following a fault event. 
 
Generators shall be equipped with automatic voltage-control equipment and normally operated 
with the voltage regulation control mode enabled unless written authorization (or directive) from 
the Transmission Provider is given to operate in another control mode (e.g. constant power factor 
control). The control mode of generating units shall be accurately represented in operating studies. 
The generators shall be capable of operating continuously at their maximum power output at its 
rated field current within +/- 5% of its rated terminal voltage.  
 
All generators are required to ensure the primary frequency capability of their Facility by 
installing, maintaining, and operating a functioning governor or equivalent controls as indicated 
in FERC Order 842. 
 
As required by NERC standard VAR-001-4.2, the Transmission Provider will provide a voltage 
schedule for the POI. In general, Generating Facilities should be operated so as to maintain the 
voltage at the POI, typically between 1.00 per unit to 1.04 per unit, or other designated point as 
deemed appropriated by Transmission Provider. The Transmission Provider may also specify a 
voltage and/or reactive power bandwidth as needed to coordinate with upstream voltage control 
devices such as on-load tap changers. At the Transmission Provider’s discretion, these values 
might be adjusted depending on operating conditions. 
 
Generating Facilities capable of operating with a voltage droop are required to do so. Voltage 
droop control enables proportionate reactive power sharing among Generation Facilities.  Studies 
will be required to coordinate voltage droop settings if there are other facilities in the area. It will 
be the Interconnection Customer’s responsibility to ensure that a voltage coordination study is 
performed, in coordination with Transmission Provider, and implemented with appropriate 
coordination settings prior to unit testing.  
 
For areas with multiple generating facilities additional studies may be required to determine 
whether or not critical interactions, including but not limited to control systems, exist. These 
studies, to be coordinated with Transmission Provider, will be the responsibility of the 
Interconnection Customer. If the need for a master controller is identified, the cost and all related 
installation requirements will be the responsibility of the Interconnection Customer. Participation 
by the Generation Facility in subsequent interaction/coordination studies will be required pre- and 
post-commercial operation in order ensure system reliability.

3.2 
Distribution Voltage Interconnection Requests 
The Generating Facility and interconnection equipment owned by the Interconnection Customers 
are required to operate under constant power factor mode with a unity power factor setting unless 
specifically requested otherwise by the Transmission Provider. The Generating Facilities are 
expressly forbidden from actively participating in voltage regulation of the Transmission 
Provider’s system without written request or authorization from the Transmission Provider.  The 
Generating Facilities shall have sufficient reactive capacity to enable the delivery of 100 percent 
of the plant output to the applicable POI at unity power factor measured at 1.0 per unit voltage 
under steady state conditions. 
 
Generators capable of operating under voltage control with voltage droop are required to do so. 
Studies will be required to coordinate the voltage droop setting with other facilities in the area. In 
general, the Generating Facility and Interconnection Equipment should be operated so as to 
maintain the voltage at the POI between 1.01 pu to 1.04 pu. At the Public Utility’s discretion, these 
values might be adjusted depending on the operating conditions. Within this voltage range, the 
Generating Facility should operate so as to minimize the reactive interchange between the 
Generating Facility and the Public Utility’s system (delivery of power at the POI at approximately 
unity power factor). The voltage control settings of the Generating Facility must be coordinated 
with the Public Utility prior to energization (or interconnection). The reactive compensation must 
be designed such that the discreet switching of the reactive device (if required by the 
Interconnection Customer) does not cause step voltage changes greater than +/-3% on the Public 
Utility’s system. 
 
All generators must meet applicable WECC low voltage ride-through requirements as specified in 
the interconnection agreement. 
 
As per NERC standard VAR-001-1, the Public Utility is required to specify voltage or reactive 
power schedule at the POI. Under normal conditions, the Public Utility’s system should not supply 
reactive power to the Generating Facility.

Methodology 
 
Reliability Analysis 
1. Innovation Lab 138 kV – Peak Load Discharging 
The maximum additional load at Innovation Lab 138 kV is determined (in MW) under full 
discharging (i.e., injection) during peak load conditions. The additional load is obtained 
by gradually increasing the injection at the study bus until new system reliability 
violations are observed in the model. 
2. Innovation Lab 138 kV – Light Load Charging 
Full charging (i.e., withdrawal) is simulated under light load conditions using a process 
similar to the peak load scenario. 
3. Innovation Lab 138 kV – Light Load Discharging 
The same approach is applied to evaluate the maximum additional load at Innovation 
Lab 138 kV under discharging conditions during light load. 
 
 
4. Jug St 138 kV and Badger 138 kV 
The procedure described above is repeated to determine the maximum additional load 
that can be accommodated at Jug St 138 kV and Badger 138 kV. 
 
 
Analysis included determining potential thermal limitations for the Project for both charging and 
discharging of N-1 and N-1-1 analysis for both peak load and light load with and without any 
additional load. 
 
 
Study Database 
To determine the amount of additional load that can be accommodated at the Jug St 138 kV, 
Badger 138 kV, and Innovation Lab 138 kV buses with the addition of the Project, five scenarios 
were evaluated under N-1 conditions. The analysis is based on the TC2 base cases (“Base 
Cases”), which represent peak load and light load system conditions. The study models 
correspond to the following scenarios: 
1. Case-SP: Base Case with peak load conditions and without Flint Storage, 
2. Case-SP-Discharging: Same as Case-SP, but with Flint Storage discharging at full 
capacity, 
3. Case-LL: Base Case with light load conditions and without Flint Storage, 
4. Case-LL-Discharging: Same as Case-LL, but with Flint Storage discharging at full 
capacity, 
5. Case-LL-Charging: Same as Case-LL, but with Flint Storage charging at full capacity.
