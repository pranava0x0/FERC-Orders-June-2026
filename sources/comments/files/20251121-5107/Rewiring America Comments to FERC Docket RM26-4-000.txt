Dear Chair Rosner and Commissioners: 
 
Rewiring America is a nonprofit focused on making American households the center of a clean, 
resilient energy future. We appreciate the opportunity to comment on Docket No. RM26-4-000 
on Interconnection of Large Loads to the Interstate Transmission System.  
 
Rewiring America takes no position on any Federal Power Act jurisdictional issues implicated 
by this proceeding, and nothing in these comments should be interpreted as expressing support 
for or opposition to those jurisdictional questions. At the same time, Rewiring America 
encourages the Commission to remain mindful of the Federal Power Act’s cooperative 
federalism framework. FERC should set floors, not ceilings, and any rules adopted in this 
proceeding should avoid preempting or impeding state authority.1 
 
Rewiring America agrees with the Department of Energy (“DOE”) that enabling the efficient 
integration of new large loads will be central to the next era of grid growth. We particularly 
welcome DOE’s recognition of load flexibility as core grid infrastructure. This is a meaningful 
validation of the view that demand-side solutions—whether achieved through large load 
curtailment, energy efficiency that permanently lowers peak demand,2 or dispatchable distributed 
energy resources3—belong at the center of energy planning, not at the edges.  
 
Many utilities, and DOE itself, have recognized that virtual power plants, which aggregate and 
control a network of distributed assets, have rapidly become among the lowest-cost pathways to 
new megawatts for the grid. There is also significant latent capacity and flexibility in existing, 
readily deployable distributed resources that can be mobilized far faster than new generation or 
major network upgrades.

Crucially, these distributed resources can not only help accelerate the reliable interconnection of 
large loads but also provide cost relief for residential customers amid rising energy bills. As the 
Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (“FERC”) evaluates next steps following the proposed 
advance notice of proposed rulemaking, we offer the following recommendations: 
 
On Principle 3 (“Load and hybrid facilities should be studied together with generation 
facilities…”): 
 
Rewiring America supports DOE’s emphasis on incorporating flexibility mechanisms—such as 
curtailable load and dispatchable generation—to facilitate timely and cost-effective large load 
interconnections. Studying large loads together with co-located generation can help identify and 
mitigate network constraints and protect customers, especially residential customers, from 
avoidable upgrade costs. 
 
However, limiting combined studies only to physically co-located resources unnecessarily 
constrains the toolbox available to large load customers. Off-site distributed energy resources 
(“DERs”)4 can deliver the same types of energy services—generation, load reduction, or 
transmission relief—that on-site assets provide. Large loads should therefore be allowed to 
incorporate aggregated front-of-the-meter and behind-the-meter DERs into their interconnection 
applications to the extent those resources can offset the transmission or generation upgrades 
triggered by the request. For example, an aggregation of residential batteries capable of 
providing import or export services, or a portfolio of high-efficiency heat pump upgrades that 
deliver durable peak reductions, can effectively mitigate network loading associated with large 
load interconnections. A long-term commitment to secure and maintain such contributions 
should be treated comparably to co-located generation for modeling purposes. 
 
Expanding eligibility to include appropriately located, non-co-located DERs aligns with the 
principles established in Order No. 2222, which recognizes that DER aggregations can provide 
dispatchable, reliability-enhancing services that directly affect transmission system needs.5 That 
recognition naturally extends to considering DER aggregations deployed in tandem with a large 
load interconnection request where those resources can relieve the same underlying constraints. 
 
On Principle 7 (“FERC should expedite interconnection study of large loads that agree to be 
curtailable…”): 
 
FERC should expand its expedited interconnection considerations beyond large load 
curtailability and co-located generation dispatchability to include appropriately located off-site 
distributed energy resources that can deliver curtailment or dispatch services under contract. 
These contracted resources should be evaluated as part of the interconnection package when they 
provide generation and/or transmission relief that offsets the impacts of the large load request. In 
this context, what matters is equivalent, verifiable performance in mitigating constraints within

Aggregated DERs are already recognized under Order No. 2222 as capable of providing 
dispatchable, reliability-enhancing services. Likewise, demand-side measures that permanently 
lower peak load can meaningfully reduce the system impacts of new large loads. Locational 
requirements can ensure these resources provide benefits on the same part of the grid affected by 
the interconnection. 
 
By allowing both durable peak reductions and aggregated distributed flexibility to qualify under 
Principles 3 and 7, FERC can give large loads more options to meet interconnection 
requirements quickly, affordably, and reliably.6 This approach will accelerate the integration of 
new loads while keeping household electricity costs in check.
