COMMENTS OF TERRAFLUX LLC
 Terraflux LLC (“Terraflux”) appreciates the opportunity to comment on the proposed advanced notice of proposed rulemaking (“ANOPR”) issued by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (“FERC”) on October 27, 2025. 
I. Detailed Comments Largely in Support of Proposed Principles for Reform
We largely support the principles outlined in the ANOPR as a framework to ensure efficient, timely, and non-discriminatory load interconnection reform. Our specific comments on each principle, based on their order in the DOE letter, is detailed below.
	•	We agree with the first principle’s assertion that FERC’s jurisdiction should be limited to interconnections directly to transmission facilities, consistent with FERC’s seven-factor test. 
	•	We agree with the second principle’s assertion that reforms should apply to new loads greater than 20MW and new hybrid facilities greater than 20MW, aligned with FERC’s generation thresholds of the LGIP 
	•	We agree with the third principle’s assertion that both co-located and near-located load plus generation facilities should be studied together as hybrid facilities, rather than forced to be studied as one separate load interconnection request and one separate generation interconnection request. We propose that ‘near-located’ means within three busbar substations of each other, while ‘co-located’ means within the same busbar substation. 
	•	Regarding the fourth principle, although we agree that nominal standardized study deposits, nominal readiness requirements, and nominal withdrawal penalties can help lower the amount of load interconnection requests, FERC should not set load interconnection requirements to exactly mirror those the amounts established in Order No. 2023. The financial and commercial requirements set in the generator interconnection reform process, although successful in lowering the number of generation interconnection requests to be studied, have dissuaded inventive but less-resourced generation interconnection customers from being studied in the queues. The reformed generation procedures favor large balance sheet and often less-nimble interconnection customers to crowd-out and morph generator interconnection queues into oligopolies. FERC should consider alternative ways to deter load interconnection requests which have no intention of being built, rather than imposing significant entry taxes and exit taxes for load interconnection customers to be studied by the grid operator. 
	•	We agree with the fifth principle’s assertion that a hybrid facility should be studied based on the amount of net injection and/or net withdrawal rights requested. However, FERC should allow the interconnection customer to indicate what those net amounts will be, rather than the grid operator assuming full nameplate of load and full nameplate of generation is netted from one another. Secondly, FERC should allow the customer to provide two additional, alternative ‘net amounts scenarios’ to also be studied, concurrent with the first scenario, which would allow the customer to right-size its hybrid facility components between facility needs and grid capacity. 
	•	We agree with the sixth principle’s assertion that hybrid interconnections shall be required to install system protections.
	•	While we agree with the seventh principle’s assertion that large load and hybrid facilities which agree to be curtailable should be expedited in a 60-day study duration, the details around curtailment allowances should be clearly and reasonably defined by FERC, such that a facility’s “high curtailment scenario” can be still be operationally understood, modeled, and financed against by the interconnection customer. 
	•	We agree with the eighth principle’s assertion that 100% of assigned network upgrades costs should be financed by the interconnection customer, however a liquid reimbursement mechanism is a cleaner and simpler approach than reimbursement crediting via transmission expenses. 
	•	We agree with the ninth principle’s assertion that the option to self-build facilities, which are triggered alone by the load interconnection, should be allowed. 
	•	We agree with the tenth principle’s assertion regarding an existing generation facility’s ability to serve a new load. 
	•	We agree with the eleventh principle’s assertion that a hybrid facility’s net, customer-defined withdrawal value should indicate how much transmission service is needed.
	•	We agree with the twelfth principle’s assertion regarding ancillary services responsibilities.
	•	We agree with the thirteenth principle’s assertion that a transition plan be established for large load interconnections already submitted, as of the date of the DOE letter. A transition process for these requests, similar to those propsoed for the LGIP reforms, should allow interconnection customers the option to revise their interconnection requests and include co-located or near-located generation facilities. 
	•	We agree with the fourteen principle’s assertion regarding NERC and OATT provisions.


II. Comment Regarding Interconnection Workforce Spanning Multiple Proposed Principles
	•	FERC or the DOE should consider if the resources of the federal government can be used to alleviate a major bottleneck of load interconnections nationwide: the shortage and overburdened conditions of the electrical engineers who perform the interconnection studies themselves. Higher compensating opportunities at grid developers and private consultancies have attracted these specialists away, and staff shortages is partly the cause of the protracted study turnaround times, which continue even after Order No. 2023 implementation. Load interconnection reform, though absolutely necessary, will further exacerbate the bottleneck. We urge FERC or DOE to consider offering monetary or non-monetary support towards these specialized interconnection engineers performing interconnection studies, to retain existing and also attract new/returning engineers to this critical function.
III. Conclusion
The Secretary of Energy's direction and proposed principles represent a watershed moment. The opportunity to mature the ad-hoc procedures guiding load interconnection may well allow the grid to meet emerging challenges posed by large loads and domestic re-industrialization.
