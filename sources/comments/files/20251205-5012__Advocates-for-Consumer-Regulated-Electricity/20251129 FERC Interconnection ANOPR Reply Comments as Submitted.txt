I. Identity of the Commenter and Interest in this Proceeding 
A4CRE is an independent, non-partisan organization dedicated to enabling the 
creation and deployment of Consumer Regulated Electricity (“CRE”) utilities—privately 
financed, physically isolated electric systems that serve new, large loads without 
connecting to the existing bulk power system, without imposing costs or risks on existing 
utility customers, and without requiring state utility regulation. 
A4CRE has participated directly in the development of CRE policy frameworks, 
including the nation’s first CRE statute in New Hampshire3, and regularly shares our work 
with state legislators, industrial customers, technology companies, and project developers 
regarding CRE policy design and regulatory classification. 
Because CRE utilities are intentionally physically islanded and serve only identified, 
contractually bound large customers, their legal status depends on clear federal 
jurisdictional boundaries under the Federal Power Act (“FPA”) and Section 215. The 
Commission’s determination in this proceeding could either enable or unintentionally 
constrain the central model of CRE. Accordingly, A4CRE has a direct and substantial 
interest in the outcome of this docket.

II. Support for the Fisher Comments and Summary of Their Conclusions 
The Fisher comments correctly describe the nature and purpose of CRE utilities. 
They accurately explain that: 
1. CRE utilities are physically disconnected from the bulk power system and do not 
participate in interstate wholesale electricity markets. 
2. CRE utilities are financed entirely with private capital and do not shift costs, risks, or 
reliability obligations to existing ratepayers. 
3. CRE provides a parallel, voluntary path for meeting large-load demand—particularly 
data centers and industrial facilities—without burdening existing systems or 
requiring complex interconnection studies. 
4. CRE can relieve pressure on transmission planning, interconnection queues, and 
regional resource adequacy by diverting large loads that would otherwise require 
significant system upgrades. 
A4CRE supports these conclusions and agrees that the Commission should 
recognize the legal distinctiveness of islanded CRE utilities. 
 
III. Physically Islanded CRE Utilities Are Not Jurisdictional Under Part II of the Federal 
Power Act 
CRE utilities do not make wholesale sales in interstate commerce, do not transmit 
electricity in interstate commerce, and do not hold themselves out to serve the general 
public. Their service relationships are limited to specific contractual counterparties on fully 
intrastate, physically isolated networks. 
Under the plain language of the FPA, such entities are not “public utilities” within the 
meaning of Part II. Historically, the Commission has not asserted jurisdiction over 
industrial or campus generation and distribution systems that lack electrical 
interconnection with the interstate grid and do not make wholesale sales in interstate 
commerce. CRE utilities align with this category. 
A Commission statement confirming that such systems remain outside jurisdiction 
so long as they remain physically islanded from the bulk power system would be consistent 
with statutory text, Commission precedent, and longstanding practice.

IV. Physically Islanded CRE Utilities Fall Outside Section 215 Reliability Jurisdiction 
Section 215 was enacted to protect the reliability of the interconnected bulk power 
system by preventing disturbances that could propagate across regional transmission 
networks. A physically isolated CRE system, by design, cannot cause such disturbances. 
A4CRE agrees with the Fisher comments that the proper trigger for Section 215 
applicability is physical interconnection, not voltage, size, or economic significance. A 
system with no physical tie to the bulk power system cannot materially affect that system’s 
reliable operation. 
A clear jurisdictional line would avoid unnecessary registration of islanded entities 
and allow NERC and the Commission to maintain focus where it is warranted—on 
interconnected facilities capable of causing cascading outages. 
 
V. Recognizing the Non-Jurisdictional Status of Islanded CRE Utilities Supports 
Reliability and Reduces Cost Pressures 
CRE utilities relieve the bulk power system of the obligation to plan for and serve 
very large loads. By allowing those loads to internalize their own costs and risks, CRE 
reduces upward pressure on transmission expansion, generation additions, and 
interconnection queues. 
For existing ratepayers, CRE reduces the likelihood of cost-shifting associated with 
new large loads. For states, CRE provides an option for managing industrial growth without 
compromising system reliability or increasing ratepayer exposure. 
These benefits align with the objectives of this proceeding, which include improving 
timely and non-discriminatory access to power for large loads without compromising the 
reliability of the bulk power system. 
 
VI. Recommended Commission Actions 
A4CRE respectfully recommends that the Commission: 
1. Clarify that electricity systems which are physically islanded, serve only identified 
contractual customers, and do not engage in interstate wholesale sales are not 
public utilities under Part II of the FPA. 
2. Clarify that Section 215 reliability jurisdiction applies only to systems physically 
interconnected with the bulk power system.

3. Confirm that any rules adopted in this proceeding apply only to entities that are 
interconnected with, or seeking service from, the bulk power system. 
4. Recognize CRE utilities as a lawful, voluntary, non-jurisdictional path that can 
complement Commission-jurisdictional solutions to large-load interconnection 
challenges. 
 
VII. Conclusion 
A4CRE supports the comments submitted by Travis Fisher and encourages the 
Commission to adopt the clarifications described herein. Recognizing the non-
jurisdictional status of physically islanded CRE utilities will provide regulatory certainty, 
support private investment, reduce pressure on the existing bulk power system, and help 
state and local governments manage rapid industrial and data-center growth without 
increasing costs to existing ratepayers.
