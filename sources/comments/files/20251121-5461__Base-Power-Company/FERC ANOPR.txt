INITIAL COMMENTS OF BASE POWER COMPANY, INC. 
In response to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission’s (the “Commission”) notice dated October 27, 
2025, and November 7, 2025, Base Power Company (“Base Power”) provides the following initial 
comments on the Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (the “ANOPR”) on large load interconnection 
released by the Secretary of Energy, Interconnection of Large Loads to the Interstate Transmission Sys., 
Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, Docket No. RM26-4-000. Base Power develops, installs, 
owns and operates utility-grade dispatchable energy systems that participate in wholesale markets and 
deliver capacity to customers in regulated and deregulated energy markets. Our experience as a resource 
owner and operator, electricity supplier, and load-serving entity, managing both load and generating 
capacity at shared points of interconnection in real time, provides a practical perspective on how new 
interconnection frameworks can facilitate and accelerate interconnection for critical large loads, ensuring 
the U.S. maintains technical leadership in AI and achieves energy dominance.  
 
Executive Summary 
As the Secretary of Energy has emphasized, the unprecedented pace of new large load interconnections 
necessitates standardized interconnection procedures and agreements to enable timely, orderly, and 
non-discriminatory access to the transmission system. Base Power supports the Commission’s efforts to 
advance this objective and proposes three principles for the Commission’s consideration:  
 
I.​
Dispatchable load must become ubiquitously contractible. Dispatchable, meaning the precise, 
at-will control of the range of net power injection or withdrawal, generation and load should be 

--- PAGE 2 ---
equally contractible commodities. In particular, for hybrid sites constituting a mix of generation 
and load resources behind a point of interconnection, the total range of dispatchable capacity 
should be a measurable and performance-based commodity that can be procured, scheduled, 
compensated, and held to emergency operating conditions much like traditional generating 
capacity. 
II.​
New load-enabling generation siting constraints should allow for electrically proximal 
resources. Siting for dispatchable capacity additions in service of new loads should remain open 
to both those behind the same and electrically proximal points of interconnection on the bulk 
transmission system, reflecting the functional reality that location does not diminish reliability 
value when performance is verifiable.  
III.​
Dispatchability and load-impact-mitigating generation combined should expedite 
interconnection study. The Commission should encourage mechanisms, such as expedited 
interconnection, to incentivize voluntary participation by large loads that choose to make their 
operations available as dispatchable system assets or facilitate the deployment of net new 
dispatchable capacity resources to reliably and economically serve system needs.When the 
proposed load itself is verifiably dispatchable, system reliability is maintained. When new load is 
combined with net new generation capacity and/or dispatchable resources that increase the total 
energy output of the system, resource adequacy is achieved more economically, to the benefit of 
the rate base.  
 
Together these principles support a durable framework that enhances reliability, improves efficiency, and 
leverages existing infrastructure to meet emerging system needs.

Comments 
I.​
Dispatchable load must become contractible 
Curtailable load and/or dispatchable hybrid facilities (herein referred to collectively as ‘dispatchable 
resources’ or ‘dispatchability’) must be implemented as defined, contractible commodities in order to be 
sufficiently integrated into both operations and system planning, with respect to large load interconnection 
processes. Base Power encourages the Commission to contemplate regulatory frameworks that establish a 
“structured, transactional approach” to dispatchability, such as the “large load [dispatchability] 
commitment” presented in a recent issue brief1. In this way, dispatchable load and generation resources 
can be designed and maximally leveraged to provide resource adequacy benefits, transmission system 
benefits, or a combination thereof.  
In this context, dispatchability refers to a defined and enforceable commitment by a load or hybrid facility 
to modulate its demand to a specific power at a specific rate, or mitigate its system impact through 
dispatchable hybrid resources, under specific conditions in exchange for recognized system and market 
value. To be contractible, particularly for dispatchable load, the conditions for operation must be clearly 
delimited and well-defined, with established parameters for availability and performance requirements 
(e.g., ramp rate and target power error tolerances). These parameters allow dispatchability to function as a 
measurable capacity product, enabling operators to plan around a known performance envelope rather 
than a discretionary response. Such parameters also ensure that the dispatchability provided has 
quantifiable reliability value, in accordance with established performance criteria, and provide the basis 
for enforcement, verification and settlement.

We draw the Commission’s attention to the definition2 of Controllable Load Resources (CLR) enabled in 
ERCOT – a CLR is defined as a “Load Resource capable of controllably reducing or increasing 
consumption under Dispatch control by ERCOT”. ERCOT nodal protocols 6.6.5.1.1 details acceptable 
performance criterion for loads participating in economic dispatch processes. Performance metrics 
establish acceptable tolerances for frequency response and deviation from target power and ramp rates. 
Importantly, these loads meeting their resource qualification requirements does not jeopardize resource 
adequacy, as strict performance criterion applies directly to dispatchable resources. Comparatively, a firm 
load must be assumed to be drawing maximum load for each of a long list of contingent scenarios a 
balancing authority may face because the load cannot be assumed to be responsive to dispatch signals. 
Thus, impacts to binding congestion constraints or transient deviations in frequency are by default 
exacerbated by the introduction of this load to a model of the systems power flow when considered for 
interconnection. 
 
Proposed Properties of Dispatchable Load 
A dispatchable load resource should attain the following properties:  
1.​ The load has a controllable range of withdrawal (or injection if inclusive of hybrid sites capable 
of net power generation at the point of interconnection). 
2.​ Within this controllable range, the load is responsive to economic dispatch signals from balancing 
authorities. 
3.​ Within this controllable range, a dispatchable load can ramp and maintain specific power 
setpoints inside operationally useful error tolerances. 
4.​ Within this controllable range, a dispatchable load has a defined frequency response curve. 
5.​ A dispatchable load has otherwise defined fault tolerance characteristics (e.g. voltage 
ridethrough) such that contingencies can be sufficiently modeled.

Exhibit A3, appended to this filing, illustrates how the CLR resources in ERCOT attain these proposed 
properties and specific parameter values used, and Section 2.3 of the ERCOT Controllable Load 
Qualification Test Procedure4 demonstrates how information like frequency response characteristics are 
communicated to the balancing authority for verification and qualification. These properties inform the 
core assumptions made when a new load is studied for interconnection – allowing it to be sufficiently 
modeled, and integrated into system planning and operations. Key to this: a dispatchable load is not 
assumed to be firm and operating at an extreme system state when its impact to infrequent system 
contingencies are analyzed.  
 
Importantly, how the dispatched power is achieved at the point of interconnection is less important than 
the market-enforced adherence to performance criteria, such that said capacity is reliably contractible. A 
load, together with colocated or proximal dispatchable generation or storage can combine as a hybrid 
resource to achieve a net power at the point of interconnection with specific minimum and maximum 
capabilities and expected response rates established by a balancing authority, similar to the parameters 
specified for CLR in ERCOT.

on the system, offer functionally identical value to reliability and grid stability where modeled and 
verified in the course of typical, day-to-day grid operating conditions. The defining feature of resource 
adequacy is dispatchability, not geography. A resource that can increase or decrease its output or load in 
response to an operator signal provides the same system benefit whether it is co-located with the facility it 
serves or operates under a contractual arrangement elsewhere. Therefore, constraining generation in 
service of supporting new load interconnection dispatchability to a narrow class of assets would 
unnecessarily exclude a significant portion of capacity already supporting grid operation.  
 
The Southwest Power Pool’s (SPP) recent High Impact Large Load (HILL) filing4 before the Commission 
provides a current and concrete example of how regional operators are already treating dispatchability as 
a system reliability concept rather than a fixed interconnection category. In that filing, SPP proposed 
Tariff Attachments BA and BB to create new study and operational frameworks for HILLs and for “High 
Impact Large Load Generation Assessments’ (HILLGA). Under the HILLGA process, supplemental 
generating facilities procured to serve such loads may be considered in a combined application with the 
load, expediting interconnection, when located electrically proximal, defined as no more than two 
bulk-system substations away from the load.  
 
Importantly, the HILLGA model expands opportunities for large loads to contribute to resource adequacy 
in a manner that is more cost-effective to the rate base than relying solely on load curtailment. SPP’s 
proposal recognizes that, in the course of normal grid operations, it is far less expensive and far more 
reliable to meet incremental resource adequacy needs with supplemental generation, whether located 
on-site or electrical proximal, than to compensate a large load for curtailing during tight but otherwise

requires comparatively costly compensation, while other forms of dispatchable generation can provide 
predictable, verifiable output that is directly incorporated into forward-looking reliability models. As SPP 
asserts, the HILL and “local area” framework improves “market efficiency”, “cost-effectiveness”, and 
"confidence in expected costs and timelines”. These concepts reflect the reality that adding dispatchable  
resources is a cost-efficient, expeditious and more reliable resource adequacy solution than treating 
largeload curtailment as the marginal resource. By contrast, emergency price response cannot satisfy 
Commission-approved planning criteria because its performance is neither firm nor modelable. 
Committed dispatchable capability, as recognized in SPP’s HILLGA filing, can be included in operational 
assumptions to improve study accuracy and support more efficient system operations. The HILLGA 
framework confirms this approach by allowing contingent or supporting generation to be considered 
alongside the load, acknowledging that resource adequacy can be delivered more economically, through 
dedicated dispatchable resources, than through high-cost curtailment alone. This treatment mirrors Base 
Power’s position that dispatchable capability can be provided by proximal, verifiable resources capable of 
delivering predictable system response, including customer-sited or aggregated technologies.  
 
The Point of Interconnection (POI) remains the essential reference for determining how facilities are 
studied, monitored, and integrated into system operations. However, as SPP’s HILL proposal illustrates, 
reliability impacts and operational dispatchability are not confined to the single load-interconnected POI 
itself. Dispatchable assets located near or electrically proximal to a load, whether sharing the same POI or 
connected within a limited local area, can materially affect flows, voltage stability, and balancing 
requirements at the node. Maintaining an open approach to dispatchability allows system operators to 
evaluate such resources in relation to their effect on the POI, rather than by strict interconnection 
topology. This ensures that operationally relevant capacity, whether physically co-located with the load or 
situated a few substations away, can be considered within interconnection and planning studies, consistent 
with the localized assessment structure already proposed by SPP.

III.​
Dispatchability and load-impact-mitigating generation combined should expedite 
interconnection 
 
 
Principle Seven in Secretary Wright’s letter5 to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission asserts that 
“large loads…and hybrid facilities that agree to be curtailable and dispatchable should be expedited.” 
Base Power concurs that establishing clear incentives for dispatchable performance is an appropriate and 
effective means of enhancing system reliability. Structuring expedited interconnection pathways around 
voluntary “dispatchability commitments” creates a measurable reliability benefit and helps ensure that 
study assumptions more accurately reflect actual system behavior. Base Power defers to large-load 
stakeholders on the appropriate sequencing of studies, whether serial or otherwise structured, and on the 
operationally reasonable timelines for processing such studies. Our position is limited to the principle that 
interconnection frameworks should reflect verifiable system responsiveness and align procedural 
efficiencies with demonstrated dispatchability, thereby improving study accuracy, reducing unnecessary 
system upgrades, and supporting product grid planning.
