On October 27, 2025 the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC or 
Commission) issued a notice requesting public comments in this Docket after being 
directed by the Secretary of Energy pursuant to the Secretary’s Authority under Section 
403 of the Department of Energy Organization Act. Consumer Energy Alliance (CEA) 
respectfully submits the following comments in response to the Commission’s Advanced 
Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR). 
I. 
Background 
Founded in 2006, CEA is a nonpartisan, nonprofit organization advocating for 
balanced energy policy and responsible access to resources. CEA’s nearly 400 affiliate 
members, and thousands of individual members, represent virtually every sector of the 
U.S. economy – from the iron and steel industry to truckers, airlines, agriculture, labor 
organizations, restaurants, chemical manufacturers, small businesses, and families across 
the nation – that are concerned about U.S. energy policies, energy security, and long-
term price and supply stability.

Our members support rational, all-of-the-above energy policy that utilizes all of 
our domestic natural resources – both traditional and renewable – while ensuring 
commonsense environmental protections are in place. 
II. 
Comments 
 
 
As an organization whose mission is to advocate for affordable, reliable 
and secure energy for families and business, CEA enthusiastically supports reforms that 
improve and expedite the siting of energy infrastructure – whether it be electric and gas 
transmission, electric generation or large-load interconnection.  We commend both the 
Secretary of Energy and FERC for acknowledging the need to improve the large-load 
interconnection process.  With that in mind CEA offers the following comments: 
A. 
Avoid “one-size-fits” all solutions. 
 
 
Market and regulatory structures vary from state-to-state and region-to-
region with each having their own set of unique characteristics.  States like California 
(CAISO), New York (NYISO) and Texas (ERCOT) are single-state restructured markets 
that not only operate differently from each other, but also differently than other 
RTOs/ISOs (e.g. ISO-NE/PJM/MISO/SPP) that have both vertically integrated and 
restructured states as participants; not to mention states like Florida which are vertically 
integrated and do not participate in organized markets. 
 
 
Given the complexities and differences in these regulatory structures, 
FERC should avoid implementing rules that treat each jurisdiction the same. According 
to reports, many of the largest planned capacity hubs (by GW) are in vertically integrated 
states. As of mid-2025, vertically integrated states like Virginia (NOVA), Arizona 
(Phoenix), and Nevada (Las Vegas/Reno) together account for 13.6 GW of planned 

--- PAGE 3 ---
 
capacity while fully restructured Texas (Dallas-Fort Worth)  accounts for 3.9 GW.1 As 
for Texas, both Google2  and Vantage Data Centers3 are planning a combined $65 billion 
in investments. Texas is also building out natural gas infrastructure in support of data 
centers as (pipeline) capacity agreements have been reached for projects like Fermi4 and 
Cloudburst.5 In Louisiana, Energy Transfer has entered into agreements with Entergy to 
supply natural gas to support ~1.5GW of natural gas-fired electricity generation which 
will power Meta’s planned data center in Richland Parish.6 Different market structures, 
similar results. Simply put, FERC should not preempt jurisdictions that are expeditiously 
connecting large loads.7 
B. 
Ensure that growth pays for growth. 
CEA agrees with the Secretary that “…load and hybrid facilities should be 
Responsible for 100% of the upgrades that they are assigned through interconnection 
studies.”8 This sentiment aligns with the cost causation principle of utility ratemaking 
which limits cross subsidies and  protects system reliability by requiring that costs be 
borne by those who cause them.

D.

jurisdiction as a backstop.

collaboration between the federal government and the states. For this reason, FERC 
should only exert its jurisdiction11 on large load interconnection when states have failed 
to act. Essentially,  FERC should mirror its role in siting of interstate transmission when

E. 
Conclusion 
As stated previously, CEA supports the Secretary’s goal of interconnecting large 
loads as expeditiously as possible while protecting existing electricity customers from 
rate shocks. A number of states, with different market structures, have already moved 
swiftly to enter into agreement to bring large loads to market. Respectfully, FERC should 
resist the urge to insert blanket jurisdiction on all large load interconnections and focus 
on reforms to markets that aren’t delivering timely interconnections. A one-size-fits-all
