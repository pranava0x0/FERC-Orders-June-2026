within the State of New Mexico.1 The NMPRC is responsible for regulating and overseeing every

III. Summary of Comments 
 
The NMPRC supports the Commission’s efforts to provide transparent, standardized, and 
efficient interconnection procedures for large loads and hybrid facilities on the transmission 
system. However, the NMPRC seeks to ensure that the rulemaking adequately accounts for 
coordination with state regulatory processes and considers direct and indirect impacts to the 
NMPRC’s authority over retail rates and cost recovery from retail customers in New Mexico. The 
State of New Mexico has directed the NMPRC to prioritize affordable and reliable service and 
continue to implement the Energy Transition Act3 and to enforce Renewable Portfolio Standards. 
Accordingly, federal intervention raises complex legal and technical issues. FERC should 
narrowly tailor its final rule to ensure continuation of state priorities and should look for 
comprehensive ways to enact a cooperative approach to interconnection of large loads that comport 
with the state’s own legislative and constitutional directives.

IV.  Comments of the New Mexico PRC 
A. FERC should not infringe on a state’s authority to regulate and supervise retail 
electricity sales. 
FERC has consistently contended that the Federal Power Act creates two separate, 
exclusive spheres of authority: FERC regulates wholesale sales of electricity and interstate 
transmission, while states retain exclusive control over retail sales, retail rate design, and the siting 
and operation of generation and distribution facilities. In co-location arrangements, states decide 
who may serve retail load and how wholesale costs are recovered in retail rates, regardless of 
where the load physically connects. FERC’s jurisdiction applies only when a generator makes 
wholesale sales in interstate commerce or when interstate transmission service is used.4 
This allocation of authority aligns with longstanding New Mexico law, in which the 
Legislature has charged the NMPRC with providing safe, reliable, and affordable retail service; 
overseeing resource adequacy and planning; ensuring prudent utility investment; and 
implementing major state energy policies, including the Energy Transition Act5, Grid 
Modernization Act6, and Renewable Energy Act.7 
As FERC explained, states retain exclusive authority over retail sales, including retail rate 
design and the allocation of costs among retail customers. States alone determine which entities 
may sell power at retail and the rates, terms, and conditions of those retail sales. These state powers 
apply regardless of how or where a load physically connects—whether at the distribution system, 
the transmission system, or directly to a generator. States also possess exclusive authority to

In New Mexico, these jurisdictional protections serve critical legislative and policy 
objectives. The Legislature has entrusted the NMPRC with responsibility for ensuring that retail 
rates remain affordable, that electric service is reliable, and that resource decisions align with state 
statutes. A federal rule that interferes with state authority over resource planning, siting, or 
economic development decisions would undermine the Legislature’s design and the NMPRC’s 
statutory duties. FERC cannot require New Mexico ratepayers to finance new generation or 
infrastructure that has not gone through New Mexico’s planning and approval requirements, 
whether initiated through the IRP process, certificate proceedings, or any of the other regulatory 
pathways the NMPRC uses to review and approve new resources. The NMPRC is concerned that 
if FERC assumes jurisdiction over approving new large loads, those interconnections could be 
approved without any proceeding to evaluate their associated costs, depriving the NMPRC of its 
ability to regulate interconnecting customers and determine how associated generation and 
transmission costs should be allocated to New Mexico ratepayers. 
As New Mexico—like other states—confronts rising wholesale and retail electricity costs, 
the protection of state jurisdiction is essential to preventing retail customers from subsidizing 
large-load customers or bearing unplanned risks. FERC should ensure that any final rule respects 
the FPA’s jurisdictional boundary and preserves New Mexico’s sovereign authority to regulate 
retail service, resource decisions, and economic development consistent with state law and 
legislative priorities.

B. Any Rule Should Incorporate the Idea of “Cooperative Federalism.” 
 
FERC should approach this rulemaking through a model of cooperative federalism, which 
the United States Supreme Court has described as a program “that allows the States, within limits 
established by federal minimum standards, to enact and administer their own regulatory programs, 
structured to meet their own particular needs.”9 In New Mexico, the Legislature has entrusted the 
NMPRC with safeguarding retail affordability, overseeing resource planning and siting, and 
implementing landmark state policies such as the Energy Transition Act and Grid Modernization 
Act. A cooperative-federalism approach ensures that federal rules establish consistent national 
baselines while still allowing New Mexico to administer its own retail regulatory framework, 
reflect local conditions, and advance state policy priorities without displacement by federal 
regulation. 
C. Comments on the ANOPR’s Principles for Reform. 
 
Regarding the proposed minimum of 20 MW, this threshold may be too low at this time of 
an extremely large number of load requests. FERC should consider a larger threshold, and should 
consider if this threshold compliments, or conflicts with, similar initiatives being pursued by 
RTOs. The NMPRC suggests starting with a minimum threshold of 50 MW.  
 
In line with our previous comments about a state’s ability to regulate retail sales, any 
approval process for hybrid facilities should allow states the same discretion they currently have

V. Conclusion 
The New Mexico Public Regulation Commission recognizes that the rapid emergence of 
large loads is reshaping the electric system in ways that require coordinated national attention and 
strong state leadership. While the challenge is widespread, its consequences—on system 
reliability, customer costs, and long-term resource planning—are felt most directly by local 
communities within the states that oversee retail service. A federal framework that aligns with the 
Federal Power Act’s allocation of responsibilities and also respects states’ authority to regulate is 
therefore essential. New Mexico urges FERC to build its final rule in collaboration with state 
regulators, who are uniquely positioned to understand and manage local conditions, rather than 
viewing state authority as a barrier. The New Mexico Public Regulation Commission offers these 
comments in that spirit.
