A. Flexibility will Facilitate Timely Interconnections 
Stakeholders responding to the ANOPR consistently emphasize that speed to market has 
become a critical national priority.  Commenters agree that flexibility is essential to achieving 
timely interconnection while maintaining grid reliability and affordability.  As noted in ATC’s 
initial comments,3 and echoed by several other stakeholders, efficient and expeditious 
interconnection of large loads is no longer merely a commercial preference, but a matter of 
national security and an economic development imperative.4   
Flexibility remains paramount.  Stakeholders agree that flexibility enables creative 
solutions, which are ultimately the most effective means of facilitating efficient large-load 
interconnections.5  The Commission should carefully evaluate how any proposed reforms might 
impact existing, and effective, processes to interconnect loads.6  A “one-size-fits-all” approach 
could inadvertently hinder progress by upending established processes and increasing the risk of 
implementation delays.  ATC supports commenters recommending that the Commission

B. Studying and Interconnecting Hybrid Facilities  
Initial comments reveal diverse perspectives on studying hybrid and co-located facilities.  
Many stakeholders advocate for coordinated studies, while some favor serial processes and 
others prefer cluster studies.10  ATC, along with other commenters, urges the Commission to 
separate studies for generation and load while preserving the flexibility for local planning 
authorities to rely upon their expertise to adjust sequencing and modeling.11  Imposing rigid, 
nationwide requirements would risk undermining reliability and effectiveness, whereas 
regionally tailored solutions ensure fair cost allocation and facilitate seamless integration into 
existing interconnection processes.   
Coordinated studies raise challenges, including complex cost allocation and reliability 
risks from inaccurate modeling.  Generation and load are functionally different and should be 
studied separately.  Therefore, to the extent hybrid facilities are studied together, safeguards such

Finally, several commenters encourage the Commission to consider technological 
solutions for large load integration, such as Grid Enhancing Technologies (GETs), Dynamic Line 
Ratings (DLRs), Advanced Transmission Technologies (ATTs), advanced power electronics, 
among others, to increase reliability and more efficiently interconnect large loads.17  While these 
technologies can offer meaningful operational benefits, they are not substitutes for robust 
transmission expansion, particularly, given the size of the loads being interconnected, with some 
surpassing 1 GW.  These tools can optimize existing assets, improve flexibility, and support 
localized stability, but they cannot fully mitigate reliability risks posed by hybrid facilities and 
large, highly variable loads.  The physical scale and volatility of these new demands require both 
advanced operational solutions and additional transmission capacity to maintain system integrity.  
ATC supports leveraging these technologies as complementary measures within a 
comprehensive planning and operations framework—not as replacements for essential 
transmission infrastructure.

C. Cost Allocation  
ATC, along with several stakeholders, supports continued allocation of network upgrade 
costs to all transmission customers that benefit from the enhanced transmission system, 
consistent with Commission precedent.18  While some commenters favor the ANOPR proposal 
to directly assign 100% of network upgrade costs triggered by large load interconnections to the 
load owner,19 ATC believes such direct assignment would be highly complex and likely result in 
disputes, confusion, and delays.  ATC agrees that large loads should bear the costs they cause; 
however, the existing rolled-in rate treatment aligns with cost-causation principles and avoids 
unnecessary complexity and disputes.  Under Schedule 9 of MISO’s Tariff, transmission service 
costs are allocated to local distribution companies based on usage through their transmission 
rates and passed on to load via retail tariffs based on the service taken.  Consequently, network 
upgrade costs that benefit all customers are allocated according to usage, aligning with cost-
causation principles.

preserve existing processes and cost allocation methods to avoid disputes and delays.  Tight and
