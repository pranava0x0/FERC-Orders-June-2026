The Case in Brief 
American households are being asked to pay for transmission infrastructure built to serve hyperscale 
data centers. Under the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission's current rules, when a utility 
constructs new high-voltage transmission lines and substations to connect a single new data center, 
the cost of that infrastructure is spread across all customers in the surrounding region. Ordinary 
families and small businesses see their electric bills rise to support facilities that were built to serve 
the new data center facilities. 
A generation ago, the same Commission confronted a structurally identical problem in the natural gas 
industry, where individual expansion projects were driving system-wide rate increases for the new 
expansion customers. The Commission developed a durable solution: a rate-design framework 
requiring the customer driving the expansion to bear the associated costs while leaving the remainder 
of the system unaffected. 
That framework has now operated for more than twenty-five years, has been refined through 
numerous proceedings, and has repeatedly been affirmed by federal appellate courts. 
The same framework can be adapted to electric transmission infrastructure without requiring new 
legislation or novel regulatory authority. Doing so would protect households from unforeseen cost 
shifts, provide data center developers with predictable and financeable cost structures, and preserve 
the conditions necessary to build the transmission infrastructure the nation urgently requires. 
I. Two Industries, One Problem, Thirty Years Apart
The electric transmission cost-shift now emerging across the United States is structurally identical to a 
problem the natural gas industry confronted three decades ago—and one FERC already solved. 
In the early 1990s, FERC confronted a recurring problem in the interstate natural gas pipeline industry. 
Pipeline operators routinely sought authorization to construct expansion projects and roll those costs into 
system-wide rates, increasing bills for all existing customers regardless of whether those customers 
benefited from the new facilities. In several cases, individual expansion projects raised rates by five percent 
or more, prompting judicial concern over “the magnitude of the projects threaten[ing] a massive shift of costs 
onto existing customers.”1

The pattern was new at the time, but the principle at stake was longstanding: cost causation. Customers 
should pay for infrastructure built to serve them, not for infrastructure developed to serve others. Beginning 
with a 1995 Policy Statement establishing a five-percent materiality threshold, and culminating in its 1999 
Policy Statement, the Commission moved incremental pricing from the exception to the default.2 The 1999 
framework explicitly embraced a “no subsidy” principle: expansion projects should be financially supported 
by the customers and capacity contracts driving the need for them, rather than by existing captive customers 
across the broader system.3 
The electric transmission system today faces the same underlying challenge. A single hyperscale data center 
may require hundreds of millions of dollars in transmission upgrades, including new substations, network 
reinforcements, and high-voltage transmission facilities. Under current rolled-in ratemaking practices, those 
costs are often distributed across all customers in the transmission zone—even where residential 
households, small businesses, and industrial customers neither requested nor directly benefit from the 
infrastructure. 
The magnitude of these costs can readily exceed the same five-percent threshold that originally triggered 
reform in the natural gas context. 
The fact pattern is familiar. The legal authority is established. The policy 
rationale has already been tested.
Importantly, broad consensus already exists around the core policy principle. In March 2026, Amazon, 
Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI signed the White House “Ratepayer Protection Pledge,” 
publicly committing to bear the costs of the power-delivery infrastructure required to support their 
facilities.4 What remains absent is a regulatory mechanism capable of implementing that commitment under 
existing transmission rules. FERC already possesses such a mechanism in the framework it established for 
the natural gas industry.

II. How FERC Built the Framework 
 
FERC addressed the natural gas cost-shift problem through a deliberate and incremental policy process that 
ultimately reshaped expansion pricing across the interstate pipeline industry. 
Its 1995 Policy Statement established a rebuttable presumption under which rolled-in treatment remained 
appropriate where a proposed expansion project would increase existing customer rates by no more than 
five percent and where demonstrable system-wide benefits existed. Above that threshold, however, the 
burden shifted to the pipeline operator to justify why existing customers should bear the resulting costs. 
By 1999, the Commission concluded that the earlier framework did not provide sufficient protection for 
captive customers and formally adopted incremental pricing as the default approach for new expansion 
projects.5 The Commission articulated several core policy objectives underlying the revised framework: 
protecting existing customers from subsidizing expansions not built for them, fostering competitive markets, 
encouraging efficient infrastructure construction, avoiding unnecessary environmental impacts, and 
supporting economically rational customer decisions.6 
The mechanics of the framework were straightforward. A customer seeking new pipeline capacity would 
execute a long-term precedent agreement obligating it to pay two distinct charges: first, its pro-rata share of 
the existing system rate, and second, an incremental expansion charge specifically designed to recover the 
costs of the new facilities required to serve that customer. Typically, these agreements operated over a 
fifteen-year term. At the conclusion of the term, the expansion facilities—having been fully depreciated—
would roll into the broader integrated system rate. 
The framework has now operated continuously for more than twenty-five years. While refined in particulars 
over time, its essential structure has remained intact and repeatedly survived judicial review. In recent years, 
federal appellate courts have reaffirmed FERC’s authority to require incremental pricing and reinforced the 
broader principle that expansion-related costs should follow cost causation principles rather than be 
imposed on existing customers who derive no corresponding benefit.7,8

Adapting the 1999 natural gas framework to electric transmission infrastructure would require neither 
statutory change nor a novel assertion of agency authority. FERC has long recognized that interconnection 
service is a component of transmission service, and its jurisdiction over interstate transmission rate design 
is well established.9 
Under an electric transmission expansion-rate framework, a hyperscale data center seeking transmission-
voltage interconnection would enter into a long-term agreement with the host transmission owner. That 
agreement would be filed with and reviewed by FERC under the same just-and-reasonable standard 
applicable to other jurisdictional transmission arrangements. 
The agreement would obligate the data center customer to pay two distinct rate components. First, the 
customer would pay its standard pro-rata share of the existing zonal transmission rate, applying the same 
tariff structure applicable to all transmission customers within the zone. Second, the customer would pay an 
expansion rate specifically designed to recover the costs of the network upgrades required to support the 
interconnection. 
Expansion-facility costs would be depreciated on a straight-line basis over the term of the agreement. The 
transmission owner’s return on equity, capital structure, and debt assumptions would mirror those already 
embedded in the incumbent zonal transmission rate, thereby avoiding either preferential treatment or 
punitive pricing. To protect existing customers from residual exposure, the data center developer would also 
maintain creditworthy collateral support equal to the remaining unrecovered balance of the expansion 
facilities over the life of the agreement. 
The default contract term would be fifteen years, consistent with the established natural gas model. At the 
conclusion of the term, the expansion facilities—having been fully recovered—would transition into the 
integrated system rate base. 
Additional structural protections could further strengthen the framework. For example, the customer’s 
transmission-rate contribution could be calculated based on the higher of actual demand or a minimum 
contractual-demand threshold, preventing cost-shifting through underutilization. Likewise, expansion-rate 
obligations could be reallocated annually as additional customers interconnect to the same facilities, 
reducing the cost burden borne by any individual participant over time. 
Importantly, because the data center would separately finance the expansion facilities, its contribution to the 
broader zonal transmission rate would represent additional revenue supporting existing infrastructure

One legal question warrants attention. FERC has long prohibited what it terms “and pricing” — billing the 
same customer twice for the same transmission capacity.11 The Expansion Rate framework does not raise 
this concern. Each customer pays its pro-rata share of the zonal transmission rate, which covers the existing 
system; only the data center driving the expansion pays the additional expansion rate, which covers the new 
facilities built to serve it. 
Even read more broadly, the “and pricing” prohibition is Commission policy, not a court-imposed rule. The 
federal appellate courts that have addressed it have confirmed that “all transmission service... must be 
provided at a just and reasonable rate,” but they have not adopted a categorical rule requiring FERC to apply 
the prohibition.12 The Commission can adjust the policy where circumstances warrant, as it did in the 1990s 
when the natural gas pipeline industry confronted a similar shift. 
IV. A Framework That Works for Everyone 
 
American Energy Consumers 
Households and small businesses would no longer be required to subsidize infrastructure developed 
primarily to serve hyperscale facilities. Expansion costs would remain tied to the customer driving the need 
for the infrastructure, improving fairness and strengthening energy affordability protections. 
Data Center Developers and Hyperscale Operators 
Large capital-intensive projects require predictability above all else. A FERC-reviewed, long-term filed rate 
would provide a known and financeable cost structure from the outset, replacing uncertain ad hoc 
negotiations with a transparent and commercially workable framework aligned with the operational life of 
the facilities. 
Utilities and Transmission Owners 
Transmission owners are currently caught between the economic-development imperative to accommodate 
large new loads and the obligation to shield existing customers from resulting rate impacts. An expansion-

American Economic Competitiveness and Jobs 
Data centers represent foundational infrastructure for artificial intelligence, cloud computing, financial 
systems, logistics, and national security applications. A predictable transmission-cost framework would 
encourage large-scale digital infrastructure investment in the United States, supporting construction 
employment, supply-chain activity, local tax revenues, and long-term technological leadership. 
The Broader Energy Transition 
The same transmission system serving data centers also supports renewable energy, battery storage, and 
broader electrification initiatives. Clarifying how large-load customers fund network upgrades would reduce 
interconnection bottlenecks, improve planning certainty, and accelerate investment across the broader 
electric grid.13 
Conclusion 
 
FERC already possesses the authority, policy framework, and judicial support necessary to address the 
emerging transmission cost-shift associated with hyperscale data centers. The Commission solved a 
materially identical problem in the natural gas sector more than two decades ago through an incremental 
pricing framework grounded in cost causation principles. Adapting that framework to electric transmission 
infrastructure would protect consumers, provide developers with regulatory certainty, and strengthen the 
nation’s ability to build the modern grid required for economic growth and technological leadership. The 
mechanism already exists. The need is immediate. The time to apply it is now.
