What are the main items of the ComEd filing? 
68 
A:  
ComEd’s filing includes the following core proposed tariff changes: (1) defines a “Large 
69 
Demand Project Applicant or Customer” as a customer expecting greater than or equal to 
70 
50 MW within ten years; (2) formalizes a cluster study approach for large load

III. 
COMED'S 50 MW THRESHOLD FOR LARGE LOAD PROVISIONS 
102 
Q: 
What are your concerns with ComEd’s 50 MW threshold for large load provisions? 
103 
A: 
ComEd uses a fixed threshold of 50 MW within ten years to trigger the “Large Demand” 
104 
provisions. Material bulk power system reliability impacts can occur from large load 
105 
facilities that fall below this 50 MW threshold depending on a variety of factors, including 
106 
system strength at specific locations, congestion on the system, and the type of large load 
107 
facility and operational characteristics. A single MW threshold could risk missing high-
108 
impact projects that are below 50 MW in size but located in a weak, stressed, or congested 
109

part of the transmission system. I would recommend that ComEd keep the 50 MW 
110 
threshold as a primary trigger mechanism for large load provisions, but add a supplemental 
111 
trigger that would invoke the large load provision/processes under a set of defined 
112 
conditions, including but not limited to: (a) any project whose interconnection point is 
113 
identified as “weak” or constrained in ComEd’s studies; (b) any project that requests 
114

115

117

118 
additional supplemental trigger language when added to the tariff language would provide 
119 
ComEd flexibility for unique and difficult interconnection projects.

Q:   What are the potential grid reliability risks when large loads connect without 
155 
corresponding electricity supply commitments?  
156 
In addition to the above-mentioned reliability impacts, rapid large load growth poses a 
157 
generation resource adequacy challenge. Unlike incremental demand increases that can be 
158 
forecast and planned for years in advance, the sudden addition of hundreds, or even 
159 
thousands, of MW of new load can create a step-change in local and regional generation 
160 
capacity needs. If generation resources are not procured or available to serve these new 
161 
loads, the system may face shortages during peak conditions, requiring increased reliance 
162 
on imports or emergency procedures. The NERC LLTF White Paper notes that such 
163 
concentrated demand can strain capacity margins and accelerate the need for new 
164 
generation, which challenges the industry’s existing resource planning and market 
165 
activities to adequately procure the sufficient amount of generation resources. Without 
166 
close coordination between utilities, who are both managing the large load growth and 
167 
procuring generation resources, and ISO/RTO grid operators who are performing capacity 
168 
planning and auctions, there are likely to be system-wide reliability risks in the form of 
169

173

Q:  
What do you recommend that utilities and commissions do to mitigate these risks? 
176 
A: 
Overall, NERC has made clear that large load interconnections present emerging reliability 
177 
concerns that require substantial technical considerations during the large load 
178 
interconnection process. The Elevate/GridLab report provides a series of recommendations 
179 
to utilities and commissions to help mitigate these bulk power system reliability risks. 
180 
These recommendations include: (1) perform the full suite of models and studies of large 
181 
loads during the interconnection process, including steady state, dynamics, short circuit, 
182 
and electromagnetic transient studies; (2) update interconnection requirements for large 
183 
loads to include mandatory ride-through capabilities, performance requirements during 
184 
power system disturbances, power quality requirements, model and information sharing, 
185 
and protection and control coordination activities; (3) create standardized, uniform 
186 
interconnection processes for large loads with defined milestones, adequate fees for 
187 
interconnections, material modification rules, and cluster study arrangements; and (4) 
188 
adopt flexible interconnection arrangements for large loads. Across the country, 
189 
interconnection requirements, processes, and tariffs for large loads should be updated to 
190 
reflect these new requirements in order to ensure bulk power system reliability while 
191 
supporting the safe, reliable, and cost-effective integration of large loads.

Q: 
What does it mean to have a flexible interconnection for a large load facility? 
278 
A: 
A flexible interconnection for a large load facility refers to an interconnection arrangement 
279 
under which the facility does not receive firm, unconstrained transmission service at all 
280 
times, but instead agrees to provide the utility with defined operating flexibility of the 
281 
facility’s loads under certain agreed upon stressed grid conditions. This flexibility can take 
282 
the form of temporary curtailment of load at the facility or reliance on on-site generation 
283 
resources during periods of grid stress or constraint. Various industry research, studies, and 
284 
references have shown that flexibility of large load interconnections can significantly 
285 
expand the amount of large load that can be interconnected without triggering immediate 
286 
and costly system upgrades. 13, 14  In practical terms, a flexible interconnection means that 
287 
the large load facility contracts with the utility or grid operator to reduce or defer load when 
288 
called upon by the utility/grid operator, thereby lowering system peak stress, accelerating 
289 
the interconnection timeline, and reducing costs borne by all customers.
