Reply Comments of GridCARE 
 
GridCARE respectfully submits these reply comments in response to initial comments filed on 
the Commission’s Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) on Large Load and 
Hybrid Interconnections. We appreciate the Commission’s leadership in recognizing that the 
current interconnection framework is not well-suited for the rapidly evolving landscape of large, 
hybrid, and flexible loads, and examining a framework to ensure timely, orderly, and beneficial 
interconnection of large loads.  
 
GridCARE is a U.S. based company that provides utilities, system operators, and large-load 
customers with a secure platform capable of identifying and unlocking latent capacity on the grid 
- capacity that is already built but underutilized. Through AI-enabled temporal analysis, 
probabilistic modeling, and verifiable load flexibility, GridCARE enables faster interconnection 
pathways for large loads and drives beneficial load growth, thereby creating a bridge between 
the time needed to build new infrastructure and resources available to pay for it, while also 
aligning with national AI, economic, and security goals.  
 
Across the record, numerous commenters highlight themes that GridCARE strongly supports, 
including:  
 
●​ Better grid utilization can enable faster interconnections without compromising reliability 
or long-term planning needs;  
●​ Modernized planning is essential to understanding latent grid capacity and the role of 
flexibility; and  
●​ An expedited or “fast-track” study path for projects able to demonstrate verifiable 
flexibility is both feasible and beneficial.   
 
These themes are consistent with regional efforts already underway and reflect a constructive 
path for the Commission’s framework.  
 
II. Background  
 
Several commenters noted that longstanding planning and interconnection 
methodologies—relying heavily on static load profiles, worst-case peak assumptions, and 
deterministic single-snapshot analyses—no longer reflect how the modern grid operates nor 
how large loads actually behave. These methods were designed for an era dominated by 
centralized generation and predictable demand patterns. They do not capture the temporal 

--- PAGE 2 ---
diversity, non-coincident peaks, thermal behavior of assets, or the flexible operational 
characteristics of today’s large loads, including AI data centers and other digital infrastructure 
facilities. 
As a result, planners can underestimate the hosting capacity that exists across the vast majority 
of hours. This gap between actual operational conditions and modeling assumptions contributes 
to the perception that only long-lead infrastructure can serve new load, when in many cases the 
constraint may be limited to a small number of hours or conditions. 
Comments also reflect that utilities and RTOs lack standardized or consistent formats for 
customer-provided data, making it challenging to assess large numbers of requests or 
differentiate between short-duration constraints and structural limitations. These process 
challenges—not physical scarcity—frequently limit access to existing capacity. 
A practical example of improved approaches is emerging through GridCARE’s collaboration with 
Portland General Electric (PGE). Using advanced analytics, PGE and GridCARE identified and 
validated meaningful amounts of latent capacity on existing transmission and distribution 
infrastructure. As publicly reported by PGE,1 more than 80 MW of additional capacity is 
expected to be available for new large loads in 2026, increasing over time as additional 
operational insights are incorporated. In a region where conservative study assumptions 
historically extended interconnection timelines, this work has helped PGE refine planning 
models and identify near-term capacity with greater confidence. 
This analytical foundation has supported early projects, such as the recently announced flexible 
energy deployment by Aligned Data Centers and Calibrant Energy in Hillsboro, Oregon2—an 
example of how utilities and developers can coordinate flexibility and operational data to 
accelerate new load connections while maintaining system reliability and affordability. 
To illustrate the broader potential of latent capacity, GridCARE conducted representative 
modeling demonstrating that interconnecting large AI loads through existing 
infrastructure—where operationally feasible—can reduce systemwide costs by improving asset 
utilization, increasing utility revenues without imposing new costs on existing customers, and 
supporting future capital investments. While specific outcomes will vary by region and system 
topology, the analysis demonstrates that beneficial load growth can occur when interconnection 
pathways reflect real-world system conditions and flexible operational capabilities.3 
By modernizing planning practices, improving visibility into system operations, and enabling a 
process to bring speed to power, at scale, through incentivizing large load interconnections that 
are hybrid or willing to be flexible, the United States can unlock capacity that already exists on 
the system—supporting AI leadership, domestic manufacturing, and national security priorities 
without compromising reliability and waiting years for new infrastructure to come online. 
Modernizing interconnection and planning approaches can complement long-term investment 
rather than replace it, enabling near-term load growth while utilities build the assets needed for 
the next decade.

III. Initial Comments Support Modernized Interconnection and Latent-Capacity 
Approaches 
The initial comments reflect considerable alignment across diverse stakeholders—RTOs, 
utilities, data center operators, technology providers, and public interest organizations—on 
several themes relevant to unlocking latent capacity and incorporating flexibility.  
Simultaneous study of load, flexibility, and any co-located resources 
Commenters across the spectrum—including PJM, ISO-NE, SPP, Amazon Energy, PG&E, 
National Grid, and the Energy Storage Coalition—agree that modern large loads cannot be 
evaluated as static, inflexible facilities. They increasingly come with on-site storage, 
demand-shaping capabilities, thermal management flexibility, backup or supplemental 
generation, or the ability to coordinate operations across multiple sites. 
Studying load and resources together can reveal significantly different system impacts than 
studying each independently. Commenters note that this approach better reflects actual 
operating conditions and can avoid overstating upgrade needs. 
Recognizing flexibility in interconnection studies and planning  
Many commenters support the Commission’s proposal to establish an expedited or alternative 
pathway for large loads willing and able to operate flexibly. Others highlight that operational 
flexibility, when verified, may reduce peak-hour impacts and allow planners to distinguish 
between constraints that are structural versus those that are transient or condition-specific. 
GridCARE agrees that flexibility, when defined in measurable and verifiable terms, can help 
accelerate interconnections, reduce restudy risks, provide near-term operational headroom, and 
maintain reliability. This does not replace long-term investment; rather, it enables the system to 
serve new load while that investment is underway. 
RTOs and ISOs are already developing frameworks that incorporate flexibility and latent 
capacity 
PJM’s Critical Issue Fast Path, SPP’s High-Impact Large Load process, and ISO-NE’s ongoing 
work on load characterization all demonstrate that regions are exploring ways to streamline 
studies, incorporate temporal and behavioral data, better align study processes with actual 
system operations, and improve coordination with utilities.  
GridCARE supports the Commission enabling this direction through a national framework while 
allowing regional entities to tailor implementation. 
Higher grid utilization supports affordability and protects current customers 
Public interest organizations, state commissions, and utilities highlight the need to prevent 
unnecessary cost shifts. Better identification of latent capacity and more precise 
characterization of load behavior can help ensure that upgrades are only required where they 
are truly necessary.

IV. Recommendations 
Based on the record and GridCARE’s experience working with utilities and large-load 
customers, GridCARE respectfully offers the following recommendations: 
 
1.​ Establish a national framework that recognizes flexibility and modern operational 
characteristics.  
The comprehensive portfolio of resources and technologies now available—ranging from 
front-of-the-meter and co-location flexibility (like utility-scale storage and fast-ramping 
generation) and energy storage (grid-side and customer-side battery systems) to coordinated 
behind-the-meter generation (backup generators and CHP) and advanced transmission 
technologies (Dynamic Line Ratings, PSTs)—collectively form a significant and valuable 
flexibility ecosystem that can be orchestrated to reduce net loading on critical elements during 
peak or emergency conditions, shape and cap large load demand, and unlock additional 
transfer capability. Systematically incorporating this flexibility ecosystem into planning and 
operations will unlock existing capacity on the grid, accelerate interconnections, and enable 
beneficial load growth to preserve customer affordability and fund new infrastructure investment.  
Realizing the value of this flexibility ecosystem requires a transparent, consistent national 
framework, with high-level expectations for incorporating temporal, behavioral, and operational 
data into studies, rules and tariffs. The Commission can leverage its approach in establishing 
the framework and principles of measurement, telemetry, verification, and performance-based 
obligations in Order No. 2222 for non-traditional distributed resources to guide a modernized, 
technology-neutral approach for flexible large-load interconnections, while retaining regional 
discretion to customize methodologies. 
2.​ Encourage a First-Served, Flex-Ready Cluster Study Process 
Several transmission providers have begun implementing cluster studies for large load 
interconnections. However, these analyses do not offer a proactive, system-wide view of 
transmission headroom in high demand areas. Cluster studies themselves do not create 
additional capacity or materially accelerate large load interconnections; they simply allocate 
whatever transmission capacity already exists. 
To improve transparency and reduce siting uncertainty, the Commission could encourage or 
require transmission providers to publish transmission level hosting capacity maps or locational 
headroom indicators that give large load customers signals on where interconnection is most 
feasible. If proposed loads can demonstrate verifiable operational flexibility, transmission 
providers should also offer clear estimates of how such flexibility could reduce study duration or 
accelerate interconnection timelines. 
3.​ Encourage optional expedited pathways for large loads able to demonstrate flexibility 
A national framework should recognize that some loads can materially change their system 
impacts through operational or technological measures. An expedited study and interconnection 
pathway, subject to verified performance and data requirements, could improve study efficiency, 
reduce queues and restudies, accelerate beneficial load growth, and help bridge the gap 
between near-term demand and long-term infrastructure buildout.  
While not mandating specific technologies, the Commission can acknowledge that modern 
planning and operational tools—capable of analyzing hourly and sub-hourly behavior,

non-coincident peaks, and operational interactions and backup generation technologies — can 
improve the accuracy of interconnection studies, provide overall system benefits, and reduce 
unnecessary upgrades.  
The Commission should also support study approaches that clearly distinguish between 
non-exporting, load-modifying behind-the-meter resources and true generation resources 
seeking firm transmission rights. Applying generator-level deliverability assumptions to 
non-export configurations can overstate impacts, increase upgrade requirements, and erode the 
very flexibility the ANOPR seeks to unlock. This distinction is fully consistent with DOE’s 
proposed principle that hybrid facilities should be studied according to the specific injection and 
withdrawal rights they request. A non-exporting behind-the-meter storage system, by definition, 
requests zero injection rights and should therefore be studied as a load-modifying resource 
rather than as a generator seeking firm deliverability.  
At the same time, a modern framework should recognize that these behind-the-meter assets 
may play a more significant system role in the future. As data center energy efficiency improves 
and additional headroom becomes available, some non-exporting resources could be capable 
of providing broader reliability or energy services—subject to safeguards and appropriately 
scoped future interconnection review. Utilities and system operators already demonstrate the 
practicality of such approaches; for example, ERCOT permits limited-export and non-firm 
injection configurations for hybrid and storage resources, providing a workable template for how 
controlled export rights can be managed without triggering full deliverability requirements.4 
Designing study methods that differentiate these configurations today, while anticipating 
pathways for optional future system contributions will reduce uncertainty, avoid unnecessary 
costs, and preserve incentives for large loads to invest in flexibility that supports both 
interconnection efficiency and system reliability. 
4.​ Establish load-based study fees and performance incentives to improve queue discipline 
and accelerate interconnection timelines  
Consistent with DOE Principle #4 and the study-fee structure adopted in Order No. 2023 for 
generator interconnections, the Commission should establish a load-interconnection study-fee 
framework that scales with the size and complexity of the proposed load and reflects the level of 
operational flexibility demonstrated by the customer. Such fees would discourage duplicative or 
speculative requests, reduce inflated load forecasts, and ensure that study resources are 
aligned with actual system impacts.  
In parallel, the Commission should consider performance-based incentives for transmission 
providers tied to each megawatt of load interconnected on an accelerated or 
performance-defined timeline. Providing timely, flexible large-load interconnections delivers 
systemwide benefits, including improved grid utilization, increased utility revenues without 
shifting costs to existing customers, and faster realization of national economic and security 
priorities. Incentives tied to verified outcomes would encourage utilities to adopt modernized

5.​ Reinforce that latent capacity and flexibility complement, but do not replace, 
transmission and resource development 
Numerous commenters emphasize—and GridCARE agrees—that long-term resource adequacy 
and transmission expansion remain essential. A framework that better utilizes existing assets 
can serve near-term load, maintain affordability, and support the investment environment for 
utilities while long-lead projects progress through permitting and construction. 
6.​ Allow RTOs/ISOs and utilities to tailor implementation 
Given regional diversity in grid structure, generation mix, and regulatory frameworks, 
implementation should be grounded in regional stakeholder processes, subject to Commission 
oversight. FERC’s role should be to establish a consistent structure and principles, while 
enabling flexibility in how regions meet them. 
V. Conclusion 
The record demonstrates strong support for modernizing interconnection frameworks to reflect 
the operational realities of today’s large loads and the evolving grid. Approaches that 
incorporate temporal behavior, flexible operations, and co-located resources can help unlock 
latent capacity and accelerate interconnections while maintaining reliability and affordability. 
GridCARE appreciates the Commission’s leadership in advancing this discussion and supports 
a final framework that recognizes flexibility, encourages modern analytical approaches, enables 
optional expedited pathways, and allows regions to adapt implementation to their specific 
system needs. 
Such an approach can help bridge the gap between immediate large-load requirements and the 
timelines for building new infrastructure, supporting national economic and security priorities 
while protecting existing customers and upholding reliability standards.
