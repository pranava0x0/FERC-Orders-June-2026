Comments in response to the proposed ANOPR.

managing affordability — all while working to promulgate new rules and regulations to allow for 
the transformation of our nation’s aging electricity grid to accommodate newer technologies, novel 
load configurations, and support national security and national interests. With an evolving resource

II. 
COMMENTS  
A. Electricity Demand Is Projected To Increase Exponentially Due To Large 
Load Additions And Standalone Storage Is A Solution 
While U.S. electricity consumption was essentially flat for the last two decades, the 
electricity demand picture has changed dramatically in just the last two years. Grid operators have 
been sounding the alarm of the incongruent supply and demand outlook leading to potential supply 
deficits, and while there are many contributing factors, e.g., rapid deactivations of thermal 
generation, slow pace of entry of new supply resources, increasing electrification, and onshoring 
of manufacturing, and population growth, it is the unprecedented projected electricity demand for 
the integration and expansion of large load additions, namely data centers to support national 
security and American Leadership in Artificial intelligence, driving this unprecedented electricity 
demand growth. 
Artificial intelligence is a national priority integral to national security and American 
economic leadership. The Administration recently released its America AI Action Plan, which 
underscores that winning the global “AI race” will require not only rapid innovation in models and

applications, but also an unprecedented buildout of domestic AI infrastructure, chips, data centers, 
and “the energy to power it all.” Upstream of data center buildout is the energy needed to power it

demands “vastly greater energy generation than we have today” and calls for streamlined 
permitting for data centers and associated energy infrastructure, as well as a comprehensive

Given this framework, esVolta agrees that there is a compelling need for the Commission 
to adopt reforms as outlined in the Department’s Letter initiating this ANOPR. Absent clear, 
standardized procedures for large load and hybrid interconnections, transmission providers will 
continue to rely on ad hoc approaches that risk delaying critical projects, exacerbating reliability 
concerns, and shifting costs to non-participating customers in ways that are inconsistent with open 
access and just and reasonable rate requirements.  With the initiation of a comprehensive and 
transparent rulemaking process, the Commission can continue the work it has started and provide 
the regulatory certainty necessary to support national AI and manufacturing priorities, while 
protecting grid reliability and existing customers. If an ANOPR is issued, it should seek to include 
a proposal that articulates core principles, such as studying large loads and hybrid facilities 
together with generation where practicable, recognizing curtailable and dispatchable 
configurations (including those supported by BESS) for expedited treatment, and aligning cost 
responsibility with the specific injection and withdrawal rights requested.

1. BESS Can Enhance Power Quality to Assist in Facilitating the 
Timely and Orderly Interconnection of Large Load Consistent with 
the Principles of Reform 
 
As the Department correctly recognizes, continued U.S. leadership in AI and the digital 
economy will depend in significant part on the timely development and interconnection of new 
data centers and other large, power-intensive computing facilities. esVolta supports this growth 
and agrees that Commission-jurisdictional transmission providers should facilitate the orderly 
integration of such large loads in a manner consistent with open-access and reliability obligations. 
At the same time, this new class of high-density, power-electronics-rich demand introduces distinct 
power quality and system performance challenges that legacy interconnection and planning 
frameworks were not designed to address.  
In esVolta’s view, the Commission’s large-load reforms present an important opportunity 
to recognize that BESS can directly mitigate these power quality concerns and thereby assist in 
facilitating the timely and orderly interconnection of large loads consistent with the principles of 
reform outlined in this proceeding.

Data centers are known to display large load spikes and immediate drops in the matter of 
seconds due to dramatic rises in server rack power density. Recent reporting based on Bloomberg 
analysis underscores that large AI data centers are already degrading power quality on portions of 
the U.S. grid.8 The analysis found a strong correlation between proximity to data centers and 
elevated “bad harmonics,” with the most severe power-quality distortions concentrated in areas of 
dense data center development. At the same time, ERCOT and national laboratories have 
documented that sub synchronous oscillations from data center training can cause catastrophic 
turbine damage to facilities in close proximity. Data Centers tend to exhibit steep, short-duration 
changes in demand and highly power-electronics-intensive load profiles that can cause voltage 
excursions, frequency deviations, flicker, and other power quality issues at the point of 
interconnection. If not mitigated, these impacts can degrade service to neighboring customers and 
generation, and in many cases, necessitate more extensive and costly network upgrades to ensure 
continued compliance with applicable reliability standards and Open Access Transmission Tariff 
(“OATT”) requirements. 
BESS is particularly well-suited to address the power quality challenges associated with 
large, rapidly changing loads such as AI data centers and high-performance computing facilities. 
Standalone BESS also can furnish a suite of power quality and reliability services that directly 
advance the principles for reform identified in this proceeding, including maintaining reliability, 
maximizing the efficient use of existing infrastructure, and supporting just and reasonable cost 
allocation.   
Because BESS interfaces with the grid through advanced inverters and digital controls, it 
can function as a fast, controllable buffer between the large load and the bulk power system. When 
properly configured, BESS can be dispatched to smooth the net load seen by the transmission

2. Battery Energy Storage is Electron and Fuel Agnostic Presenting 
Power Source Flexibility for Data Centers  
 
BESS provides data center and grid operators alike with the needed flexibility as it is 
electron and fuel agnostic, curtailable, and dispatchable pairing well with all other energy 
generation resources, including on the power grid and/or hosted at a data center’s site. BESS is 
electron-agnostic in that it charges or stores generation produced by all resources—natural gas, 
coal, and nuclear generating facilities, as well as solar and wind. BESS discharges the stored 
energy back onto the grid when energy demand is high and the transmission system is experiencing 
constraints. Battery energy storage is a critical resource relied on to maintain resource adequacy 
and reliability. 
BESS also complements all resource types, for example, BESS can enhance the efficiency 
and flexibility of natural gas plants by responding to dispatch orders to provide ancillary services, 
allowing thermal resources the ability to offer output into consistent need, high-demand energy 
markets, minimizing continuous ramping of the thermal facilities. The continuous ramping up to 
offer short spurts of energy provides needless wear and tear on the equipment, causing more 
occurrences of maintenance outages and ultimately decreasing the overall life of the thermal asset. 
For example, in Texas, ERCOT’s CEO Pablo Vegas mused about this very issue: 
“In most of the summer, wind has ramped up as solar output diminished at nightfall, which 
"has aligned well with the demand profile," Vegas said. Also, the state's thermal 
dispatchable fleet has delivered "a very strong and steady performance," he said …but 
"Coming off a long stretch of very high demand and high utilization, it's not surprising to

3. Battery Energy Storage Can Ensure “Speed to Power” With the 
Ability to Be Commercially Operational Within 12-18 Months from 
Interconnection 
RTO/ISOs have made significant improvements in the timelines for processing and 
studying interconnection request, but the immediate need for power to fuel data centers cannot 
wait through the normal processing timeframes. In supporting the President’s “energy dominance” 
and “AI” agenda, esVolta supports expedited interconnection processes for all of the above 
resources that have agreements with large loads.   
Battery energy storage is a proven solution to meeting near-term and longer-term resource 
adequacy and reliability needs, with a modular design that can be right sized based on need, and 
one of the shortest construction time frames of any resource (12-18 months). As the nation faces 
imminent resource adequacy and reliability shortfalls, flexible resources that can be deployed in 
the near term, such as battery energy storage, are essential and must be properly valued for their 
speed to market. In contrast, it was mentioned at the technical conference that, on average, gas 
plants and other resources will take approximately five+ years.  
With more than 12 GWs of energy storage resources added to the grid in 2024, storage has 
become the fastest-growing and most rapidly deployed energy infrastructure, it flexible in its 
configuration, and has proven its ability to deploy quickly.10 Given its fast deployment, data 
centers would benefit from the ability to share interconnection rights with existing storage 
interconnection rights.

B. Load and Hybrid Facilities Should Be Studied Together With Generating 
Facilities  
The ANOPR properly recognizes that there would be efficiencies gained in studying load 
and hybrid facilities together with identified generating facilities. 11 Studying large loads together 
with BESS aligns closely with the Department’s emphasis on the timely and orderly 
interconnection of large loads. Where a large load is electrically proximate to BESS that is 
designed to provide the services described above, the combined configuration is better able to 
satisfy applicable reliability and power quality criteria without imposing excessive incremental 
burdens on the transmission system. 
 From the perspective of transmission providers and RTOs/ISOs, such paired 
configurations are more straightforward to study and integrate because the BESS can be modeled 
as a controllable injection and withdrawal resource at the same point of interconnection that 
actively mitigates the grid impacts of the associated large load. BESS enables the large loads to be 
curtailable or flexible and should jointly be eligible for an expedited pathway to interconnection.12

1. Standalone BESS Located Within the Same Load Deliverability 
Area or Local Resource Zone is Well-suited to Serve Large Loads 
and Receive Expedited Interconnection Study Treatment Under 
Any New Commission Policy  
esVolta encourages the Commission to consider a similar approach in any rulemaking it 
promulgates to address the integration of large loads and ensure that standalone BESS either  
located “in close proximity”, “near by”  the data center, or either electrically or co-located onsite 
with the data center or the large load is deemed an eligible supply resource to receive expedited 
interconnection study treatment along with the data center load. esVolta would go even further to 
support approaches in PJM that rightfully identify supply resources in the same “load 
deliverability area or LDA” as an eligible resource for the expedited interconnection process and 
similarly, in MISO, standalone storage and other resources that have agreements with large loads 
to provide needed resources and are located in the same “local resource zone or LRZ” should be 
deemed eligible for an expedited interconnection process completed within 90 days. esVolta also 
supports allowing data centers to share interconnection rights and integrate with energy storage 
interconnection requests to facilitate sharing of grid capacity.
