RE: Mandatory Invocation of Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act 
(NHPA) and Executive Order 13175 Regarding the Proposed Stratos Data Center and 
Associated 9-Gigawatt Energy Infrastructure, Box Elder County, Utah. 
 
To Whom It May Concern, 
 
I am writing as an affected 5th generation descendent of 7 of the founding families of Brigham 
City, Utah, and stakeholder regarding the proposed Stratos AI data center project spanning 
approximately 40,000 contiguous acres in the Hansel Valley region of Box Elder County, Utah. 
This massive project requires a direct infrastructure interconnection to the interstate Ruby 
Pipeline (under the jurisdiction of the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) and a major New 
Source Review (NSR) Air Permit (under the jurisdiction of the U.S. Environmental Protection 
Agency) to burn natural gas for a massive, on-site 9-gigawatt power generation facility.  
 
Pursuant to 36 CFR Part 800 (Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act), I hereby 
demand that the responsible federal agencies formally invoke the Section 106 review process 
immediately. The agencies cannot legally approve any technical permits, air quality 
modifications, or infrastructure taps for this project until this mandatory review is completed. 
 
1. Establishment of a Clear Federal Nexus The Stratos project constitutes a federal 
undertaking under 36 CFR § 800.16(y) due to multiple required federal actions:  
 
FERC Jurisdiction: The physical tap and delivery interconnect on the interstate Ruby Pipeline 
require federal regulatory approval. 
 
EPA Jurisdiction: The immense carbon and particulate emissions from the proposed 9-GW gas 
plant require federal Title V / Clean Air Act approvals. 
 
Department of Defense Land Nexus: The project boundaries intentionally encompass a parcel of 
the Utah Test and Training Range, a federal military asset. 
 
2. Direct Threats to Culturally Significant, Historic, and Sacred Landscapes 
The Area of Potential Effects (APE) for this 40,000-acre development sits within a highly 
sensitive regional landscape that is dense with irreplaceable historical, tribal, and archaeological 
assets. Physical grading, heavy construction, and long-term industrial operations directly 
threaten: 
Shoshone Ancestral Heritage: The footprint is culturally continuous with the sacred ancestral 
lands of the Northwestern Band of the Shoshone Nation and the broader Bear River Massacre 
landscape, containing known and unmapped Native American artifacts. 
 
Pioneer and Transit History: The project threatens the paths walked by early pioneers, including 
visible, un-excavated pioneer ruts and historical western migration trails. 
 
Historical Gravesites and Cemeteries: The boundary encompasses or sits immediately adjacent to 
the historic Kelton Cemetery, the Kalpikoff Graves, and numerous vulnerable unmarked burial 
sites spanning centuries of local history.

Institutional and Wartime Landmarks: The region's history includes the legacy of the 
Intermountain Indian School and the Bushnell World War II-era hospital, sites of immense 
historical consequence to northern Utah that require rigorous protective buffers. 
 
3. Severe Environmental Implications 
Beyond cultural degradation, the scale of this project poses catastrophic environmental risks that 
require full federal environmental impact assessments: 
 
Air Quality and Climate Footprint: Burning natural gas to generate 9 gigawatts of power will 
result in a projected 64% spike in Utah's entire carbon emissions, dumping massive particulate 
pollution directly over regional communities. 
 
Hydrological Strain and Stormwater Runoff: Transforming 40,000 acres of open rangeland into 
an impervious concrete campus of data facilities will permanently alter natural drainage basins. 
This creates severe stormwater runoff, local stream erosion, and flooding risks. 
 
Disaster Exacerbation and Ecological Threat: The increased runoff and water table degradation 
pose a direct threat to the fragile ecosystem of the nearby Great Salt Lake, potentially worsening 
toxic dust storms and regional environmental crises already managed under federal emergency 
frameworks. 
 
4. Formal Requests and Demands 
As a local stakeholder, I explicitly request the following actions from the reviewing federal 
agencies: 
 
Immediate Invocation: Formally trigger the Section 106 process and coordinate with the 
Advisory Council on Historic Preservation (ACHP).Mandatory Tribal Consultation: Initiate 
formal, government-to-government consultation with the Northwestern Band of the Shoshone 
Nation under Executive Order 13175, independent of any state-level MIDA proceedings. 
 
Granting of Consulting Party Status: I hereby request official Consulting Party Status under 36 
CFR § 800.2(c)(5) as an interested citizen, and demand to be included in all future 
correspondence, public hearings, and reviews regarding the definition of the Area of Potential 
Effects (APE). No federal agency may legally bypass these requirements by deferring to Utah's 
Military Installation Development Authority (MIDA), as state-level zoning overrides do not 
exempt federal agencies from their statutory obligations under the National Historic Preservation 
Act.
