I. 
Introduction 
The Working for Advanced Transmission Technologies (WATT) Coalition offers these 
supplemental comments in response to the Secretary of Energy’s (“Secretary”) proposed 
Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) regarding the interconnection of 
large loads to the interstate transmission system in the above-docketed proceeding. 
Our initial comments,1 submitted with the Advancing Modern Powerlines (AMP) 
Coalition, defined and discussed the key value of Advanced Transmission Technologies 
(“ATTs”) as the fastest way to increase transmission capacity to meet the needs of new

II. 
New examples of Grid Enhancing Technologies facilitating large load 
interconnection 
Some leading American transmission owners are using GETs to manage new large load 
interconnections.  
Georgia Power Company and Pacific Gas & Electric Corporation are demonstrating the 
value of APFC, with deployments that have unlocked more than 100 MW of capacity for 
new industrial and computing loads.7 Georgia Power’s first large-scale deployment uses 
21 SmartValve modules installed across two 230kV circuits. Georgia Power also 
announced a further deployment expected to be live in 2028. PG&E’s deployment is 
expected to be live by the end of 2026. The APFC devices in California will continue to 
be used after a transmission upgrade expected to come online in 2032, but with different

Basin Electric Power Cooperative8 has deployed DLR to help manage growth of oil and 
gas development in Northwest North Dakota, where load has more than doubled in the 
last 10 years and is forecasted to continue growing. Northwestern North Dakota had the 
most congested single transmission line in the Southwest Power Pool (SPP) footprint in

earliest possible relief 18 months away. DLR was an immediate solution. 
Basin Electric presented their initial DLR results at DTECH Midwest 2025, with findings 
between July and December 2024 of: 
• Capacity above static rating 90% of the time. 
• Avoided 500 hours of congestion (12% of period). 
• Paid back full capital cost after just 38 days of operation. 
• Over $20 million of realized savings from congestion in 2024 alone. 
These examples show that the Commission action on GETs could unlock significant 
additional grid capacity for large loads. With Commission guidance and encouragement, 
many other transmission owners could unlock capacity comparable to these examples.

IV. 
Policy lever: require reporting on potential of GETs to address grid 
congestion and improve investment efficiency 
The following suggestions are focused on reporting requirements, including: (1) requiring 
regional transmission organizations (RTOs) to study the potential for DLR to address 
congestion; (2) requiring RTOs to study the potential for other GETs to address 
congestion; and (3) requiring transmission owners to report on asset replacement and 
maintenance investments. The Commission has broad authority to direct reporting; 
reporting is generally a low-burden requirement; and the increased transparency from 
reporting on congestion and the potential for GETs to mitigate such congestion and

We also recommend that the Commission require all transmission owners to report on 
asset replacement and maintenance investments to establish a baseline to understand how 
much of transmission investment is going into this category compared to capacity 
expansion. As so much grid expansion took place in the 1930s-1980s, the transmission 
system is naturally in an asset replacement cycle. However, asset replacement decisions 
must be made with consideration of load growth, changing generation sources, and other 
dynamics. In many cases, it will be cost effective to use ATTs in addition to or in place of 
traditional infrastructure.  
To understand how much investment is being made without increasing grid capacity, and 
identify leading transmission owners who are investing strategically to increase capacity, 
the Commission should require all transmission owners to report on grid investment 
compared to grid expansion, i.e., how much money they put into system maintenance and 
replacement, compared to system expansion. A metric could be a ratio of additional 
transfer capacity added or enabled (potentially scaled by the length of the pathways) per 
million dollars invested in grid infrastructure. Transmission owners should calculate this 
metric every year based on past investment and assess whether investment efficiency 
could be increased through adoption of new technologies.

Section 219 of the Federal Power Act authorizes FERC to establish appropriate 
incentives to encourage transmission development.45 This includes encouraging ATTs.46 
FERC Order No. 679, issued pursuant to that statutory authority, allows transmission 
owners to propose packages of incentives for transmission investment that are “tailored to 
address the demonstrable risks or challenges faced by the applicant,” and create just and 
reasonable rates.47 But the Order stops short of clarifying the incentives that could be 
proposed to encourage accelerated and widespread deployment of ATTs. 
Order No. 679 does enumerate options for incentive enhancements, including return on 
equity (ROE) adders to attract investment and ensure cost recovery if the transmission 
owner has to cancel the project for reasons outside of its control. The Order also leaves 
the door open for “any other incentives approved by the Commission, pursuant to the 
requirements of this paragraph, that are determined to be just and reasonable and not 
unduly discriminatory or preferential.”48 In 2019, FERC reported that it had acted on 109 
incentive applications for more than $80 billion in anticipated construction costs, but we 
have not heard of any proposals for ATTs to receive incentive treatment.

IX. 
Conclusion 
ATTs can deliver meaningful increases in transmission capacity to facilitate the 
interconnection of large load in less than three years. While recent deployments by 
PG&E, Georgia Power and others validate this potential, utility adoption remains limited. 
In addition to the interconnection of large loads, ATTs can reduce congestion, lower 
costs for consumers, and improve reliability. 
We recommend that the Commission require consideration of ATTs in large load 
interconnection studies, consistent with recommendations from a broad range of 

--- PAGE 32 ---
 
 
 
 
stakeholders. However, limiting ATTs to the interconnection process alone will not 
unlock their full value. To achieve the ANOPR’s goals of enabling faster load 
interconnection, supporting economic growth, and maintaining affordable and reliable 
electric service, we also recommend that the Commission encourage and facilitate the 
broader integration of ATTs into transmission planning, operational planning, and system 
operations. 
Accordingly, the WATT Coalition urges the Commission to: (1) require reporting and 
studies that quantify the potential for GETs and other ATTs to address congestion and 
improve transmission investment efficiency; (2) encourage transmission owners and 
RTOs to establish operational processes focused on increasing grid efficiency and 
unlocking available capacity; (3) refresh the Commission’s record on ATT deployment 
through targeted information gathering; (4) support regional forums and technical 
conferences to accelerate knowledge sharing and best practices; and (5) provide guidance 
on transmission incentives for technologies increase the capacity and efficiency of 
existing transmission facilities and improve the operation of the facilities. These steps can 
be taken quickly and show results in the next year. Their follow-on effects will also help 
with longer-term policy, including potentially finalizing the rule proposed in Docket No. 
24-6-000 on the Implementation of Dynamic Line Ratings with a threshold-based 
requirement for DLR deployments.

That said, recently, the industry has found itself in a new age where technological 
advances, particularly grid-enhancing technologies (“GETs”), allow grid operators and 
experts to explore and embrace the “computational complexity”13 of topology 
optimization. By “topology optimization,” SPP refers to alteration of a transmission 
network’s configuration in a way that economically and reliably manages congestion. 
Such reconfiguration has the potential to reduce production and congestions costs. By 
way of example, from 2020 to 2021, SPP congestion costs increased from $450 million 
to $1.2 billion.14 GETs which allow for, say, topology reconfiguration have the 
potential to decrease costs such as these.   
 
The revisions proposed herein effectuate a supplemental approach to congestion 
management. These revisions, which, again, SPP terms “topology optimization,” aim 
to improve the efficiency and reliability of the grid by economically and reliably 
altering the network configuration. Market Participants may submit proposals for 
reconfigurations due to outages or constraints, or SPP may itself proffer a 
reconfiguration. In either case, SPP then analyzes the potential reconfiguration to 
determine whether it satisfies the requirements set forth in the Tariff, including, for 
example, whether the market as a whole will benefit from the reconfiguration. As SPP 
does with other processes today, SPP works with the affected local transmission 
operators (“TOP”) to mitigate any reliability risk posed by the reconfiguration. If none 
are identified, and as real-time conditions allow, then SPP will implement the solution.

First, Section 4.6.1 states that SPP, as Transmission Provider, will analyze only “valid” 
requests. By “valid,” the language refers to (1) those requests which satisfy the 
requirements set forth in Section 4.6, meaning those requests which submit the 
necessary information; and (2) requests which are not in exceedance of any limit 
described in the Market Protocols.  
 
Second, Section 4.6.1 describes the analysis undertaken by SPP. Specifically, SPP’s 
analysis will assess the proposed reconfiguration’s effect on (1) system reliability; (2) 
regional production cost; and (3) TCR Market funding. If the request does not satisfy 
any one of these enumerated points, then SPP will deny the request.  
 
First, with respect to system reliability, if SPP’s analysis demonstrates that the 
reconfiguration would detrimentally affect the system’s reliability, then the request will 
be denied. This is consistent with how SPP takes market actions today—no market 
action is undertaken if the market action will interfere with system reliability.  
 
Second, with respect to “regional production cost,” any reconfiguration must result in 
production cost savings, meaning that lower costs are realized as a result of the 
reconfiguration by, for example, reduced congestion costs or the displacement of
