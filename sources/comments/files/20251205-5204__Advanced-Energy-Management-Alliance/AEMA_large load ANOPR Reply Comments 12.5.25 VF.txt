I. 
INTRODUCTION 
The Advanced Energy Management Alliance (AEMA)1 respectfully submits these Reply 
Comments in response to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission’s ("Commission") October 
27, 2025 Notice Inviting Comments regarding the interconnection of large loads. AEMA submits 
these comments to address the record established during the initial comment period, which 
includes a wide cross-section of leading industry voices in support of encouraging and rewarding 
flexible loads, such as data centers. 
Review of the over 200 initial comments reveal substantial support —from approximately 112 
supportive commenters versus roughly 15 cautionary voices (and 45 neutral commenters) — for 
the implementation of expedited interconnection pathways and larger interconnection capacities 
for loads capable of flexibly managing their demand. Specifically, comments filed by 
transmission owners, technology and data center companies, and solution providers reinforce the 
need for a technology-neutral framework—such as the "Flexible Load Fast Track" (FLFT) 
proposed in AEMA’s initial comments—that recognizes that load flexibility can be achieved 
through software and hardware solutions and rewards flexibility based on performance-based 
criteria. 2 
In advancing this framework, AEMA urges the Commission to adopt an expressly ‘all-of-the-
above’ approach in which any particular technology (whether storage, thermal resources, on-site 
generation, or software-defined controls) or combination thereof is encouraged so long as it can 
verifiably deliver the flexibility attributes that support reliability. While AEMA supports the 
ability of customers to "bring their own generation" to expedite interconnection, the record 
demonstrates that this must not be the exclusive pathway. The fastest way to connect new loads 
that advances affordability, effectively drives investment into U.S. competitiveness and AI

II. 
INDUSTRY CONSENSUS FOR FLEXIBILITY AS A "BRIDGE" TO 
INTERCONNECTION 
The record reflects a convergence of opinion among diverse stakeholders that flexible load 
capabilities should be leveraged to bridge the gap between immediate interconnection needs and 
the longer timelines required for traditional transmission upgrades, accelerating AI infrastructure 
investments while also deferring or avoiding upgrades that can raise costs for ratepayers. The 
record supports the inclusion of energy resources that contribute to flexibility as well as software 
defined flexibility solutions as valid mechanisms for load management. 
● Constellation Energy Generation, LLC ("Constellation") states that "interruptible 
service can serve as a bridge to full service while transmission upgrades are completed" 
(page 24). Constellation further notes that an "interruptible co-located load does not 
require the transmission system to be planned, built, or adapted in any way" to meet the 
full potential demand because the load can be interrupted (page 25).  
● National Grid provides direct transmission owner support for this approach, stating they 
are "actively working to implement solutions to make this possible, including through our 
engagements with companies such as Emerald AI which focus on advanced analytics and 
flexible demand management" (page 2). National Grid affirms that large loads, "if 
integrated with flexibility... can help contribute to overall cost efficiency" (page 2). 
● Southwest Power Pool (SPP) is cited by multiple commenters, including the Arkansas 
Public Service Commission, for developing the "Conditional High Impact Large Load 
Service (CHILLS)," a tariff offering designed to allow large loads to connect subject to 
curtailment or interruption until requisite transmission is available (page 6). 
● Google similarly recommends that the Commission "prioritize reforms that will expedite 
the interconnection of facilities that maximize the use of existing infrastructure," 
specifically for loads that "agree to be curtailable" (page 11).  
● Microsoft similarly supports expedited study processes for loads that "voluntarily agree 
to be curtailable," noting this is critical for "accelerating the deployment of critical AI 
infrastructure" (page 10).

III. 
DEFINING TECHNOLOGY-NEUTRAL, PERFORMANCE-BASED 
FLEXIBILITY 
The comments filed in this proceeding support a definition of flexibility that is performance-
based rather than asset-specific. This performance-based construct ensures that the Final Rule 
remains genuinely technology-neutral, empowering an all-of-the-above portfolio of solutions 
rather than presuming that any single class of assets is inherently superior.  Stakeholders argue 
that whether flexibility is achieved through batteries, thermal storage, on-site generation, 
software orchestration of computational power use, or adjacent controllable resources, the value 
to the grid remains consistent.  
Diverse Technologies for Flexibility: Commenters highlight that flexibility stems from various 
technologies, all of which should be eligible under a FLFT. 
● Fluence submits that Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are "uniquely positioned 
to make large loads curtailable and flexible" and this "minimizes the system impact from 
interconnection and can minimize the need for expensive and time-consuming network 
upgrades" (pages 2-3).  
● The Thermal Battery Alliance notes that thermal energy storage can decouple industrial 
heat demand from electric demand, explaining that its "operational profile allows these 
hyper flexible loads to avoid periods of peak scarcity while absorbing energy during low-
value hours" (page 2).  
● Emerald AI notes that "the Commission should adopt a technology-neutral, 
performance-based definition of flexibility that includes software-defined flexibility as a 
valid form of ‘curtailable and dispatchable’ load..." (page 1). Emerald AI adds that this

IV. 
STANDARDIZING STUDIES BASED ON NET WITHDRAWAL AND 
CONTROLLED LIMITS 
Multiple commenters argue that interconnection studies must reflect the actual, controlled 
operating profile of a facility rather than a theoretical maximum withdrawal. 
● Critical Loop proposes the establishment of a "Large-Load Flexibility Commitment 
(LLFC)," which they define as "a contractual, tariff-backed commitment by a 
transmission-connected load to curtail to pre-specified levels" (page 5). They argue 
transmission providers should "model LLFC parameters explicitly in interconnection 
studies" rather than defaulting to a static maximum-withdrawal assumption (page 2).  
● Constellation asserts that "the final large load interconnection rule should reflect the 
withdrawal rights and different types of service that large loads and hybrid facilities wish 
to take, rather than requiring every resource to take full network service…" (page 21).  
● Google supports transmission service based on "actual withdrawal rights" consistent with 
cost causation principles (page 12).  
AEMA agrees and posits that the standardized operator-facility interface referenced in our initial 
comments (and supported by the capabilities cited by National Grid regarding Emerald AI) 
provides the necessary "real-time direct telemetry" requested by grid operators. 
 
V. 
THE COMMISSION MUST REJECT PROPOSALS THAT IGNORE DEMAND 
FLEXIBILITY OR NARROWLY REQUIRE GENERATION PAIRINGS TO 
ACCELERATE LOAD CONNECTION 
AEMA strongly urges the Commission to reject arguments from the Independent Market 
Monitor for PJM ("PJM IMM"), the Electric Power Supply Association ("EPSA"), the Illinois 
Attorney General, and others suggesting that large loads must "bring their own generation" or 
that demand-side solutions are inferior to generation assets. 
A. Matching load with generation should be just one of multiple pathways to 
dispatchability, flexibility, and curtailability. The PJM IMM’s recommendation that "new 
data center load be required to bring their own new generation" (page 23) and EPSA’s call for 
connecting loads to "designated generation supply" (page 9) ignore the fundamental efficiency of 
the transmission grid. Mandating that every new large load be paired with new generation forces 
the construction of redundant capital assets when cost-effective flexibility could otherwise allow 
that load to be served by the existing system. This approach would unnecessarily inflate the cost 
of AI infrastructure and add years of supply chain backlog. It would also delay U.S. 
competitiveness without providing additional reliability benefits that verifiable curtailment can 
already achieve. 
AEMA does not oppose customers electing to pair large loads with new generation where that is 
the most efficient way to meet their needs; rather, AEMA urges the Commission to treat 
generation matching as one optional pathway among many to achieve flexibility, not as a 
mandatory precondition for timely interconnection. In an improved framework, customer-sited 
generation, software-defined flexibility, storage, and other tools would be evaluated on a

technology-neutral, performance-based basis for how effectively they satisfy reliability and 
adequacy criteria. 
B. Data Center Flexibility is Not "Illusory": AEMA specifically rebuts the PJM IMM’s 
characterization of demand-side options as "illusory" (page 22). Modern AI data centers are not 
traditional industrial loads relying on manual intervention. As noted by National Grid in the 
record, today’s flexibility is driven by advanced analytics, automated orchestration, and precise 
telemetry. When a large load commits to a tariff-backed operational limit—monitored in real-
time by the grid operator—it provides a reliability service physically equivalent to generation. 
The capabilities of AI to provide flexible power consumption to relieve grid stress in real time 
has been rigorously peer-reviewed in the literature, and AI data centers can provide credible, 
enforceable, and verifiable curtailment.5 
Accordingly, AEMA urges the Commission to modernize its conception of “demand response” 
for large loads. For roughly the past two decades, demand response has largely meant that 
customers reduce load a limited number of hours per year in response to utility or ISO signals, 
often during peak or emergency conditions. Those signaling tools can now support more 
frequent, granular adjustments in demand. Large, telemetry-rich computing and industrial 
facilities increasingly use software, advanced controls, and real-time data to adjust demand 
continuously, not only during occasional “demand response events.” This type of demand 
flexibility goes beyond traditional event-based demand response to include price-responsive 
operations that serve the same core purpose of reducing effective load on the system when 
needed. Tailoring market access and interconnection procedures to the capability of flexible 
large loads is crucial for enabling innovative business models to operate economically, 
support reliability, and minimize system impacts. 
C. Expedited Treatment for Flexible Loads is Not Discriminatory: AEMA disagrees with the 
California Department of Water Resources ("DWR") and Transmission Access Policy Study 
Group (TAPS) who argue that expedited study treatment for curtailable loads is discriminatory 
(page 1). It is a fundamental principle of interconnection policy that customers who impose 
different impacts on the system should be treated differently. A load that is legally and 
technically bound to curtail during constraints does not require the same network upgrades as a 
static, inflexible load. Granting a "Flexible Load Fast Track" to the former is not discrimination; 
it is a recognition of the superior reliability attributes of the resource. 
D. Exclusion of Flexibility Would Be Unduly Discriminatory and Contrary to Established 
Jurisdiction: Finally, AEMA posits that a final rule singling out loads or hybrid facilities that 
pair matching generation as the sole mechanism for expedited interconnection—while excluding 
verifiable load flexibility—would be unduly discriminatory. From the perspective of grid physics 
and reliability, a verifiable, dispatchable reduction in demand is functionally equivalent to an 
increase in supply. To grant expedited interconnection to a load based on its ability to build a 
generator, while denying the same treatment to a load capable of equivalent performance, would 
treat similarly situated resources differently without justification. Furthermore, any hesitation

VI. 
CONCLUSION 
The record established in this proceeding demonstrates widespread industry agreement that 
flexible load is essential to resolving interconnection backlogs. Stakeholders from across the 
sector—spanning National Grid, Constellation, Google, Microsoft, Industrial Consumer 
Organizations, and others—have identified that expediting interconnection for flexible resources 
reduces the need for immediate physical infrastructure and aligns with efficient grid planning. 
Accordingly, AEMA respectfully requests that the Commission establish a Flexible Load Fast 
Track (FLFT) and adopt a technology-neutral and performance-based definition of flexibility in 
its proposed large load interconnection rules.
