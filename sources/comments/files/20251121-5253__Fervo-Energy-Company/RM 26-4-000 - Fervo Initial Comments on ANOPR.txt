INITIAL COMMENTS OF FERVO ENERGY COMPANY 
 
In accordance with the October 27, 2025 and November 7, 2025 notices of the Federal 
Energy Regulatory Commission (the “Commission” or “FERC”), Fervo Energy Company 
(“Fervo”) submits initial comments on the advance notice of proposed rulemaking (“ANOPR”) 
on large load interconnection released by the Secretary of Energy.1  Fervo generally supports the 
effort to address interconnection of large loads reflected in the ANOPR and provides comments 
responsive to the ANOPR. 
Executive Summary 
Fervo supports the Commission’s efforts to provide greater clarity, standardization, and 
efficiency in the interconnection process for large loads and hybrid facilities.  In particular, the 
proposed enhancements to readiness requirements and the coordinated study of load and 
generation address challenges that Fervo has encountered firsthand.  These reforms have the 
potential to enable Fervo’s emerging hybrid geothermal projects to reliably and cost-effectively 
serve large industrial loads.  With well-designed improvements to the interconnection 
framework, the Commission can help unlock the development of reliable, baseload geothermal 
resources and large-load facilities alike, thereby strengthening U.S. energy security, supporting

I. 
About Fervo. 
Fervo Energy (“Fervo”) is a next-generation geothermal developer deploying Enhanced 
Geothermal Systems (“EGS”) to provide 24/7, dispatchable, carbon-free power by applying 
proven technologies from the oil and gas sector.  Next-generation geothermal resources have the 
potential to supply tens of gigawatts of reliable, baseload capacity to the U.S. electric grid over 
the next decade.  However, growing interconnection congestion has constrained the pace at 
which these resources can be developed and deployed.  Timely, efficient interconnection access 
for geothermal projects is essential to ensuring that geothermal energy can play a central role in 
advancing the nation’s energy-dominance and reliability objectives. 
 
II. 
Comments. 
 
Fervo’s comments are organized around the set of principles, First through Fourteenth, 
identified in the ANOPR (paragraphs 18-31), and more specifically the specific requests for 
comments raised in the ANOPR with respect to those various principles.  Fervo does not provide 
comment in response to every principle, and it reserves the right to provide further comments in 
possible reply comments. 
 
A. 
Second Principle:  We seek comment on alternative thresholds, including whether 
such a threshold is necessary at all. 
 
Fervo Comments:  Fervo agrees that a 20 MW threshold is reasonable, appropriately 
tailored to the types of high-impact projects this rulemaking is intended to address, and fully

consistent with the structure of the existing pro forma LGIP and LGIA. The LGIP framework is 
the Commission’s established mechanism for studying and processing generation 
interconnection requests that can materially affect transmission system reliability. Aligning the 
applicability threshold for large-load and hybrid interconnections with this framework can ensure 
procedural consistency, reduce administrative complexity for transmission providers, and create 
a predictable pathway for customers whose system impacts are similar to those of large 
generators. 
 
B. 
Fourth Principle:  We seek comment on the extent to which the existing study 
deposits, readiness requirements, and withdrawal penalties can be adopted. We also seek 
comment on whether additional commitments or financial penalties would be appropriate. 
 
Fervo Comments:  Fervo supports the Commission’s focus on ensuring that large-load 
and hybrid interconnection customers demonstrate commercial readiness before entering and 
advancing through the interconnection queue.  Fervo encourages the Commission to prioritize 
readiness criteria that mirror the maturity expectations already applied to large generator 
interconnection customers, including demonstrated site control or contracting milestones. 
Applying a readiness framework to load and hybrid customers can help transmission providers 
distinguish commercially viable projects, improve queue efficiency, and reduce speculative 
requests without imposing unnecessary financial barriers. 
 
C. 
Sixth Principle:  We seek comment on whether other operational limitations 
should be considered. We also seek comment on the minimum technical requirements for such 
system protection facilities, whether a hybrid interconnection customer should be subject to 
penalties for unauthorized injections or withdrawals, how any such penalties should be designed, 
and how such penalties should be allocated to other transmission customers.

Fervo Comments:  Fervo agrees that appropriate system-protection requirements may be 
necessary for hybrid facilities but emphasizes that such requirements should be proportionate to 
the actual operational characteristics and risks of the resource. Geothermal has stable and 
predictable operating profiles and does not exhibit fast-changing injection or withdrawal patterns 
that drive many protection concerns for other technologies. As a result, protection systems for 
these hybrids can be tailored rather than broadly prescriptive, ensuring reliability without 
imposing unnecessary or overly burdensome technical mandates. Fervo also encourages the 
Commission to ensure that any enforcement mechanisms or operational limits for unauthorized 
injections or withdrawals reflect the physical realities of hybrid geothermal operations and do not 
inadvertently constrain resources that already pose minimal system-protection risk. 
 
D. 
Seventh Principle:  We seek comment on whether this should be accomplished 
through a serial interconnection study process or by some other means. We also seek comment 
on appropriate deadlines for such an expedited study process, including whether such studies can 
be completed in 60 days. 
 
Fervo Comments:  Fervo supports the Commission’s proposal to expedite interconnection 
studies for large loads and hybrid facilities that agree to be curtailable or dispatchable, as these 
operational flexibilities materially reduce system impacts and allow transmission providers to 
study such projects with greater confidence. Geothermal-based hybrid projects are particularly 
well suited for this approach because they can provide highly predictable, operator-controlled 
responses that improve system reliability during peak or contingency events. An expedited study 
process that recognizes the reliability value of curtailable or dispatchable hybrids would help 
accelerate deployment of clean firm resources while allowing transmission providers to prioritize 
projects that offer the greatest operational benefit. Fervo encourages the Commission to ensure

that the expedited pathway is structured to account for the unique controllability characteristics 
of hybrid geothermal facilities and supports clear timelines that maintain study discipline without

E. 
Eighth Principle:  We seek comment on whether such costs should be offset 
through a crediting mechanism and, if so, over how many years. 
 
Fervo Comments:  Fervo supports clear cost-allocation rules but emphasizes that any 
cost-allocation framework must recognize when a project’s design meaningfully reduces its 
system impact. Hybrid geothermal facilities that “bring their own power” can substantially 
decrease the need for upstream transmission reinforcements by co-locating firm, dispatchable 
generation with load, often avoiding or minimizing upgrades that a stand-alone load would 
otherwise require. Fervo therefore encourages the Commission to ensure that cost responsibility 
is applied in a way that reflects actual system benefits and not over-assign costs to hybrids that 
reduce net flows or improve local reliability. Where appropriate, crediting mechanisms should 
remain available to acknowledge these benefits and maintain fair treatment across different 
project types. 
 
F. 
Thirteenth Principle:  We seek comment on appropriate transition plans, including 
the treatment of large load interconnections that are already being studied for interconnection. 
 
Fervo Comments:  Fervo supports the Commission’s effort to establish a clear and 
orderly transition plan and encourages the Commission to design that transition to recognize the 
system benefits of projects that co-locate large loads with clean generation.  Fervo therefore 
recommends that generation projects already in the generation interconnection queue, or in late-
stage development, be permitted to elect into the new coordinated hybrid study framework where
