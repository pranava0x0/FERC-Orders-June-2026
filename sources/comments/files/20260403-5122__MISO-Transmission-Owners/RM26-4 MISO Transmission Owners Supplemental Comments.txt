A. 
Transmission 
In pursuing additional reforms to accelerate speed to power, it is important to 
evaluate tools that would speed up transmission development for large load integration, as 
large load timelines are often driven by economic development, national security, and 
technological imperatives.  Creating streamlined processes and ensuring the ability of 
transmission owners to act expeditiously are just a few examples of the tools that are critical 
to achieving the ANOPR’s objectives. 
1. 
Proactive Long Range Transmission Planning  
It is no secret that the sooner long range, backbone transmission is built the faster 
generation and load can come online.  Transmission serves as the critical conduit 
connecting load with generation.  Regional transmission, in particular, is key to supporting 
interstate energy transfers, providing economic value through least-cost energy 
procurement, bolstering regional reliability, and supporting the integration of generation 
and load.  Since its inception, MISO and its members have recognized and worked to 
realize these benefits.21  Since 2003, MISO’s MTEP has “guided the approval of more than 
6,800 total projects and upgraded or added nearly 79,000 miles of transmission 
infrastructure, with over 10,000 miles of transmission line additions or improvements 
planned.”22  In fact, the Commission has specifically recognized MISO’s regional

B.

1. 
ERAS 
The Expedited Resource Addition Study (“ERAS”) process, which was approved 
by the Commission in July 2025, is designed to fast-track needed new generation to meet 
growing load.  On March 9, 2026, MISO announced the third cycle of projects to be 
evaluated under the ERAS process.31  This cycle includes fifteen projects totaling 
approximately 8 GW of proposed new capacity and features a mix of natural gas resources 
and battery storage spread across MISO’s North, Central, and South regions.  MISO states 
that all projects are expected to be in service by 2031 or earlier.  Since the program 
launched, “53 projects representing nearly 27 GW of proposed capacity have been accepted 
or are pending validation into ERAS” and “[t]en have already advanced to completed 
generator interconnection agreements [(“GIAs”)], with an additional 15 projects nearing 
completion.”32

1. 
Defining “Large Load” and Related Interconnection Reliability 
Requirements. 
Because large loads can have a disproportionate impact on system reliability due to 
their size and unique characteristics, MISO is pursuing the development of reliability 
requirements related to the integration of large loads, including defining a “large load.”  
Consistent with our Initial Comments regarding the Second Principle of the ANOPR, the 
MISO Transmission Owners generally support a definition that avoids a threshold based 
solely on the MW size of the load and instead provides reasonable flexibility in defining 
large loads, given the technical experience and judgment that is required to appropriately 
and reasonably weigh operational, reliability, and administrative factors that are critical to 
defining large loads.  Currently, MISO expects to file Tariff revisions including such new 
requirements in June 2026.35 
2. 
ZGIA 
MISO is discussing with stakeholders its proposal for a ZGIA for generation co-
located with load.  The Third Principle of the ANOPR recognized that siting a large load 
near or at the same point of interconnection as a new generating facility could reduce the 
network upgrades needed to interconnect only the load or only the generating facility.  
MISO’s proposed ZGIA is specifically designed with that goal in mind.  The ZGIA would 
allow a generator to serve load directly but grants no interconnection service.  The proposed 
ZGIA process leverages existing interconnection frameworks in terms of study aspects,
