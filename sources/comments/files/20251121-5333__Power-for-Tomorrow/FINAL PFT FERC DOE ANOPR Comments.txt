I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY 
Power for Tomorrow (“PFT”) submits these comments in response to the Federal Energy 
Regulatory Commission’s (“FERC” or “Commission”) Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking 
(“ANOPR”) established in Docket No. RM26-4-000 following the Department of Energy’s (“DOE”) 
October 23, 2025, directive under Section 403 of the DOE Organization Act.1  
The Commission and DOE correctly identify large load interconnection as a critical national issue 
affecting grid reliability, resource adequacy, and cost allocation. The explosive growth in data 
center demand, coupled with the emergence of novel co-location arrangements that bypass 
traditional interconnection processes, presents legitimate challenges to grid planning and 
operations, and is one of the reasons for growing electricity costs for customers.  
PFT’s mission is to ensure consumers have access to affordable, reliable, and adequate energy 
through sensible utility regulation. The issue of large load interconnection goes directly to these 
core principles. As background, PFT was launched in response to Winter Storm Uri in 2021, 
which left millions of people in Texas without power for days causing hundreds of deaths and 
billions of dollars in economic losses. This man-made catastrophe—which occurred in the most 
“competitive” energy market in the country— was a clear example of a market failure and 
provides a powerful contrast to the well-regulated public utility model that has been delivering 
reliable, affordable power to customers for more than 100 years.

Now, a familiar tension between regulatory models is at the heart of the current large load 
interconnection debate. In an attempt to remedy the large load interconnection problem 
through federal means, the Secretary of Energy has leveraged authority conferred by DOE’s 
Organizational Act Section 403 2 to direct FERC to respond to a proposed rulemaking on large 
load interconnection at the transmission level. Facilitating expeditious interconnection of these 
new customers is an important goal, but the proposal raises key questions about FERC’s 
jurisdiction and, ultimately, could exacerbate the challenges of new large loads: speedy 
interconnection without expanding supply will strain reliability further and increase costs for all. 
FERC should be wary of proposals that could make conditions worse rather than better. 
While PFT does not dispute the very real challenges of large load interconnection in some parts 
of the country, these challenges are not uniform. In these comments, PFT offers another point 
of focus: the effectiveness of vertically integrated utilities regulated by state utility commissions 
in addressing the challenges posed by the demands of new large loads. These utilities and their 
state regulators have demonstrated effective and timely management of large load additions 
through established regulatory tools including certificate of need proceedings, integrated 
resource planning, and sophisticated rate design and cost allocation. FERC should not take any 
steps that could undermine these successes. 
As the Commission continues with this rulemaking proceeding, PFT respectfully urges the 
Commission to: (1) recognize the vital state regulatory role in effectively managing and serving 
large loads, overseeing intrastate transmission, and determining cost allocation; and (2) 
cautiously examine its jurisdictional authority so as to remain within the bounds of the law and 
preserve states’ jurisdiction to continue to develop and deploy successful approaches to the 
large load challenge. Should the Commission move forward with a rulemaking consistent with 
DOE’s proposal, it should ensure that any steps taken to facilitate large load interconnections 
are paired with efforts to protect reliability and minimize costs for customers. 
II. LARGE LOAD INTERCONNECTION: A CRITICAL BUT REGIONALLY 
VARIED CHALLENGE 
A.​  The Significance of Large Load Growth

PFT acknowledges the unprecedented scale and pace of large load interconnection requests 
facing the electric industry. Hyperscale data centers driven by artificial intelligence computing, 
cryptocurrency operations, and advanced manufacturing facilities are requesting 
interconnection at loads measured in hundreds of megawatts or even gigawatts—orders of 
magnitude larger than historical industrial load additions.3 
 
This growth trajectory creates legitimate concerns across multiple dimensions. Grid reliability 
faces new stresses from geographically concentrated load additions that can exceed available 
transmission capacity and generation resources, particularly given ongoing thermal generation 
retirements. The pace and magnitude of load growth strains planning processes that exist to 
ensure adequate generation capacity.  
 
The emergence of co-location arrangements—large loads connecting directly to generation 
facilities—adds complexity to these challenges. Such arrangements potentially avoid 
transmission upgrade costs and network service charges while creating reliability concerns: 
largest customers that can satisfy their demand close to the point of generation, effectively 
reducing the pool of generation available to all other customers participating in markets.4 At 
DOE’s behest, the Commission rightly seeks to understand whether its jurisdictional authority, 
as well as its oversight of existing market tariff frameworks, adequately address these novel 
arrangements within the broader large load conversation. 
 
B.​ Understand the Root of Regional Variation in Interconnection Processes 
 
While large load interconnection presents challenges nationwide, the nature and severity of 
these challenges vary dramatically based on regional market structure and the effectiveness of 
state regulatory oversight. Recently, the policy debate has generalized problems as systemic 
failures across all regulatory models while failing to shine a light on which regulatory models are 
succeeding at interconnecting new large loads while also protecting existing customers. 
 
In some organized wholesale markets or areas within wholesale markets—Regional 
Transmission Organizations (“RTO”) and Independent System Operators (“ISO”)—governed by 
FERC-approved tariffs, large load interconnection has indeed experienced process problems. In

other RTOs/ISOs, such as MISO South, those problems are not occurring due in large part to the 
processes established by retail regulations in those states. The challenges facing some organized 
wholesale markets must be understood in the context of FERC’s Open Access Transmission Tariff 
(“OATT”) framework, which applies to all public utilities providing transmission service in 
interstate commerce. FERC Orders 888 and 889 established open access principles,5 while 
Orders 2003 and 2023 created standardized generator interconnection procedures6—but these 
frameworks were designed for generation resources, not loads. 
Such a generator-focused framework has collided with unprecedented demand growth, creating 
severe interconnection queue backlogs exceeding 200 GW and resulting in multi-year 
processing delays in some regions. ISOs and RTOs have implemented cluster-based reforms and 
initiated “first-ready, first-served” approaches to address generation backlogs, but in some 
regions large load interconnection has received comparatively little attention. As a model for 
large load interconnection, however, this process leaves much to be desired. 
Some market operators are attempting to address large load interconnection at the 
transmission level in a more systematic way—notably Southwest Power Pool’s development of 
High Impact Large Load (“HILL”) Integration solutions, including Conditional High Impact Large 
Load (“CHILL”) service and the High Impact Large Load Generation Assessment (“HILLGA”) 
framework to study generation and load interconnections together.7 However, these efforts 
remain nascent and have not yet been broadly adopted across organized markets and do not 
address concerns about how to provide the supply necessary to serve these customers. 
The emergence of co-location arrangements further exposes gaps in the approaches taken by 
some ISOs and RTOs, as tariff provisions designed around generator interconnection fail to 
adequately address loads that can effectively island from the transmission system while 
potentially relying on it for backup service. While the OATT framework applies broadly, these 
structural challenges—tariff provisions structured for generation rather than load—have proven 
particularly acute in some organized markets where ISO/RTO processes struggle to 
accommodate the kind of load becoming common from data center development.

However, in regions served by vertically integrated utilities operating under traditional state 
regulatory oversight, the picture looks markedly different. State public utility commissions have 
decades of experience regulating utility service obligations, cost allocation, and resource 
planning. These commissions employ well-established regulatory tools that address large load 
interconnection more holistically—looking at interconnection and the supply needed to serve 
new demand—within existing frameworks: certificate of need proceedings evaluate whether 
serving new large loads is in the public interest; integrated resource planning processes 
incorporate large load forecasts and ensure adequate generation and transmission resources; 
rate design proceedings allocate costs fairly between new large loads and existing customers; 
and special contracts for exceptionally large customers receive commission review and 
approval. 
 
C.​ Jurisdictional Considerations for Reshaping Large Load Interconnection 
 
While the interconnection of large loads is important, FERC’s ability to address this issue 
holistically is limited by its jurisdiction over wholesale sales and interstate transmission, which is 
grounded in the framework of the Federal Power Act and the Constitution.8 The Commission 
cannot unilaterally expand this jurisdiction, even to solve an important challenge like facilitating 
the interconnection of new large loads. FERC should cautiously examine DOE’s request to assert 
jurisdiction and, should it pursue a final rule in this proceeding, take care not to step into states’ 
jurisdiction with respect to franchised service territories, terms and conditions of retail service, 
utility cost recovery and rate setting, and generator siting and permitting. Most importantly, the 
Commission should not take action that would derail the successful efforts of state regulators 
and vertically integrated utilities to address large load interconnections in ways that support the 
overall reliability and affordability of the electric grid for all customers, not just new large loads.   
 
Certainly, FERC has some jurisdiction over the interconnection of large loads at the transmission 
level. Many transmission owners and operators already have interconnection procedures 
included in their FERC-approved tariffs and a request to ISOs/RTOS and others with approved 
Open Access Transmission Tariffs to revise these tariffs to provide a more standardized approach 
to interconnection requests would be within the Commission’s jurisdiction.  
 
However, even though DOE’s proposal purports to limit its proposed interconnection policies to 
loads over 20 megawatts (“MW”) that interconnect at facilities that satisfy the seven-factor

test,9 the agency’s approach has the potential to significantly expand FERC’s jurisdiction to the 
extent that it reimagines interconnection as providing access not only to the transmission 
system but to bundled service that includes energy. As DOE notes, any large load that requests 
to interconnect to the transmission system is seeking not only transmission services but also 
“the appurtenant benefits of such,” which DOE explicitly defines as including capacity and 
energy.10 DOE also equates access to the transmission system for generators—without which 
they cannot provide energy to the markets or wholesale customers—with access to the 
transmission system for new load, claiming both have equal implications for the justness and 
reasonableness of wholesale rates.11 
 
DOE recognizes that states have authority over retail rates and generation siting or expansion, 
but its claims that loads are equivalent to generators when it comes to transmission 
interconnection (and that transmission service necessarily encompasses energy and capacity 
services) clearly implicates states’ roles and jurisdiction. It is critical to recognize that generators 
and loads are fundamentally different and seek to interconnect for vastly different reasons. 
Generators seek access to wholesale markets, within FERC’s jurisdiction, but large loads seek to 
interconnect to obtain bundled retail services, within states’ jurisdiction. DOE’s assertion that 
transmission service encompasses “the appurtenant benefits of such” threatens state 
jurisdiction to the extent that new large loads could, for example, argue that failure to provide 
them energy and capacity is a violation of the terms of their interconnection or entitles them to 
direct access to wholesale markets.  
 
As discussed in more detail below, states are moving to solve the holistic challenges of large 
load interconnection, which go beyond mere interconnection to include the actual provision of 
energy and capacity. Any steps that FERC may take in the future should avoid encroaching on 
states’ efforts, and the Commission should be careful not to assert jurisdiction in such a way 
that expands the conception of interconnection to require the provision of retail services (or de 
facto restructuring) that are explicitly within the purview of states.

A.​ Successful State Regulatory Models 
 
State commissions overseeing vertically integrated utilities have demonstrated effective 
management of large load interconnection requests through established regulatory processes. 
These successes are often overlooked as “business-as-usual” operations, despite these players 
facing the same unprecedented growth in data center demand.   
 
In Georgia, for example, the Public Service Commission (“Georgia PSC”) has managed numerous 
large industrial and data center interconnections through comprehensive regulatory oversight. 
In January 2025, the Georgia PSC unanimously approved new rules requiring data centers using 
more than 100 MW to be billed based on project risks and to pay transmission and distribution 
costs as construction progresses.12 Georgia PSC Chairman Jason Shaw stated: “By approving this 
new rule, the PSC is helping ensure that existing Georgia Power customers will be spared 
additional costs associated with adding these large-load customers to the grid.”13 Now, all 
contracts exceeding 100 MW must be submitted to the PSC for review, ensuring comprehensive 
state oversight. 
 
Georgia Power is not the only example of success. Throughout the Southeast, vertically 
integrated utilities have successfully interconnected large loads without the queue backlogs and 
cost-shifting controversies that exist in some markets. This success stems from regulatory 
frameworks that give state commissions clear authority to evaluate proposed service in the 
public interest, utilities that maintain generation and transmission planning capabilities, and 
integrated resource planning processes that can accommodate large load forecasts. 
 
B.​ State Regulatory Tools Function as Designed 
 
State public utility commissions possess sophisticated regulatory tools specifically designed to 
manage the challenges presented by large load interconnection. These tools have evolved over 
decades to balance utility service obligations, ratepayer protection, and economic development 
objectives.

Certificate of need proceedings provide comprehensive review of proposed large load 
interconnections. Commissions evaluate whether the utility can reliably serve the proposed 
load, how costs will be allocated between new and existing customers, what transmission or 
generation additions are required, and whether serving the new load is consistent with the 
public interest. 
 
Integrated resource planning processes incorporate large load forecasts and ensure adequate 
resources are planned and procured. Unlike energy and capacity-only markets that rely on price 
signals for resource adequacy, utility planning processes under state commission oversight 
provide certainty that generation and transmission will be available to serve forecasted load 
including major new large customers. 
 
On the customer protection end, rate design and cost allocation allow utilities to structure 
service to large loads in ways that protect existing customers while facilitating interconnection. 
Special contract provisions can require large customers to fund necessary upgrades, accept 
interruptible or curtailable service, or pay rates that reflect their system impact. 
 
C.​ State Processes Achieve Federal Objectives Without Federal Intervention 
 
The outcomes achieved through state regulatory oversight of large load interconnection in 
vertically integrated markets demonstrate that federal intervention is not necessary in places 
where state processes function effectively. Timely interconnection occurs without the 
multi-year delays afflicting some organized market queues. Existing ratepayers receive 
protection through commission review of cost allocation and rate design. Grid reliability is 
maintained through comprehensive resource planning and system impact analysis. 
 
Importantly, these successful outcomes occur even where vertically integrated utilities 
participate in organized wholesale markets for energy and ancillary services. Utilities like 
Southern Company and Entergy operate in or adjacent to organized markets yet demonstrate 
effective large load interconnection under state regulatory oversight. This suggests that the 
critical variable is regulatory structure and state commission effectiveness rather than market 
participation per se. 
 
And, at a minimum, FERC should take steps to require that transmission-interconnected large 
loads participate in efforts to ensure the reliability of the grid and support good transmission 
and generation planning by providing accurate and timely information about their plans and

IV. FEDERAL INTERVENTION RISKS DISRUPTING SUCCESSFUL STATE 
MODELS 
A.​ Preemption Would Undermine Effective State Regulation 
 
While it may be reasonable to attempt to standardize transmission-level interconnection 
procedures, FERC should avoid taking action or asserting jurisdiction in such a way that would 
serve as an effective preemption of large load interconnection through DOE Section 403 
authority or sweeping changes to FERC-jurisdictional interconnection procedures could disrupt 
effective state regulation without corresponding benefits in jurisdictions where state processes 
work well. 
 
State public utility commissions possess deep institutional knowledge about the utilities they 
regulate. This expertise includes understanding of local transmission constraints, generation 
portfolios, load characteristics, and economic development priorities that federal agencies 
cannot replicate. 
 
The state regulatory compact balances multiple policy objectives beyond the narrow question 
of interconnection procedures. State commissions consider state and local policy goals, 
economic development, consumer protection, and service reliability when evaluating proposed 
large load service. Federal intervention focused solely on expediting interconnection could 
obscure these broader considerations. 
 
B.​ Unintended Consequences of Federal Intervention 
 
Federal intervention carries risks worth considering. Projects currently moving forward 
smoothly under state processes could be delayed pending development of federal rules—such 
an outcome would run counter to DOE’s stated objectives. Similarly, utilities and large 
customers with negotiated agreements approved by state commissions might find those 
agreements challenged by federal requirements issued after the agreements were executed.

PFT asks FERC to be mindful that federal procedures designed for organized wholesale markets 
may not translate to vertically integrated utilities. ISO/RTO interconnection processes assume 
independence between generation and load, distinct transmission providers, and market-based 
resource adequacy mechanisms. As a result, imposing ISO/RTO-style procedures on vertically 
integrated utilities could create bureaucratic complexity without corresponding benefits. 
 
V. RECOMMENDED APPROACH: TARGETED FEDERAL INTERVENTION 
A.​ Limit Federal Jurisdiction to Regions with Demonstrated Regulatory Failures 
 
DOE and FERC should consider targeted intervention in areas where existing regulatory 
frameworks have struggled to manage large load interconnection effectively. This approach 
would respect successful state models while addressing acute problems in some organized 
wholesale markets. 
 
Federal oversight may best apply within some areas of ISO/RTO footprints, specifically those 
where tariff-based interconnection processes have proven insufficient and where state 
authority over large load service is limited or unclear. In these regions, FERC possesses clear 
jurisdiction over wholesale market rules and can require modifications to tariff provisions 
governing large load interconnection. As discussed, this is within FERC’s jurisdiction as long as 
the Commission does not take action beyond creating processes for interconnection that 
implicate the provision of retail electric service.  
 
For regions served by vertically integrated utilities under effective state regulatory oversight, 
federal intervention could be avoided. DOE and FERC could consider, for example, establishing 
safe harbor provisions whereby states demonstrating effective management of large load 
interconnection remain free from federal procedural mandates or that encourage states to 
continue to holistically solve the interrelated challenges of interconnection of large loads and 
critical new generation. 
 
B.​  Federal-State Partnership  
 
Given the effectiveness of vertically integrated utilities and their regulators, DOE and FERC could 
consider working with states rather than around them. A partnership model would leverage the 
institutional knowledge and regulatory capacity of state commissions while providing federal

The large load interconnection challenges in organized wholesale markets stem from deeper 
problems in market design that interconnection jurisdiction alone cannot solve. The 
Commission's November 1, 2024, rejection of the amended Susquehanna interconnection 
service agreement illustrated the complexity of co-location arrangements and cost allocation in 
organized markets.14 Commissioners Mark Christie and Lindsay See noted that co-location 
arrangements “present an array of complicated, nuanced and multifaceted issues, which 
collectively could have huge ramifications for both grid reliability and consumer costs.”15 
Concerns included potential cost shifts exceeding $140 million annually to other transmission 
customers.16 
 
Sustainable solutions require comprehensive market design reforms, not just large load 
interconnection remedies. PFT urges FERC to support the existing functions of state utility 
regulators while focusing on reforming capacity markets, strengthening transmission planning 
requirements, and taking a hard look at whether co-located loads are avoiding their share of 
grid costs and upgrades.  
 
 
 
 
VI. CONCLUSION 
 
Large load interconnection presents significant challenges that demand thoughtful policy 
responses. PFT respectfully requests that the Commission exercise care and precision in 
addressing large load interconnection challenges. Federal intervention should be narrowly 
tailored to locations where regulatory frameworks are struggling, rather than imposing uniform 
requirements that disrupt successful state models or implicate state jurisdiction. Further, PFT
