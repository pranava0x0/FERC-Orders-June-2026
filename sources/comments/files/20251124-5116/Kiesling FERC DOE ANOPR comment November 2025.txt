Executive Summary 
This comment evaluates the Department of Energy’s Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking 
(ANOPR) regarding large-load interconnection through the lens of transaction-cost economics, 
regulatory economics, and technological change. The rapid growth of AI data center load 
presents a critical coordination challenge due to the temporal mismatch between rapid data 
center deployment (18–24 months) and slow grid infrastructure expansion (7–10 years). 
To address this pacing mismatch and ensure nondiscriminatory access, the Commission 
should adopt a modular, principles-based framework grounded in three economic propositions: 
●​ Flexibility as a System Value: Modern data centers possess significant latent flexibility 
through workload shifting, UPS-based frequency response, and hybrid on-site 
generation. Current interconnection rules treat these loads as static and inflexible, 
ignoring capabilities that could significantly reduce system stress and upgrade costs. 
●​ Reducing Transaction Costs: Legacy interconnection rules create high asset specificity 
and hold-up risks, driving developers toward inefficient bypass strategies. Co-location 
and hybrid configurations are rational economic responses to these high transaction 
costs ("make vs. buy" decisions) and should be facilitated rather than penalized by 
extending generator-style interconnection regimes to loads. 
●​ Foundational Cost Allocation Reform: The ANOPR’s suggestion of 100% participant 
funding for upgrades relies on flawed embedded-cost methodologies that ignore 
marginal impacts. Efficient cost recovery should instead follow Ramsey-Boiteux 
principles, allocating fixed costs inversely to elasticity. Because large loads are highly 
elastic and can relocate or self-supply, assigning them full upgrade costs encourages 
inefficient grid defection.

Recommendations: The Commission should establish a federal framework that lowers barriers 
to flexible resources, modernizes market participation rules for technologies like thermal energy 
storage (TES), and reforms cost allocation to reflect marginal impacts. This approach will 
encourage the integration of grid-supportive flexibility, reducing the need for costly 
infrastructure while ensuring reliability and competitive neutrality. 
 
I. Introduction and Interest of the Commenter 
The accelerating growth of data center and artificial intelligence (AI) electricity demand is 
reshaping the U.S. power system. Forecasts suggest that U.S. data center load, roughly 25 
gigawatts (GW) in 2024, may exceed 80 GW by 2030. Annual electricity consumption 
attributable to these facilities could rise by approximately 400 terawatt-hours (TWh) over the 
decade.1 Globally, the International Energy Agency projects data center electricity use to more 
than double, increasing from an estimated 415 TWh in 2024 to roughly 945 TWh by 2030.2 
These estimates remain uncertain because of rapid evolution in AI chip efficiency, model 
architectures, inference patterns, cooling requirements, and data center design. Several recent 
analyses describe these rapid forecast revisions as “forecast whiplash,” reflecting the difficulty 
of relying on planning institutions built for a world of slow, predictable demand growth.3

This comment evaluates the Department of Energy’s ANOPR on large-load interconnection 
through three related economic propositions. First, flexibility has become the highest-value 
attribute in modern power systems, largely because it a novel, technology-enabled 
manifestation of the price elasticity that power systems have lacked historically. Second, 
legacy interconnection rules create substantial market barriers for new 
technologies—particularly digital demand controls, hybrid on-site generation, flexible and hyper 
flexible loads, and microgrid-ready designs—that could otherwise mitigate system stress. 
Third, cost allocation reform is foundational: embedded-cost and fully distributed cost (FDC) 
methodologies conflict with marginal-cost principles, Ramsey-Boiteux efficiency conditions, 
and Bonbright’s criteria for sound regulation. Together, these three propositions support a 
federal approach that lowers system-wide transaction costs and encourages flexible 
technologies and business models through modular and principles-based methods. 
II. Clarifying the Problem the ANOPR Is Trying to Solve 
The ANOPR responds to a sharp shift in electricity demand dynamics driven by the rapid 
expansion of AI-centric data centers. After more than a decade of flat or marginal load 
growth—from 2008 to 2021, U.S. electricity demand rose by only 0.1 percent 
annually—forecasts have shifted dramatically upward.4 This reversal reflects the rise of a new

class of large, concentrated, and time-sensitive loads whose operational profiles differ

1. Large-load demand and the intertemporal coordination challenge 
Data centers can be built in eighteen to twenty-four months, enabled by modular construction 
and rapid IT deployment cycles.5 In contrast, major power system infrastructure upgrades often 
require seven to ten years to site, permit, and build. This temporal mismatch forces utilities and 
developers to make high-stakes decisions under deep uncertainty about future load 
trajectories. 
The core policy challenge is therefore not simply the magnitude of new demand. It is a 
coordination problem under uncertainty in which actors with heterogeneous expectations and 
incomplete information must align investment, infrastructure, and operational decisions across 
very different time scales. 
2. Cross-border impacts and interstate relevance 
Because many hyperscale facilities seek to interconnect directly to the high-voltage 
transmission system, their effects propagate across regional networks. A single campus 
requesting 500 to 1,000 MW, comparable to the load of a mid-sized city, can influence 
congestion patterns, thermal limits, and power flows across an entire regional transmission 
organization (RTO) footprint. 
Multiple authorities have highlighted these interstate implications. NERC has identified new 
data centers as a primary driver of rising peak-load forecasts and as an emerging reliability 
concern.6 PJM and other RTOs have documented significant upward revisions to multi-year 
load forecasts driven largely by data center development, alongside billions of dollars in 
transmission expansion required to serve these loads. 
Because the impacts spill across state boundaries, allocating upgrade costs has already 
become contentious. This interstate character creates a legitimate federal interest in ensuring 
that processes governing transmission access for large loads are nondiscriminatory and 
account properly for cross-border consequences. 
3. Current institutional fragmentation 
Large-load interconnection and cost recovery are now shaped by a patchwork of state policies 
and utility practices. States such as Ohio, Michigan, and Georgia have adopted large-load 
tariffs and new cost-recovery mechanisms designed to shield retail ratepayers from the risks of 
large, uncertain industrial loads. Several counties in Georgia and Virginia have enacted 
restrictive zoning rules or moratoria on data center development, complicating siting decisions.

Utilities employ bespoke approaches to evaluating load additions, negotiating service

processes, required studies, and cost responsibility vary widely across jurisdictions. The result 
is high search and negotiation costs, significant uncertainty about project viability, and wide

4. The policy question before FERC 
The ANOPR explores whether FERC should establish a standardized national framework for 
interconnecting large loads to the bulk transmission system. One central question is whether 
such a framework would reduce barriers to efficient load integration, strengthen reliability, and 
promote nondiscriminatory access, or whether extending the existing generator-style 
interconnection regime to large loads would add complexity, reinforce outdated assumptions, 
and discourage the flexible, grid-supportive behaviors modern power systems require. 
This comment evaluates that question through the lenses of transaction-cost economics, 
regulatory economics, and the operational realities facing utilities, grid operators, and 
large-load developers. 
III. Proposition 1: Flexibility as the Central System Value 
The ANOPR proposes expedited interconnection queue access for flexible large loads 
(Principle 7). Flexibility has become the binding constraint and the highest-value capability in 
modern power systems. Historically, both supply and demand have been price-inelastic. 
Supply is inelastic because new generation, transmission, and distribution infrastructure require 
long lead times, substantial capital, and multiple regulatory approvals. Demand for electricity is 
inelastic because electricity is an input with very few substitutes in nearly all economic activity, 
and because for data centers, the opportunity cost of curtailment is high due to the expected 
value of the computation. Demand for transmission is similarly inelastic due to the strict 
complementarity of the electricity transaction and the transmission transaction. AI inference 
and training workloads create large economic value per kilowatt-hour and cannot easily be 
deferred without eroding that value. When both sides of the market respond weakly to price, 
the outcome of demand growth is price increases. 
In this environment, we should think of the opportunity for flexibility as the system’s “shadow 
price.” In constrained optimization, the shadow price measures the marginal value of relaxing a 
binding constraint. Here, the binding constraint is the inability of either supply or demand to 
adjust rapidly to other changes. Because supply cannot expand quickly and because demand 
cannot curtail without foregoing valuable activity, any resource or behavior that provides 
operational flexibility relaxes this constraint and thus has high marginal value. Flexibility 
restores the ability to adjust and to improve coordination.

1. Large load flexibility 
Modern data centers possess far greater latent flexibility than legacy regulatory categories 
typically recognize, due in large part to recent and ongoing innovation. Advances in digital 
control systems, workload orchestration, and power‐electronics architectures now permit these 
facilities to modulate demand across multiple time scales. Norris et al. (2025) quantifies this 
potential: it finds that, with modest curtailment of just 0.25 %–0.5 % of annual uptime, U.S. 
balancing authorities could integrate nearly 76 GW–98 GW of new large loads using existing 
capacity without disruptive grid upgrades.7 
Four categories illustrate these emerging capabilities: 
●​ Operational flexibility through workload shifting. Modern AI inference and training 
shapes are increasingly schedulable: inference tasks can shift across clusters and 
geographies, and training jobs can be staged to avoid peak‐stress intervals. As 
distributed compute architectures and global traffic‐balancing systems mature, the 
economic feasibility of temporal and spatial work shifting improves markedly, a finding 
echoed in the Norris et al.’s concept of “spatial flexibility” and “temporal flexibility”. 
●​ UPS- and storage-based fast response. Uninterruptible power supply (UPS) systems 
originally designed for IT equipment protection can now function as fast 
frequency-response assets by modulating inverter-battery power flows on sub-second 
time scales. Norris et al. frames such on-site storage and generation as elements of 
“on-site power and storage” flexibility—capabilities that lower the incremental burden a 
new load places on the system. 
●​ Hyper flexible loads. Flexible, and particularly hyper-flexible loads such as new 
technologies like thermal energy storage (TES), represent a significant opportunity for 
enhancing market performance.  Long duration, highly flexible storage technologies with 
high charge to discharge ratios can provide continuous energy output over multi-day 
periods while only charging during periods of low energy demand and prices, when 
there is typically minimal stress on the grid. This operational profile allows these hyper 
flexible loads to avoid periods of peak scarcity while absorbing energy during low-value 
hours. In doing so, these hyper flexible and price sensitive loads improve price 
formation, mitigate congestion, reduce wasted energy from existing generators, and 
improve utilization of existing infrastructure. Hyper flexible loads like TES are being 
installed now with the operational capability and business model incentive to make 
robust, enforceable and dependable flexibility commitments.  These loads have load 
factors of approximately 33% and can ramp up and down within milliseconds based on

●​ Hybrid configurations with on-site generation. Many developers are exploring on-site

Together, these capabilities transform large loads from passive grid takers into potential active 
system participants—able to provide ancillary services, reduce net peak demand, and mitigate 
the stability risks that reliability authorities increasingly identify. Notably, the Duke study defines 
“curtailment‐enabled headroom” as a measure of how much additional load the system can 
absorb if flexible loads are available, and its results provide empirical weight for the argument 
that this capability is available now. 
2. Current rules treat large loads as inflexible “problems” 
Despite these emerging capabilities, the regulatory treatment of demand continues to assume 
that large loads are static, inflexible, and uncontrollable. This assumption permeates the 
interconnection and cost-recovery framework. Interconnection studies still model demand as a 
fixed block, ignoring the very types of flexibility that Norris et al. (2025) shows can reduce a 
load’s marginal impact on the system to a meaningful degree. Tariff structures often reinforce 
this static view, applying demand charges and cost-recovery riders that make no distinction 
between a load capable of sub-second frequency response and one that simply draws at its 
maximum nameplate level. The regulatory response to rising load forecasts has followed the 
same logic: delaying retirements of legacy fossil generators or extending the operating life of 
aging units, even where flexible loads could provide stability services that reduce these 
perceived needs. 
This persistent mismatch between growing technological capability and backward-looking 
institutional assumptions weakens the alignment between private incentives and system needs. 
When flexibility is neither recognized nor rewarded, firms naturally invest in self-contained 
microgrids, behind-the-meter generation, and resilience-enhancing infrastructure that provide 
private reliability but contribute little to system-wide efficiency or stability. The result is a pattern 
of rational bypass driven not by a lack of technical solutions, but by regulatory structures that 
continue to treat demand as an inert quantity rather than a grid-supportive resource.

Flexibility also reduces the marginal cost of integrating large loads. A flexible load that can shift 
or reduce demand during peak conditions lowers the system’s incremental capacity needs. 
When it co-locates with dispatchable on-site generation, it moderates congestion and reduces 
required network upgrades. Flexibility, therefore, improves reliability and helps bridge the 
pacing mismatch between data center speed-to-power and slower infrastructure expansion. 
3. Implications for the ANOPR 
The ANOPR rightly focuses on nondiscriminatory access and timely interconnection, but this 
framing risks overlooking the economic and reliability benefits of flexible demand. Its expedited 
queue access proposal is one step in the direction of valuing flexibility as a capability. In 
addition, a well-designed federal framework should: 
●​ Incorporate load-provided system services into interconnection options and 
cost-allocation structures. 
●​ Enable partial rights, curtailable service, and hybrid configurations that recognize 
the full range of flexible behaviors. 
●​ Acknowledge that flexibility affects both the magnitude and marginal cost of 
network upgrades. 
If implemented well, a federal framework could harness latent flexibility to improve system 
stability, reduce upgrade costs, and moderate the pacing mismatch between load development 
and infrastructure expansion. 
If the regulatory regime treats all loads as inflexible and relies on embedded-cost allocation 
methods disconnected from marginal impacts, developers will rationally shift toward 
behind-the-meter solutions. That pattern is already visible in response to unreliable 
interconnection timelines and unpredictable upgrade costs. When grid rules fail to reward 
system-beneficial flexibility, investment flows toward private resilience that does little to 
strengthen the wider grid. 
In a system marked by rapid load growth, variable renewable generation, and increasing 
dependence on power electronics, flexibility is economically and operationally indispensable. 
The regulatory framework must reflect that reality rather than rely on legacy assumptions that 
treat large loads as rigid and must-serve. The ANOPR’s framework acknowledges the potential 
role of flexible loads, but could recognize the unique operational capabilities of various 
manifestations of flexible and hyper-flexible loads more explicitly as valuable resources for 
modernizing load interconnection. A federal regime that fails to integrate flexibility into 
interconnection and cost allocation will undervalue the very capabilities that matter most for 
system stability.

IV. Proposition 2: Legacy Interconnection Rules Create Significant Market Barriers 
The second proposition is that the current interconnection framework is misaligned with the 
economic characteristics of modern large loads. It creates significant market barriers and raises 
transaction costs. These frictions are best understood through transaction-cost economics, 
especially Williamson’s emphasis on asset specificity.8 Large-load interconnection requires grid 
infrastructure that is sunk, location-dependent, and ill-suited to alternative uses. At the same 
time, data center developers make their own site-specific investments—land, cooling systems, 
fiber connectivity, and construction—long before the grid is prepared to serve the facility. Both 
parties therefore face the classic hold-up risk: once one side commits irreversible capital, the 
other may act opportunistically. 
From a competition perspective, interconnection rules play a central role in market entry 
conditions. When tariffs treat all loads as inflexible peak contributors, they distort competition, 
raise barriers to entry, and suppress innovation in flexible industrial technologies. The ANOPR’s 
call for a modernized, consistent load-interconnection structure is therefore essential not only 
for reliability but for strengthening competition. By enabling flexible loads to compete on the 
basis of their actual operational profiles, the Commission would reduce regulatory frictions, 
support entrepreneurial risk-taking, and improve market efficiency. ERCOT has demonstrated 
the value of establishing rules that allow for integrated market participation of loads and hybrid 
facilities. Texas’ Private Use Network rules, enabling participation of “energy parks” that may 
integrate load, storage, and generation components, has contributed to the region being one of 
the most competitive locations for new load and hybrid facilities 
A durable standardized large load interconnection framework must begin with a clear 
understanding of the frictions that impede coordination among large-load developers, utilities, 
and grid operators. Transaction-cost economics offers a useful lens because it focuses on the 
features of the transaction—asset specificity, uncertainty, and frequency—and identifies the 
institutional arrangements most capable of reducing those costs. Large-load interconnection 
displays all three characteristics. It involves highly specific assets with limited alternative uses, 
it must navigate deep uncertainty surrounding load development and upgrade timelines, and it 
requires repeated interactions among parties with imperfectly aligned incentives. 
1. Large-load interconnection as a high-asset-specificity transaction 
Transmission upgrades needed to serve large loads are sunk investments that exist primarily, 
but often not exclusively, to serve the requesting facility. Similarly, the load itself often reflects 
investments tailored to a specific site that cannot easily relocate without forfeiting substantial 
value. This symmetry of sunk, site-specific investment generates two forms of hold-up risk. 
Utilities worry that if a developer cancels or delays a project, ratepayers will be saddled with

stranded assets. Developers, having acquired land and begun construction, fear that an 
incumbent transmission owner may delay studies, escalate costs, or impose onerous

The hybrid or co-location contracts that large loads and generators are entering are 
system-enhancing examples of the “make or buy” decision in institutional economics.9 The 
decision to interconnect (Buy) or co-locate/build on-site generation (Make) is determined by the 
relative transaction costs of each path. Long-term contracting through co-location is often a 
rational economic response to high transaction costs and reliability risks in the market, given 
asset specificity and the opportunity for bilateral hold-up. Co-location is a market response to 
the pacing mismatch between data center speed-to-power and infrastructure construction 
timeframes.  
The risks of asset specificity and holdout are magnified by the sheer scale of today’s loads. A 
facility that once required 20 MW may now request 500 to 1,000 MW at a single location, 
raising the stakes of interconnection negotiations and amplifying the value of credible 
commitments, transparent processes, and predictable cost-allocation rules. Where these 
features are absent, both utilities and developers hedge against risk, creating a self-reinforcing 
cycle of caution and delay. Ronald Coase’s insight that scarcity is rationed through non-price 
mechanisms when prices fail to reflect marginal cost is directly relevant: the interconnection 
queue becomes a rationing device rather than a coordination tool.10 
2. Legacy interconnection rules intensify these frictions 
The existing interconnection framework rests on assumptions about load behavior that no 
longer reflect technological reality. It treats loads as passive and uncontrollable, despite the 
flexibility described earlier. Studies model load at its maximum nameplate demand, discounting 
the ability of flexible facilities to maintain power quality through on-site generation, UPS 
response, or automated load controls. Cost-allocation methods presume that upgrades are 
fully attributable to the requesting load, even when they also relieve preexisting constraints or 
support future growth. 
Applying a generator-style interconnection regime to large loads risks entrenching these 
problems further. The generator process is designed for resources that inject power, not for 
large, flexible loads whose operational behaviors differ fundamentally from generators. 
Importing this regime without careful consideration of the other market rules and study 
procedures involved risks embedding outdated assumptions about load rigidity and peak 
coincidence, and fails to recognize the operational capabilities large loads can bring to system 
stability.

3. Beyond interconnection: Market rules as barriers to flexibility 
Interconnection is not the only institutional bottleneck confronting large flexible loads. Even 
when a resource can technically interconnect and is willing to provide grid services, legacy RTO 
and ISO participation rules can prevent it from doing so. The market categories, performance 
requirements, telemetry obligations, and settlement structures embedded in wholesale power 
markets were developed for a system dominated by conventional generators and static loads. 
As a result, many flexible resources, particularly those capable of fast response, hybrid 
operation, or multi-service functionality, struggle to participate on equal terms with incumbent 
technologies. 
The experience of thermal energy storage (TES) illustrates the problem. FERC’s current 
jurisdictional framework treats TES inconsistently depending on which energy vector it 
dispatches. A TES resource that charges with electricity and discharges only electricity is 
treated as a wholesale participant. But a TES resource that charges with electricity and 
discharges thermal energy to an industrial host becomes simultaneously a wholesale and retail 
customer, even though its grid-facing behavior is indistinguishable from a purely electrical TES 
asset. If the TES resource dispatches only thermal output, it is treated exclusively as a retail 
customer. These three configurations impose similar net loads and provide the same 
demand-valley-filling services, yet the regulatory treatment diverges sharply. The resulting 
complexity raises transaction costs, introduces legal risk, and inhibits investment in a 
technology that could improve system flexibility materially. Clarifying that wholesale purchases 
of electricity for conversion into thermal energy qualify as wholesale transactions would reduce 
barriers to TES participation, improve competitive neutrality, and support innovation in 
industrial energy management. 
This regulatory fragmentation is not limited to TES. Many innovative load-side and hybrid 
technologies face similar structural barriers rooted in outdated market definitions. Hybrid 
resources as defined in the ANOPR often do not fit neatly into either generator or load 
categories, pushing developers into duplicative or incompatible participation pathways. 
Demand-side flexibility resources that can respond with granular, automated precision 
encounter telemetry, metering, and aggregation requirements designed for large generators 
rather than for distributed or fast-acting loads. Fast frequency response and inverter-based 
services, including those provided by UPS systems and behind-the-meter batteries, frequently 
cannot qualify for compensation because existing product definitions overlook the temporal 
precision and performance characteristics modern power-electronics technologies can deliver. 
Even aggregated flexible loads, from industrial process controls to AI workload shifting, remain 
ineligible to participate as unified market entities despite offering aggregate capabilities that 
rival or exceed those of utility-scale assets. In each of these cases, the barrier is institutional 
rather than technical. Market rules built to maintain reliability within a twentieth-century 
generation mix now constrain the participation of twenty-first-century flexible resources,

creating a growing misalignment between the system’s emerging needs and the regulatory 
structures designed to procure the services that flexibility can provide. 
From the perspective of institutional and transaction-cost economics, these market barriers 
resemble the same pattern observed in interconnection: when legacy rules impose high 
administrative burdens, misclassify flexible behaviors, or treat new technologies as edge cases, 
the system increases the cost of innovation. Flexible resources that could contribute to 
frequency control, peak reduction, congestion relief, or ramping support are instead assigned 
to categories that view them as deviations from normal operations. The outcome is predictable: 
firms invest in privately beneficial but system-inefficient solutions because the wholesale 
market rules do not provide a viable channel for monetizing their flexibility. 
Lowering these barriers requires modernizing market participation categories, redefining 
ancillary service products to reflect high-resolution flexibility, and explicitly integrating 
load-modulation technologies into wholesale markets. Doing so would align market design with 
the system’s evolving physical characteristics and enable flexible demand-side resources to 
participate on equal footing with traditional supply-side assets. 
V. Proposition 3: Cost Allocation Reform Is Foundational 
The third proposition is that cost allocation reform is foundational to efficient large-load 
integration. The ANOPR suggests that large loads and hybrids should bear the full cost of any 
network upgrades identified in the interconnection study process. The implicit reasoning is 
straightforward: if the load triggers the need for upgrades, the load should pay. At first glance, 
this approach appears consistent with the principle of cost causation. While framed as a 
neutral application of cost causation, it rests on an economically fragile premise: that the 
method producing upgrade assignments accurately reflects long-run marginal cost. 
But this logic holds only if the assigned costs reflect the marginal cost of serving the load. In 
practice, most upgrade assignments do not. Embedded-cost and fully distributed cost (FDC) 
methods routinely generate distorted outcomes that shape siting choices, suppress flexibility, 
and push developers toward bypass strategies that undermine reliability. 
Transmission upgrade cost assignment remains one of the least transparent components of the 
interconnection process. Because most jurisdictions rely on embedded-cost or FDC methods, 
the relationship between a project’s marginal impact and its assigned upgrade cost is weak. 
Two similarly sized projects can face orders-of-magnitude differences in upgrade charges due 
to queue position or earlier network investments. These inconsistencies are incompatible with 
the “just and reasonable” standard, create substantial barriers to rational siting, and encourage 
developers to hedge through speculative queue entries, parallel site evaluations, and 
behind-the-meter self-generation.

The economic analysis of joint product cost allocation is grounded in the regulatory economics 
literature. Coase’s analysis of the marginal-cost controversy showed that marginal-cost pricing 
is essential for allocative efficiency but insufficient to recover the fixed costs characteristic of 
capital-intensive networks.11 Ramsey’s inverse-elasticity rule and Boiteux’s formulation of 
peak-load pricing and multi-part tariffs clarified how to recover fixed costs with minimal 
distortion and how to reflect marginal system conditions in periods of scarcity.12 These 
concepts underpin the cost-causation and beneficiary-pays principles of efficient rate design 
codified by Bonbright.13 
1. The limits of embedded-cost and FDC methods 
Embedded-cost and FDC methods allocate fixed and sunk transmission costs using historical 
accounting data and administrative formulas about contribution to peak load, usage shares, or 
historical averages, rather than actual marginal impacts. This disconnect produces several 
predictable distortions: 
●​ Non-linearity: Costs change discontinuously depending on queue position or the 
triggering of discrete investments. 
●​ Lumpiness: A single entrant may be assigned the full cost of an upgrade sized to serve 
future growth. 
●​ Backward-looking allocation: Assignments embed historical planning decisions 
unrelated to current marginal needs. 
●​ Elasticity blindness: All megawatts are treated alike even when some are highly flexible 
or deferrable. 
Under conditions of rapid load growth, these weaknesses become more consequential. A 
500–1,000 MW request can trigger substation expansions or major transmission additions, 
many of which are joint, lumpy investments intended to serve long-term development with 
multiple beneficiaries. Assigning the entire cost to the final entrant is not marginal cost 
causation; it is an artifact of administrative allocation. 
Moreover, small differences in modeling assumptions—dispatch patterns, contingencies, 
topology—can yield substantially different upgrade requirements. A load may be assigned a 
major upgrade not because it caused the need but because it happened to be evaluated last. 
That outcome highlights why upgrade cost “stickiness” persists even after Order No. 2023 
reforms.

FDC methods are inherently arbitrary and detach cost allocation from marginal impact.14 As 
Bonbright emphasized, these approaches rely on accounting conventions rather than 
economic causation, producing allocations that bear little relationship to the incremental costs 
imposed by individual users. Braeutigam further showed that such methods suppress efficient

The “100 percent upgrade cost” principle is only sound if the underlying methodology is 
economically sound. It is not. 
2. Ramsey-Boiteux as the correct benchmark 
Rather than relying on embedded-cost or fully distributed cost formulas, the Ramsey–Boiteux 
framework offers the standard economic approach for recovering fixed network costs with 
minimal distortion.15 In natural monopoly settings, where marginal-cost pricing cannot cover 
the full cost of lumpy, capital-intensive infrastructure, the Ramsey–Boiteux solution identifies 
how prices should deviate from marginal cost to preserve efficient behavior. Its central insight 
is simple: when fixed costs must be recovered through markups, those markups should be 
inversely related to each customer class’s elasticity of demand. Customers with inelastic 
demand can bear somewhat higher markups without inefficiently reducing use, while 
customers with elastic demand will cut consumption, relocate, or bypass the system if charged 
too much. 
In transmission planning, most upgrades create joint, shared benefits across multiple customer 
types—retail customers, generators, flexible loads, and hybrid resources—each with different 
elasticities. The Ramsey–Boiteux logic applies directly in this multi-beneficiary context: the 
fixed cost of a lumpy upgrade should be allocated in proportion to each beneficiary’s elasticity, 
not according to the timing of queue entry or arbitrary embedded-cost rules. Doing so 
minimizes the aggregate distortion created by fixed-cost recovery. Highly elastic users—those 
capable of relocating, deploying on-site generation, or strategically managing load—should 
face lower markups because pushing them away imposes a large welfare cost. More inelastic 
beneficiaries, whose consumption is less sensitive to price, can bear a larger share of 
fixed-cost recovery with fewer adverse system effects. The result is a cost-allocation structure 
that better reflects actual marginal impacts and preserves efficient usage and siting decisions 
among all parties.

Applied to large-load interconnection, this principle has clear implications. Modern data centers 
and other flexible loads are among the most elastic customers on the system. They can shift 
locations, adopt hybrid on-site generation, or build microgrids if confronted with high upgrade 
charges. Assigning them the full cost of lumpy network upgrades—many of which provide 
long-run benefits to multiple users—maximizes distortion and encourages inefficient bypass, 
behaviors that the ANOPR seeks to avoid. 
A Ramsey-aligned allocation would instead assign lower markups to highly elastic loads, 
especially those that provide measurable flexibility or operational benefits, while allocating a 
greater share of fixed-cost recovery to inelastic beneficiaries. This approach is forward-looking, 
efficiency-oriented, and well suited to a system experiencing rapid demand growth and 
substantial technological change. 
3. Why excessive upgrade charges are counterproductive 
When cost-allocation methods ignore elasticity and flexibility, predictable patterns emerge: 
●​ Developers avoid high-cost jurisdictions regardless of system need. 
●​ Projects migrate to regions with lower allocators, distorting regional development. 
●​ Firms invest in behind-the-fence microgrids, often relying on natural gas or fuel cells, to 
secure faster time-to-power. 
●​ Loads that could provide UPS-based frequency response or controllable demand 
instead isolate themselves electrically. 
These responses work directly against nondiscriminatory access, reliability, and efficient 
integration of new load. 
As load grows rapidly, the economics of these distortions become more visible. Many 
upgrades triggered in interconnection studies are joint, forward-looking investments. Assigning 
their full cost to a single entrant is economically unsound and ignores the shared nature of 
transmission infrastructure. 
The ANOPR’s suggestion of 100% participant funding (Principle 8) assumes that a new load is 
a static addition to a fixed system. In a dynamic grid, a flexible load provides system-wide 
benefits by increasing asset utilization. Assigning 100% of costs to an entrant who creates 
positive spillover effects violates the beneficiary-pays principle and discourages the very 
dynamic, decentralized participation that modern digital technologies enable. 
4. Toward economically sound cost allocation 
FERC should not adopt a blanket rule obligating large loads to bear 100 percent of assigned 
upgrade costs without first reforming the allocation methodology to reflect marginal cost 
principles. A coherent federal framework would:

●​ Use two-part tariffs separating marginal operating costs from fixed network cost 
recovery. 
●​ Rely on long-run marginal cost (LRMC) for forward-looking upgrade assessments 
rather than historical accounting. 
●​ Incorporate locational differentiation so signals reflect true incremental cost rather 
than arbitrary allocations. 
●​ Provide reduced upgrade burdens for loads with verifiable flexibility, including 
UPS-based fast response, curtailable service, and hybrid on-site generation. 
●​ Minimize bypass incentives, ensuring flexible, grid-supportive loads face a rational 
economic case for interconnection rather than isolation. 
Cost allocation should reflect the principle that customers pay the costs they impose on the 
system, minus any benefits they provide. Flexible loads that avoid peak demand (including 
based on grid operator signals) do not cause peak-driven upgrades or capacity additions and 
should not be assigned such costs. When modeling assumptions reflect enforceable 
commitments and hybrid facility strategies, cost assignments become more accurate and more 
consistent with competitive neutrality. This change in methods, alongside a move away from 
FDC and toward Ramsey-Boiteux allocation methods, improves the efficiency of investment 
decisions and ensures that tariff design does not favor legacy technologies over innovative 
ones. 
Aligning cost allocation with marginal impacts ensures that flexible loads remain part of the 
bulk system rather than retreating into private microgrids. It allows large loads to integrate as 
reliability-enhancing resources rather than becoming disconnected pockets of generation and 
demand. Most importantly, it supports the ANOPR’s objective of enabling timely, efficient, and 
nondiscriminatory access to the transmission system. 
VI. Design Principles Derived from Propositions 1–3 
The analysis in Sections I–V points toward a coherent set of design principles for a modern 
federal approach to large-load interconnection. These principles flow directly from the 
economics of flexibility, the transaction-cost burdens of legacy rules, and the need for 
marginal-cost-aligned cost allocation and fixed cost recovery. 
A sensible framework begins by treating flexibility as a system value rather than an operational 
irregularity. Modern large loads can supply flexibility that substitutes for missing supply 
elasticity and reduces marginal system costs. Regulatory structures must therefore embed their 
capabilities into interconnection studies, market-participation pathways, and cost allocation. 
The federal role should also focus on reducing transaction costs. Many of the barriers 
examined earlier arise because interconnection and market rules treat loads as passive, 
uncontrollable entities, which increases uncertainty and magnifies hold-up risk. A

well-designed framework lowers these frictions and supports credible commitments that reflect 
the operational realities of flexible loads. 
Finally, cost allocation must be anchored in marginal-cost principles and informed by the 
Ramsey–Boiteux logic for recovering fixed network costs. Transmission expansion is lumpy and 
jointly beneficial; allocating those fixed costs through embedded-cost formulas is economically 
incoherent and distorts investment and siting decisions. A modern approach must instead align 
charges with beneficiaries’ elasticities and marginal impacts. 
Together, these principles outline a federal role that is modular and adaptive, one that 
encourages innovation rather than constraining it. 
VII. Recommendations for a Federal Framework for Large-Load Interconnection 
The design principles translate into a set of recommendations that modernize the federal 
framework in a way that aligns interconnection processes with the operational capabilities of 
large, flexible loads. 
A modern framework requires interconnection options that explicitly recognize and value 
flexibility. Curtailable or limited-service arrangements give developers access to the grid at 
lower cost and faster timelines when they can accept conditional service. Loads capable of 
demonstrating enforceable flexibility—whether through UPS-based response, TES operation, 
hybrid generation, or workload shifting—should be eligible for accelerated study treatment. 
Interconnection analyses should reflect these capabilities by evaluating the actual operational 
profile of the load rather than assuming peak nameplate demand during scarcity events. 
Market-participation pathways must be modernized as well. Participation categories, 
performance requirements, and telemetry obligations within RTOs and ISOs were designed for 
a system dominated by large generators and static loads. To enable modern demand-side 
flexibility, these rules must evolve. Flexible loads and hybrid resources need pathways that 
treat them as legitimate grid participants capable of supplying ancillary services, stability 
support, and demand response. Clarifying the jurisdictional status of technologies such as TES 
would resolve inconsistencies that currently prevent these resources from accessing wholesale 
markets on competitive terms. 
The framework should also support competitive and transparent mechanisms that reduce 
strategic behavior and ease siting. Merchant and independent transmission projects can 
provide alternatives when incumbent processes lag. Tradable queue positions and upgrade 
rights can improve project sequencing and reduce incentives for speculative delay. Transparent 
hosting-capacity information and standardized modeling assumptions can lower search costs 
and provide developers with visibility into where their flexibility is most valuable and where it 
least strains the system.

VIII. Economically Sound Cost Allocation Framework 
The analysis in Section V demonstrates that cost allocation is a pivotal element of an efficient 
interconnection regime. The inadequacies of embedded-cost and FDC methodologies are by 
now well understood. These approaches rely on historical accounting rather than economic 
causation, producing non-linear, backward-looking, and elasticity-blind assignments that 
distort siting decisions and create inconsistent outcomes across similar projects. 
Embedded-cost allocation obscures marginal impacts, embeds sunk costs into pricing 
structures, and encourages consumption and investment patterns inconsistent with efficient 
network use. 
Moving toward an economically coherent framework requires adopting the logic of 
Ramsey–Boiteux pricing for joint, lumpy transmission investments. The central insight is that 
fixed network costs should be recovered in a way that minimizes distortions, which means 
allocating the burden based on beneficiaries’ relative elasticities. In transmission planning, 
upgrades almost always benefit multiple user classes with differing degrees of elasticity, 
including retail customers, generators, flexible loads, and hybrid resources. A 
Ramsey-consistent approach allocates fixed costs across these classes in a way that imposes 
lower markups on elastic users—those capable of relocating, self-supplying, or modulating 
consumption—and higher markups on inelastic ones. This logic applies directly to modern 
large loads, which tend to be among the most elastic participants in the system. Charging them 
the full cost of lumpy upgrades predictably induces bypass, undermining both efficiency and 
reliability. 
A forward-looking, marginal-cost-based approach therefore requires more than abandoning 
FDC. It calls for LRMC-based upgrade assessments, cost reductions for loads that provide 
verifiable flexibility or hybrid on-site generation, and tariff designs that separate marginal 
operating costs from fixed network recovery. These elements create a cost-allocation 
framework that reflects actual system impacts, minimizes bypass incentives, and encourages 
the integration of grid-supportive flexibility into the bulk system. 
IX. How the Recommended Framework Advances the Objectives in the ANOPR 
A federal framework built on these principles directly advances the ANOPR’s goals of timely, 
orderly, and nondiscriminatory interconnection. Flexibility-centered interconnection options and 
transparent study processes shorten timelines, reduce queue congestion, and improve 
alignment between load development and grid expansion. By standardizing modeling 
assumptions and requiring clearer documentation of study practices, the framework also 
increases predictability, reduces disputes, and improves coordination across regions. 
Modernizing market rules and integrating flexible loads into ancillary service products promotes 
nondiscriminatory access. Treating flexible loads on equal terms with traditional resources 
recognizes their ability to contribute to frequency response, peak reduction, and congestion

X. Conclusion 
The structural load growth associated with AI and data center expansion presents a real 
challenge, but one well within the capability of a modernized regulatory framework designed to 
recognize and reward flexibility. Legacy interconnection rules, market categories, and 
cost-allocation methodologies were developed for a system with very different characteristics. 
Left unchanged, they will suppress flexibility, slow innovation, and encourage inefficient 
bypass. 
The analysis in this comment shows that a principles-based, modular federal approach—one 
that integrates flexibility into interconnection and market design, reduces transaction costs, and 
aligns cost allocation with marginal impacts—offers a more effective path forward. Such a 
framework will allow FERC and DOE to meet the moment: enabling timely, orderly, and 
nondiscriminatory integration of large loads while strengthening system reliability, improving 
market performance, and supporting the evolution of a flexible, resilient, and innovation-ready 
electricity system.
