II. 
SUMMARY OF THE JOINT CONSUMER ADVOCATES’ ANOPR COMMENTS 
These Comments respond to the ANOPR on behalf of residential electricity consumers in 
Pennsylvania and Delaware. The Joint Consumer Advocates support jurisdictional federal avenues 
for regulating the interstate transmission and wholesale market aspects of large load 
interconnection, while respecting states’ existing authority, including the authority to interconnect 
large loads at the distribution level. 
The Joint Consumer Advocates’ comments prioritize the following, to the extent that they 
do not impermissibly preempt existing state jurisdiction: defining what it means to “directly” 
interconnect to transmission facilities and the implications of direct interconnection on states’ 
authority over transmission siting; supporting the ANOPR’s 20 MW threshold for large loads; 
supporting reduced interconnection study timelines, if they fully account for costs to other grid

The ANOPR’s interpretation of the FPA offers insufficient 
support for FERC to preempt any aspect of  historic state police

Pennsylvania and federal courts presume that ambiguous federal statutory text does not 
preempt laws and regulations enacted pursuant to historic state police power.48 Moreover, 
Pennsylvania courts strongly assert and protect the sovereign interests of Pennsylvania citizens in 
the federalist structure.49 Congress must make an “unmistakably clear” preemptive statement “in 
the language of the statute” to preempt the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania’s exercise of its 
historic state police power.50 Delaware law is similar:  “Courts are reluctant to find federal 
preemption because of their interest in avoiding unintended encroachment on the authority of a 
state…. Only when Congress’ intent to provide preemption is clear and manifest will Courts allow 
such measures.”51 Regarding the ANOPR at issue, the power to regulate local distribution and 
service to end-use customers is a historic state police power,52 and the FPA has no preemptive 
effect here because the authority at issue is not “unmistakably clear.” Instead, this retail 
interconnection authority has a history, tradition, and explicit acknowledgement in a landmark 
Order from FERC showing that it is a reserved state power.53

The Joint Consumer Advocates support a package of ratepayer protections to the extent 
that the Commission finds them to be jurisdictional: 1) minimum contractual duration, renewal, 
and cancellation provisions; 2) minimum demand payments and demand ramping requirements; 
3) financial collateral and guarantees. This package of protections and safeguards is necessary to 
prevent cost-shifting from large load and hybrid facilities to existing ratepayers, as the 
methodology for allocation of transmission costs in unbundled retail sales is subject to the 
Commission’s jurisdiction. 
 
Due to the significant transmission investment required to serve large load customers, it is 
imperative to ensure that the customers will pay and continue to pay the costs of necessary 
upgrades, even if such upgrades provide benefits to other network customers.120 If the large load 
customer ceases to receive service at the point of interconnection, any costs directly assigned to 
the customer paid-for through the transmission utility’s formula rates become stranded and are 
folded into the revenue requirement recovered from other network customers of the transmission 
utility. Additionally, other network customers lose the benefit of the large load’s contributions to 
overall network rates as, at present, large load customers may reduce transmission rates across the 
servicing utility’s service territory.  
 
To ensure that the costs do not become stranded, and that other network customers do not 
end up having to foot the bill for the large load customer’s premature termination of service, it is 
imperative to set certain ratepayer protections. While transmission rates are a retail charge, these 
determinations are made at the time of interconnection, not after. At the time interconnection, the 
transmission utility has a reasonable opportunity to put ratemaking protections in place to recover 
its stranded costs.

The Joint Consumer Advocates support the establishment of clear contract terms regarding

forecasted peak demand at the conclusion of the ramp period would be reasonable. Importantly, 
this minimum demand charge – if in excess of actual demand – should be credited towards the 
network rates of other transmission customers. Doing so provides immediate benefits to the 
interconnection of a large load customer while providing relief against the transmission costs 
incurred by the interconnection of that customer. As noted above, this suggestion is driven by (1) 
comments that the PA OCA reviewed in a relevant Pennsylvania regulatory proceeding, where a 
transmission owner has asserted that it credits the revenue collected from a minimum demand 
charge against the transmission owner’s formula rate revenue requirement to the benefit of all 
customer; and (2) concern for the impact of the Tri-State case in restructured/unbundled states. 
 
Another protection for other network transmission customers is requiring a security 
deposit. While the use of the security deposit may vary – because it is drawn down to pay for 
minimum charges or because it is directly applied to the transmission utility’s plant in service 
balance for the amount added to service the customer – it, when coupled with the minimum 
contract term length, alleviates the risk of stranded transmission plant costs.

IV. 
CONCLUSION 
In summary, when defining “interconnections directly to transmission facilities,” and 
generally, FERC should avoid preempting existing state authority under the FPA. A proposed rule 
based on the ANOPR should consider implementing the minimum terms, conditions, standards, 
and guidelines outlined in these comments. And the rule should do so according to FERC’s 
established consumer protection mandate and cost causation principles.
