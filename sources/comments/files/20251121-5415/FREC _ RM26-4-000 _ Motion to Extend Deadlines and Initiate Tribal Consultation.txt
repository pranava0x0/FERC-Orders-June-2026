I. Introduction 
Pursuant to Rule 2008 of the Commission’s Rules of Practice and Procedure, 18 
C.F.R. § 385.2008, the National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) respectfully 
submits this motion to extend the deadlines for comments and reply comments in the 
above-captioned proceeding and to formally request government-to-government 
consultation with Tribal Nations as required by federal law and Commission policy. 
Founded in 1944, NCAI is the oldest, largest, and most representative organization of 
American Indian and Alaska Native Tribal governments, and our policy positions are 
directed by consensus from our membership of nearly 300 Tribal Nations. NCAI 
acknowledges the Department’s stated intent to ensure that new technologies are 
developed and implemented in a manner consistent with its trust and treaty 
responsibilities. We also appreciate that the Order recognizes Tribal Nations as 
essential partners in this work. 
NCAI acknowledges the Commission’s “Notice Granting Extension of Time” issued 
on November 7, 2025. This minimal 7-day extension (to November 21) is wholly 
insufficient. It fails to address the unique procedural and substantive deficiencies the 
Department of Energy's Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking ("ANOPR") raises 
for Tribal Nations, which are sovereign governments, not mere stakeholders. 
We therefore reiterate our motion for a meaningful extension of the comment period 
and, critically, our formal request for the Commission to initiate immediate, 
government-to-government consultation as required by federal law and the trust 
responsibility. See, e.g., United States v. Kagama, 118 U.S. 375 (1886); Seminole 
Nation v. United States, 316 U.S. 286 (1942).

II. The Commission’s Process Remains Fundamentally Deficient 
A. The 7-Day Extension Is Procedurally Insufficient 
The Commission’s November 7 Order implicitly acknowledges that the original 16-day comment period 
was unworkable. Yet the minimal 7-day extension — granted only in response to state organizations 
citing "technical and legal complexity" — remains profoundly insufficient. It completely disregards the far 
more complex and time-consuming sovereign-to-sovereign consultation required for Tribal Nations. Tribal 
governments must conduct internal governmental review, legal analysis, and leadership deliberation 
before submitting an official position. Compressing this process into a total of 24 days renders meaningful 
participation effectively impossible and violates the spirit of the Administrative Procedure Act ("APA"), 5 
U.S.C. § 553. 
B. The Failure to Consult Has Not Been Cured 
Critically, the Commission's November 7 Order also fails to mention Tribal Nations or cure the 
proceeding's underlying failure to consult. Neither the Department of Energy’s (DOE) proposed ANOPR 
nor any subsequent Commission notice acknowledges the existence of Tribal Nations, Tribal sovereignty, 
treaty rights, or the federal trust responsibility. 
Because this proceeding will have “substantial direct effects on one or more Tribal nations,” it triggers 
explicit consultation duties under Executive Order 13175, "Consultation and Coordination with Indian 
Tribal Governments," 65 Fed. Reg. 67249 (Nov. 9, 2000). It also violates the Commission’s own binding 
policy, Policy Statement on Consultation with Indian Tribes in Commission Proceedings, 104 Federal 
Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC)   61,108 (2003). Furthermore, the ANOPR fails to analyze its 
impacts under foundational statutes governing Tribal lands, such as the Alaska Native Claims Settlement 
Act ("ANCSA"), 43 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq. Proceeding without consultation violates these binding federal 
obligations. 
III. The ANOPR Has Profound, Unexamined Tribal Implications 
This rulemaking is not a narrow technical exercise. The 14 principles for reform proposed in the DOE's 
ANOPR represent a potential jurisdictional and economic restructuring of the U.S. energy system, with 
direct and disproportionate consequences for the 574 federally recognized Tribal Nations. 
A. Implications for Tribal Sovereignty and Land Use 
The ANOPR’s proposed principles aim to accelerate infrastructure, such as data centers and expanded 
transmission corridors ("network upgrades"). These linear projects will invariably intersect with Tribal 
lands, sacred sites, water rights, cultural resources, and federally protected, off-reservation treaty rights. 
The federal government has a duty to protect these treaty-reserved rights. See, e.g., Minnesota v. Mille 
Lacs Band of Chippewa Indians, 526 U.S. 172 (1999). 
The proposal is silent on how its new, expedited processes will comply with mandatory federal 
consultation requirements, such as Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act ("NHPA"), 54 
U.S.C. § 306108, or the National Environmental Policy Act ("NEPA"), 42 U.S.C. § 4321 et seq. 
B. Implications for Tribal Economic Self-Determination 
The ANOPR's Principle “Cost Responsibility,” which allocates 100% of network upgrade costs to the load, 
effectively privileges large corporate developers while undermining Tribal Nations’ ability to pursue

energy or industrial projects. This approach contradicts the federal government’s trust responsibility to 
promote Tribal self-sufficiency. See, e.g., Indian Reorganization Act of 1934, 25 U.S.C. § 5301 et seq., 
and the Indian Energy Policy Reform Act of 2005. 
This failure is acute for the 229 Tribal Nations in Alaska, as the ANOPR fails to account for the unique 
economic and land-owning structure of ANCSA corporations or its effect on isolated Tribal microgrids. 
Moreover, the proposal creates a direct conflict with established law. The ANOPR's Principle "Option to 
Build" on Tribal trust lands requires federal rights-of-way, which are subject to Tribal consent under 25 
U.S.C. §§ 323–328. The ANOPR fails to address this fundamental legal requirement. 
Furthermore, consultation mechanisms must recognize the diversity of Tribal governance systems, 
including Pueblos, intertribal organizations, Alaska Native villages, and ANCSA corporations. A 
one-size-fits-all approach will not fulfill federal obligations. 
C. Jurisdictional Conflicts 
By framing the ANOPR’s jurisdictional discussion solely around federal and state roles, the proposal 
disregards Tribal Nations as the third sovereign within the United States. This omission overlooks the 
well-established legal principle that Tribal Nations retain inherent sovereignty over their lands. See, e.g., 
McGirt v. Oklahoma, 140 S. Ct. 2452 (2020). Excluding Tribal governments from jurisdictional 
considerations risks renewed conflicts over permitting, resource management, and taxation on Tribal 
lands: conflicts that could be prevented through meaningful consultation. 
IV. Formal Requests for Corrective Action 
NCAI acknowledges the 7-day extension granted on November 7, 2025. As this is insufficient, NCAI 
respectfully demands the following: 
1.​ Grant a further extension of the comment and reply comment deadlines to a date no less than 120 
days from the original October 28 notice. This is the minimum time necessary for sovereign Tribal 
governments to respond meaningfully. 
2.​ Initiate formal, government-to-government consultation with Tribal Nations in compliance with 
Executive Order 13175 and the Commission’s 2003 Tribal Consultation Policy. This consultation 
must occur prior to the close of any new comment period. 
3.​ Acknowledge and incorporate Tribal Nations as sovereign governments in all subsequent 
Commission notices and rulemaking documents related to this docket. 
V. Pathways for Compliance and Collaboration 
NCAI recommends that the Commission establish a structured consultation process consistent with 
Executive Order 13175, including: 
●​ Regional listening sessions co-hosted with Tribal governments across the four FERC regions; 
●​ A Tribal Advisory Technical Working Group to review implications for transmission planning and 
permitting; and 
●​ Written summaries and follow-up reports shared with all Tribal Nations prior to the issuance of any 
Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NOPR). 
These measures would operationalize consultation in a way that is transparent, efficient, and inclusive of 
the full diversity of Tribal governance structures.

VI. Conclusion 
Meaningful consultation and adequate review are not procedural preferences; they are legal and moral 
imperatives grounded in the U.S. Constitution (Art. I, § 8, cl. 3; Art. VI, cl. 2), treaties, and the federal trust 
responsibility. 
For these reasons, NCAI respectfully requests that the Commission grant a meaningful extension to at 
least February 25, 2026, and initiate formal Tribal consultation prior to proceeding further in this docket.
