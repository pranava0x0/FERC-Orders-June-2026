COMMENTS OF THE ADVANCED ENERGY MANAGEMENT ALLIANCE (AEMA) 
I. 
INTRODUCTION 
AEMA1 respectfully submits these comments in response to the Notice Inviting Comments 
regarding the proposed Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANOPR) regarding the timely 
and orderly interconnection of large loads to the interstate transmission system.2 
These comments are submitted by the Advanced Energy Management Alliance (AEMA) and a 
coalition of companies and organizations dedicated to advancing economic growth through 
advanced energy solutions while improving the efficiency and reliability of the nation’s electric 
system. Since its founding more than a decade ago, AEMA has focused on expanding access to 
demand response and distributed energy resources (DERs) to lower costs and improve grid 
stability. As the power system enters a new era of large-scale computing and manufacturing 
growth, AEMA’s work now extends to integrating flexible industrial and data center loads that 
can enhance reliability while supporting economic expansion. 
II. 
SUMMARY 
AEMA strongly supports the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission’s (Commission) initiative 
to establish standardized, transparent, and efficient procedures for interconnecting large loads to 
the transmission system. Rapid growth in artificial intelligence, semiconductor manufacturing, 
and advanced data-center infrastructure has created new patterns of electricity demand that 
require faster and more predictable interconnection processes.  
AEMA agrees with the urgent need for reform articulated in the ANOPR. AEMA urges the 
Commission to create a Flexible Load Fast Track (FLFT) for facilities that can verifiably adjust 
their consumption in response to system conditions and harness a broad set of technologies to

III. 
BACKGROUND AND POLICY CONTEXT 
The Secretary of Energy’s directive of October 23, 2025, calls for prompt Commission action to 
ensure that large loads such as AI data centers and advanced manufacturing facilities can 
interconnect to the transmission system efficiently and on equal terms. 
Historically, the Commission’s efforts to standardize generator interconnections through Orders 
888, 2003, and 2023 created transparency and certainty for new supply resources. A comparable 
framework for large loads is now needed. Current state-by-state processes are inconsistent, 
opaque, and time-consuming, resulting in multi-year delays that slow economic growth amid an 
AI revolution that presents a critical opportunity for U.S. innovation, competitiveness, and 
prosperity. 
The United States is witnessing the largest single-decade increase in industrial electricity demand 
in over forty years.3 Data centers serving AI and digital services often require hundreds of 
megawatts to more than a gigawatt of generation capacity to power them, comparable to 
traditional generation projects.4 Standardized interconnection procedures that recognize the 
flexible operating characteristics will allow these facilities to connect more quickly and at lower 
overall system cost. 
IV. 
AEMA’S OVERARCHING POSITION 
AEMA supports the Commission’s proposal to standardize interconnection procedures for large 
loads and stridently recommends that the final rule: 
1. Create a Flexible Load Fast Track (FLFT) for facilities that make verifiable flexibility 
commitments, with clear timelines for studies and approvals within the FLFT. 
2. Direct transmission owners and Regional Transmission Operators (“RTOs”) to provide 
transparency to large loads on the specific constraints and resulting grid upgrades, if any, 
their grid connection would cause, and the necessary actions to address the constraints 
and defer or avoid the upgrade. 
3. Define flexibility using performance-based criteria that are technology-neutral and 
encompass software and hardware approaches.

V. 
FLEXIBILITY AS A GRID RESOURCE 
AEMA generally supports each of the principles outlined in the ANOPR. Most importantly, 
AEMA enthusiastically supports the ANOPR’s Principle 7, which calls for expedited 
interconnection studies for large loads that agree to be curtailable or dispatchable. Flexibility is a 
grid resource that should be rewarded through faster study and approval. The 60-day timeline 
proffered by the ANOPR is reasonable and nearly aligns with the 90-day that the Commission 
approved for Southwest Power Pool as part of their “High Impact Large Load” Policy. 
The Commission, with its issuance and implementation of Order Nos. 719 and 745 is well aware 
of the resilience, reliability and economic benefits of load flexibility. A flexible facility that can 
modulate a portion of its demand during system peaks reduces the need for grid infrastructure, 
creating value for that facility and all other customers in the market. The Commission should 
direct transmission providers to treat this verifiable reduction as equivalent to a system-impact 
mitigation, allowing such projects to be studied and interconnected on a shorter timeline. The 
FLFT rewards verifiable outcomes rather than prescribing any particular asset or control 
architecture. 
I. 
AEMA recommends that the Commission direct transmission owners and RTOs to 
establish a dedicated Flexible Load Fast Track process, parallel to the existing serial 
queue. The FLFT would allow facilities meeting defined flexibility thresholds to receive 
interconnection study approval within 60 to 90 days. For the FLFT, the Commission 
should also expedite the processes for “Interconnection Agreements” for Large Loads 
and the construction of physical upgrades. After studies are completed, these agreements 
and construction can sometimes take years to complete. Such a timeline directly 
contradicts the ANOPR’s urgency and goal for “efficient, timely, and non-discriminatory 
load interconnections.”  
Critically, this FLFT commitment should be defined and bounded. The flexibility offered by a 
large load is a specific, quantifiable resource, not an open-ended obligation. AEMA recommends 
that the interconnection study process be used to define clear, upper-bound limits on the required 
flexibility (e.g., maximum number of hours per year, hours per event, depth of flexibility, and 
events per year) needed to mitigate specific system constraints. This approach is based on 
established reliability modeling and ensures that data center operators have the operational 
certainty required to invest in these high-value facilities, achieving both grid needs and economic 
productivity.

II. 
DEFINING FLEXIBILITY 
The final rule should define flexibility in functional terms that encourage innovation. AEMA 
recommends four categories: 
1. Temporal flexibility: The ability to shift or modulate computing or industrial power 
consumption over time in response to operator signals or price events. 
2. Spatial flexibility: The capability to shift computing or industrial activity geographically 
to relieve local congestion or reliability constraints. 
3. Resource flexibility: The coordinated use of on-site assets such as storage, thermal 
systems, or other controllable resources to manage total facility load.

4. Adjacent flexibility: The ability to coordinate with nearby storage, aggregated third-party 
resources, or other controllable resources so that the combined effect reduces net 
withdrawals on the locally constrained parts of the transmission system. 
Software-based controls, hardware-based assets (such as storage or on-site generation), and 
hybrid approaches are all valid methods for delivering these forms of flexibility. The final rule 
should remain strictly technology-neutral, focusing not on the method used, but on the 
measurable, verifiable, and reliable performance of the resources at their point of 
interconnection. 
III. 
SYSTEM-OPERATOR CONTROL AND VERIFICATION 
System reliability requires that operators have confidence in the performance of flexible loads. 
This can be achieved through a secure, standardized operator–facility interface—which may be 
implemented through software, hardware controls, or both—rather than direct physical utility 
control of on‑site systems. 
Under this model, the operator issues a dispatch signal, for example a 100-megawatt curtailment 
request to the facility's registered energy management interface. The facility’s flexibility 
management platform executes the reduction automatically and provides real-time or near-real-
time, auditable telemetry back to the operator to confirm compliance. This approach mirrors 
long-standing successful demand-response verification practices, maintains cybersecurity, and 
respects operational autonomy while giving operators dependable visibility into performance. 
IV. 
RELIABILITY AND COST BENEFITS 
Integrating flexibility into the interconnection process improves reliability and keeps energy 
costs in check. Fast-acting load adjustments can reduce the frequency of local congestion events, 
defer transmission upgrades, and moderate wholesale price volatility. 
For example, a 500-megawatt data-center facility that can curtail 100 megawatts during system 
peaks effectively provides a reliability service comparable to new peaking generation. The 
avoided cost of network reinforcement and generation capacity benefits all consumers. For 
high‑value computing, curtailment should be targeted and infrequent, tied to defined system 
conditions and used only as necessary to protect reliability. For hardware-based assets (such as 
storage or on-site generation), flexibility should be dispatched according to the operating 
characteristics of the resources. The FLFT balances fast access to power with predictable 
operating conditions, making flexibility a practical option for economically critical workloads. 
Flexibility also allows data centers and manufacturers to meet their growth potential while 
enabling the power system to maintain cost discipline. AI infrastructure is essential to national 
security, productivity and innovation; incorporating flexibility ensures that this growth enhances, 
rather than strains, the grid.

ALIGNMENT WITH OTHER ANOPR PRINCIPLES

AEMA’s recommendation for a performance-based Flexible Load Fast Track (FLFT) can be a 
key mechanism for implementing several of the Commission's other principles in a cohesive, 
market-based manner.  
• Principle 3 (Study Coordination): The FLFT provides a framework for studying co-
located or adjacent generation and load together. By studying the "net" withdrawal or 
injection, as this principle suggests, the FLFT can accurately value the reduced system 
impact, leading to deferred upgrades and shorter study timelines. 
• Principle 4 (Readiness Requirements): AEMA supports standardized readiness 
requirements. The FLFT can incorporate clear, transparent criteria (such as deposits and 
site control) consistent with generator interconnection, appropriately scaled for large 
loads to deter speculative projects. 
• Principle 5 (Hybrid Facilities): The FLFT can be the pathway to appropriately study 
hybrid facilities. A data center that commits 100 MW of verifiable flexibility (potentially 
spanning on-site storage, workload orchestration, and adjacent distributed energy 
resources) should be studied based on its "net" firm withdrawal rights, consistent with 
this principle. 
• Principle 6 (System Protection): The secure, verifiable interface described in Section VI 
can be the modern "system protection facility" called for in Principle 6. It is the software-
based protection that provides auditable assurance that the load will curtail when required 
within its defined operating limits, thereby preventing unauthorized withdrawals and 
protecting the system. 
• Principle 8 (Cost Allocation): AEMA believes the FLFT provides a crucial, market-based 
choice for large loads: (1) interconnect as an inflexible load and potentially bear the costs 
for the resulting, often slow and expensive, network upgrades; or (2) interconnect as a 
flexible load via the FLFT, avoid triggering those upgrades, and get online faster at lower 
cost. This protects all other ratepayers from socializing upgrade costs. 
• Principles 11 & 12 (Transmission and Ancillary Service): To operationalize the FLFT, 
AEMA supports new transmission service products. A "Non-Firm Withdrawal Right," for 
example, could be an appropriate tariff-based underpinning. This clarification ensures 
facilities can combine firm service for mission-critical loads with a separate, curtailable 
portion, which would in turn have different ancillary service responsibilities (Principle 
12). 
• Principles 13 & 14 (Transition and NERC Coordination): AEMA supports a clear 
transition plan (Principle 13) that allows projects already in the queue to opt in to the 
FLFT if they meet its flexibility and readiness criteria. We also support coordination with 
NERC (Principle 14) to establish uniform telemetry and performance reporting standards, 
which can enable the "verifiable" aspect of the FLFT. 
VI. 
IMPLEMENTATION AND TRANSITION 
To ensure effective implementation, AEMA recommends that the Commission: 
1. Direct transmission providers to file standardized Flexible Load Fast Track procedures 
within 12 months of the final rule.

2. Permit existing queue participants to opt in to the FLFT process if they can demonstrate 
verified flexibility. 
3. Coordinate with NERC to establish uniform telemetry and measurement standards. 
4. Hold a technical conference within six months of final rule issuance to share best 
practices and develop a common reporting framework across regions. 
In the interim, ISOs should pilot flexible-load interconnection processes and report results within 
one year. These steps will accelerate learning, promote uniformity, and prevent regional 
disparities. 
VII. 
CONCLUSION 
AEMA appreciates the Commission’s leadership in addressing the challenges posed by rapid 
growth in large industrial and data-center loads. Standardizing interconnection procedures and 
recognizing flexibility as a key reliability asset will enable continued U.S. leadership in AI and 
manufacturing while maintaining affordable and reliable power for all consumers. 
AEMA respectfully urges the Commission to adopt a final rule that establishes a Flexible Load 
Fast Track, defines flexibility broadly in a technology-neutral and performance-based manner, 
and ensures that large-scale load growth contributes to both reliability and national 
competitiveness. We look forward to working with the Commission as it moves through this 
rulemaking process.
