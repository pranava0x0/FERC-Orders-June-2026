The Commission Should Decline to Entertain Issues Beyond the Scope of the 
ANOPR. 
 
In the initial comments, a number of parties raised issues that are clearly beyond the scope 
of the proposed ANOPR.  The Commission, however, must maintain its focus on timing and need 
to adopt rules within an appropriate scope.  It is critical that the Commission adhere to the scope 
of the ANOPR (instead of expanding it) so that the Commission can achieve the stated goals of 
the ANOPR and avoid unintended adverse consequences to other customers.  In particular, if the 
Commission wishes to promote timely interconnections to advance artificial intelligence (“AI”) 
innovation, it should limit its inquiry and avoid expanding the scope of this proceeding to address 
issues and concerns that are not sufficiently related to achieving that goal, such as those discussed 
below.  Failure to do so will result in a larger, more confused record, a risk of increased litigation, 
and create more hurdles for both transmission providers and data center customers.5

LS Power’s request also highlights serious jurisdictional issues. Large load

distribution (not just transmission) facilities.  The regulation of local distribution facilities is 
reserved to the states,10 and thus, the Commission has no authority to impose federal planning 
requirements for local distribution upgrades.  Nothing in the ANOPR indicates an intent to regulate 
local distribution-related upgrades or distribution rates.11  Nevertheless, in order to avoid further 
concerns, and potential litigation, regarding jurisdictional boundaries, the Commission should 
decline to accept LS Power’s invitation to reconsider fundamental planning issues in the context 
of this rulemaking.  Doing so is unnecessary and would be inimical to the ANOPR’s stated goals. 
Likewise, there is no merit to LS Power’s suggestion that the Commission should 
incorporate provisions from the PJM DEAs “related to cost and schedule” into the large load 
interconnection context.12  DEAs are not interconnection agreements.  Rather, in PJM, they are 
contracts to ensure developers take on binding obligations to construct projects selected for 
inclusion in the PJM Regional Transmission Expansion Plan (“RTEP”) through a competitive 
window.  There is no logical overlap between DEAs and potential large load interconnection 
agreements, and thus no reason for the Commission to complicate the record by considering issues 
regarding DEAs in this proceeding.
