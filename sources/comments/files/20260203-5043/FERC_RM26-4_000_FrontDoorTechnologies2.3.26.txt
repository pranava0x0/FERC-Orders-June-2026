I. INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY
The Commission has requested comment on reforms to accelerate interconnection of large loads, particularly
hyperscale data centers. Recent advocacy submissions to this docket propose that Virtual Power Plants and
residential Demand Response resources should receive interconnection credit based on their Effective Load
Carrying Capability values derived from regional Resource Adequacy markets. Pew Charitable Trusts and
Rewiring America have specifically advocated for 60-70% interconnection offsets based on PJM's published
ELCC values for aggregated Demand Response.
This comment demonstrates that such proposals fundamentally conflate two distinct grid reliability mechanisms
that operate under entirely different engineering and regulatory frameworks: Resource Adequacy and Nodal
Interconnection. While Virtual Power Plants provide substantial value for regional resource adequacy, their
statistical performance across multi-state footprints cannot be translated into physical capacity relief at specific
transmission or distribution nodes without violating basic principles of power system engineering and the
Commission's own precedent regarding worst-case interconnection study assumptions.
The application of Resource Adequacy metrics to interconnection engineering creates what I term a "Regulatory
Hallucination"—a policy framework that appears mathematically valid on paper but lacks physical
correspondence to actual grid conditions at the point of interconnection. Implementing such a framework would
expose utilities, grid operators, and data center developers to reliability failures that manifest as actual
equipment damage and service interruptions, not merely financial settlements.
II. RESOURCE ADEQUACY AND INTERCONNECTION SERVE FUNDAMENTALLY
DIFFERENT RELIABILITY FUNCTIONS
A. Resource Adequacy is a Regional Statistical Insurance Mechanism
Resource Adequacy markets evaluate whether a regional system has sufficient aggregate generation capacity to
meet forecasted peak demand plus reserve margins across an entire multi-state footprint. ELCC is a statistical
methodology that assigns capacity value to resources based on their contribution to reducing regional Loss of
Load Probability.

--- PAGE 2 ---
In PJM's capacity market, a Demand Response resource with 69% ELCC receives capacity payments based on
its statistical likelihood of reducing regional load during the top 10 system peak hours annually. This is
fundamentally a financial arrangement: resources that fail to perform during system peaks incur financial
penalties, and the market procures replacement capacity in subsequent annual auctions. The time horizon for
correction is measured in years, and the consequence of underperformance is economic, not physical.
Critically, PJM's ELCC calculation for Demand Response aggregates performance across the entire RTO
footprint—65,000 square miles spanning 13 states and the District of Columbia. A smart thermostat in
Philadelphia and a residential battery in northern Virginia both contribute to the same statistical pool.
Geographic diversity is an asset in this framework because it smooths weather-driven performance variation
across the region.
B. Interconnection Studies Assess Physical Power Flow at Specific Nodes
Interconnection studies serve an entirely different function: determining whether specific transmission and
distribution equipment can safely accommodate a new point-to-point power flow without exceeding thermal,
voltage, or stability limits under worst-case conditions. These studies use AC power flow models that solve
Kirchhoff's laws for specific network topology.
When a 500 MW data center interconnects at Duquesne Light's Beaver Valley substation, the interconnection
study must demonstrate that every transformer, transmission line, and protection relay between that substation
and the nearest generation sources can handle the incremental load under N-1 contingency conditions during the
local annual peak hour. The relevant question is not whether the PJM region has adequate aggregate capacity,
but whether specific physical equipment at specific geographic locations can deliver power to that specific node
under worst-case failure scenarios.
The consequences of interconnection study failure are not financial—they are physical equipment damage,
cascading outages, and potential loss of load. Utilities cannot financially settle their way out of a transformer
that has exceeded its thermal rating and suffered insulation failure.
C. The Commission Has Consistently Required Worst-Case Physical Assumptions for Interconnection
The Commission established in Order No. 2003 and reaffirmed in Order No. 2023 that interconnection studies
must model system conditions "during peak conditions" and must account for "the most severe single
contingency." The Commission rejected proposals to use probabilistic or average-case assumptions precisely
because interconnection facilities must be physically capable of handling actual worst-case power flows.
This principle reflects basic utility engineering practice: distribution and transmission equipment is sized based
on maximum credible load, not statistical averages. A transformer rated at 100 MVA cannot safely carry 150
MVA even if load averages only 70 MVA over the course of a year. The physics of thermal limits, conductor
ampacity, and insulation degradation do not respond to statistical arguments.
III. RESIDENTIAL VPP RESOURCES FAIL THE PHYSICAL REQUIREMENTS FOR
INTERCONNECTION OFFSET
Applying the Commission's established interconnection study standards reveals that residential VPP resources

--- PAGE 3 ---
cannot provide interconnection offset credit regardless of their ELCC values in Resource Adequacy markets.
The fundamental barrier is not regulatory—it is physical.
A. Locational Specificity: VPP Resources Are Geographically Dispersed Relative to Data Center Nodes
A hyperscale data center interconnecting at a specific substation requires physical capacity relief upstream of
that interconnection point in the electrical sense. In AC power flow, "upstream" means electrically closer to
generation sources or other supply points that can deliver power to the constrained node.
Residential VPP resources are necessarily dispersed across residential neighborhoods throughout a utility
service territory or RTO region. In PJM, a Demand Response aggregator might enroll 50,000 smart thermostats
distributed across Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Maryland, and Delaware. These devices provide regional load
reduction, but they are not concentrated at the specific transmission node serving the data center.
Consider a 1,000 MW data center interconnecting at American Electric Power's Clinch River substation in
Virginia. The interconnection study identifies thermal overloads on specific 345 kV transmission lines feeding
that substation under N-1 contingency. A portfolio of 1,500 MW of residential heat pumps scattered across
PJM's entire Mid-Atlantic footprint provides zero relief to those specific transmission lines. The power flow
solution for the Clinch River node does not change based on load reductions in Philadelphia or Baltimore
because those locations are electrically distant from the constraint.
This is not a question of market design or regulatory interpretation. It is a physical consequence of how power
flows on AC transmission networks according to impedance-weighted distribution across parallel paths. Load
reduction at Location A only relieves constraints at Location B if A and B are electrically proximate in the
network topology.
B. Temporal Certainty: VPP Availability Does Not Align with Local Peak Conditions
Interconnection studies must model the worst-case hour for the specific local transmission node, which may not
coincide with the regional system peak or the hours when VPP resources achieve maximum performance.
PJM's ELCC calculation for Demand Response evaluates performance during the top 10 system peak hours
annually—typically hot summer weekday afternoons when air conditioning drives regional demand. A
residential VPP program that curtails air conditioning during these hours receives high ELCC credit in the
capacity market.
However, a data center's local interconnection constraint may peak at entirely different times:
Seasonal variation: If the data center's cooling systems are more efficient in winter, local peak may
occur on cold winter mornings when regional demand is moderate but local heating load is high.
Weather pattern divergence: The specific substation serving the data center may experience peak
conditions during weather events that do not drive regional system peaks—for example, an ice storm that
disables local generation while regional conditions are mild.
Time-of-day mismatch: Industrial facilities near the data center may create local evening peaks after
regional air conditioning load has declined.

--- PAGE 4 ---
The interconnection study must ensure the local system can handle the data center load during the local worst-
case hour, not just during the regional peaks that drive ELCC calculations. A VPP resource that performs well
during PJM summer peaks provides no value if the local constraint manifests at 7 AM on a January weekday.
Proponents might argue that VPP dispatch can be optimized for local conditions, but this introduces operational
complexity that undermines the "firm capacity" assumption required for interconnection credit. If the VPP must
be dispatched differently for interconnection purposes than for Resource Adequacy purposes, it is effectively
serving two masters with potentially conflicting requirements.
C. Operational Firmness: VPP Resources Lack the Contractual and Physical Certainty Required for
Interconnection Offset
Traditional interconnection offsets—network upgrades, generation additions, or firm load reductions—share a
common characteristic: they provide physically certain and contractually enforceable relief to identified
constraints. A utility that builds a new 345 kV transmission line has absolute certainty that the line will provide
its rated capacity. A generator that signs a binding interconnection agreement provides firm injection at a
specific node.
Residential VPP resources operate under fundamentally different contractual and operational structures:
Voluntary participation: Residential customers typically enroll in VPP programs on an opt-in basis and can
withdraw at any time. A homeowner who moves, experiences equipment failure, or simply changes preferences
reduces the VPP's capacity without triggering the type of interconnection agreement breach that would occur if
a generator abandoned a project.
Performance variability: Even for participating customers, actual load reduction during any specific event
depends on baseline usage, which varies based on occupancy, weather, and behavioral factors. A smart
thermostat set to curtail air conditioning provides zero load reduction if the homeowner is on vacation and the
air conditioner was not running anyway.
Aggregation risk: VPP performance depends on statistical aggregation across thousands or tens of thousands of
individual endpoints. Portfolio performance is more stable than individual device performance, but it remains
probabilistic rather than deterministic. The same statistical diversity that makes VPPs valuable for Resource
Adequacy makes them unsuitable for interconnection, where physical certainty is required.
Dispatch limitations: Most residential VPP programs limit the frequency and duration of dispatch events to
maintain customer satisfaction. A program might guarantee only 10 events per year of 3-4 hour duration.
Interconnection relief must be available whenever local peak conditions occur, which could be dozens of times
per year depending on load patterns and contingency conditions.
These operational characteristics are acceptable and even desirable for Resource Adequacy, where the market
provides financial settlements for underperformance and procures replacement capacity over multi-year
horizons. They are fundamentally incompatible with interconnection engineering, where the utility must have
absolute certainty that identified constraints will not be exceeded under worst-case conditions.

IV. THE ATTRIBUTION GAP: QUANTIFYING THE DISCONNECT BETWEEN
REGIONAL ELCC AND LOCAL INTERCONNECTION VALUE
To quantify the magnitude of error introduced by applying regional ELCC to local interconnection, I have
developed a stochastic framework I term the "Attribution Gap"—the ratio between a VPP's regional Resource
Adequacy credit and its actual physical contribution to relieving a specific local constraint. This analysis uses
Monte Carlo simulation with empirically grounded probability distributions to capture the inherent uncertainty
in translating regional statistical performance into local physical reliability.
A. Methodology and Probability Distributions
The Attribution Gap calculation applies three sequential probabilistic filters to a VPP's nominal ELCC-based
capacity credit:
Locational Filter: What percentage of the VPP portfolio is electrically upstream of the constrained node and
can physically relieve the identified thermal or voltage constraint?
Temporal Filter: During the specific local peak hour that drives the interconnection constraint, what percentage
of the locationally-relevant VPP capacity is actually available and dispatchable?
Operational Filter: Accounting for voluntary participation, customer opt-outs, equipment failures, and dispatch
limitations, what percentage of the temporally-available capacity can be relied upon with the certainty required
for interconnection engineering?
Each filter is modeled using probability distributions selected to reflect observed behaviors in actual VPP
programs and grid operations:
1. Locational Filter: Beta(2, 10) Distribution
VPP enrollment tends to follow regional load distribution but is bounded by geography and has natural
concentration limits. The Beta distribution is appropriate because it is bounded on [0,1] and can model right-
skewed distributions common in geographic resource allocation.
Parameters were selected based on observed load distribution in regional transmission organizations. In PJM,
individual utility zones typically represent 5-25% of total RTO load, with mean approximately 15-17%. Beta(2,
10) produces:
Mean: 16.7%
Median: 14.8%
5th-95th percentile range: 3.3% - 36.5%
This reflects that while some VPP resources may be geographically proximate to any specific data center node,
the vast majority of a regional portfolio will be electrically distant and unable to provide local constraint relief.
2. Temporal Filter: Beta(8, 4) Distribution

--- PAGE 6 ---
Temporal coincidence between regional system peaks (which drive ELCC calculations) and local nodal peaks
(which drive interconnection constraints) is imperfect due to weather pattern diversity, local load composition,
and diurnal variation. However, some correlation exists because regional and local conditions are not entirely
independent.
Beta(8, 4) produces a distribution concentrated in the 50-85% range with:
Mean: 66.6%
Median: 67.6%
5th-95th percentile range: 43.4% - 86.5%
This reflects empirical observations that local peaks often coincide with regional peaks but substantial
divergence occurs due to microclimate effects, local industrial load patterns, and specific transmission topology.
3. Operational Filter: Lognormal(μ=-1.050, σ=0.45) Distribution
Operational performance involves multiplicative degradation from multiple factors:
Customer participation rates (typically 80-95% of enrolled capacity)
Equipment availability (85-98% accounting for failures and maintenance)
Dispatch limit compliance (degraded beyond guaranteed event frequency)
Baseline load uncertainty (affects actual delivered demand reduction)
The lognormal distribution is appropriate because these factors compound multiplicatively and produce right-
skewed outcomes where occasional high performance occurs but typical performance is degraded. The
distribution is capped at 85% to reflect physical maximum accounting for customer attrition and program
structure limitations.
Parameters produce:
Mean: 38.3%
Median: 35.0%
5th-95th percentile range: 16.7% - 72.9%
This reflects observed performance in voluntary residential demand response programs where guaranteed firm
capacity is substantially lower than enrolled capacity due to operational uncertainties.
B. Monte Carlo Simulation Results
Using 100,000 Monte Carlo iterations, each simulation draws random values from the three filter distributions
and calculates:
Attribution Fraction = Locational Filter × Temporal Filter × Operational Filter

This analysis reveals that a VPP portfolio with 690 MW of regional capacity credit provides a median of only

If interconnection credit were granted at the full regional ELCC value of 690 MW, the utility would be
approving a data center interconnection based on the assumption that 690 MW of local capacity relief exists
when the actual physical relief is most likely in the 30-50 MW range. The resulting 640+ MW shortfall would
manifest as thermal overloads, voltage violations, or cascading outages when the data center operates at full
load during local peak conditions.

From an interconnection engineering perspective, this represents unacceptable reliability risk. Utilities size

F. Comparison to Deterministic Analysis
The Monte Carlo results validate and strengthen the deterministic analysis presented in advocacy comments but
reveal even greater attribution degradation than simple multiplicative calculations might suggest.
A deterministic calculation using the mean values of each filter would produce:
Deterministic Attribution = 16.7% × 66.6% × 38.3% = 4.26%
This closely matches the Monte Carlo mean of 4.25%, providing validation of the simulation methodology.
However, the Monte Carlo approach reveals additional critical insights that deterministic analysis cannot
capture:
Distributional shape: The attribution distribution is highly right-skewed, meaning median performance
(3.26%) is substantially lower than mean performance (4.25%). For interconnection engineering, which requires
worst-case analysis, the median or lower percentiles are more relevant than the mean.
Tail risk: The 5th percentile attribution of 0.62% reveals that in 1-in-20 scenarios, actual interconnection value
could be less than 1% of regional ELCC credit—a 100:1 overestimation. Such extreme scenarios might result
from coincident failures: unfavorable weather creating geographic concentration of VPP unavailability, local
peak occurring during atypical hours, and customer response degradation during extended heat events.
Correlation structure: The simulation reveals how simultaneous degradation across multiple filters produces
compounding effects. When geographic dispersion is unfavorable, it tends to correlate with lower operational
performance because remote VPP resources experience different weather patterns and may have different
customer demographics that affect program participation.

G. Implications for Interconnection Policy
The Monte Carlo analysis demonstrates that the Attribution Gap is not a minor technical correction factor—it
represents an order-of-magnitude discrepancy between regional statistical performance and local physical
reliability.
This finding has profound implications for any proposal to use ELCC-based interconnection credits:
Physical reliability cannot be probabilistic: Unlike Resource Adequacy, where underperformance is addressed
through financial penalties and multi-year procurement cycles, interconnection failures manifest immediately as
equipment damage and service interruptions. The Commission's worst-case study requirements in Order No.
2023 reflect this fundamental distinction.
Geography dominates: The strong correlation between the Locational Filter and overall Attribution Gap
indicates that even substantial improvements in VPP operational performance or dispatch optimization cannot
overcome the fundamental barrier of geographic dispersion. Residential VPP resources are inherently
distributed across broad regions; data centers are located at specific nodes.
Attribution Gap is structural, not transient: The distribution parameters used in this analysis reflect mature
VPP programs operating under best practices. The 4-6% median attribution is not a temporary limitation that
will disappear with program refinement—it is an inherent consequence of applying regional statistical resources
to local deterministic requirements.
Even optimistic scenarios are insufficient: At the 95th percentile, Attribution Gap reaches only 16.3%. This
means that even under highly favorable conditions—geographic concentration, perfect temporal alignment, and
exceptional operational performance—a VPP provides less than one-sixth of its regional ELCC value for
interconnection purposes. This is still insufficient to support meaningful interconnection offsets without risking
reliability violations.
The Commission should recognize that the Attribution Gap quantifies a fundamental category error: attempting
to use financial accounting mechanisms (Resource Adequacy ELCC) to satisfy physical engineering
requirements (interconnection capacity). No amount of regulatory creativity or program optimization can bridge
this gap because it reflects the underlying physics of AC power flow and the geographic realities of resource
distribution.
V. POLICY IMPLICATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A. Resource Adequacy Metrics Cannot Substitute for Interconnection Engineering Analysis
The Commission should reject proposals to grant automatic interconnection offset credit based on Resource
Adequacy ELCC values. These metrics measure fundamentally different grid reliability functions and operate
under incompatible analytical frameworks.
Resource Adequacy ELCC reflects a resource's contribution to regional statistical reliability—a valuable
function that deserves compensation through capacity markets. Interconnection credit requires demonstration of

--- PAGE 11 ---
physical capacity relief at specific nodes under worst-case conditions—a deterministic engineering requirement
that cannot be satisfied through regional statistical analysis.
Conflating these frameworks would establish dangerous precedent that market-based metrics can override
engineering-based safety standards. The Commission has consistently held that interconnection studies must
reflect actual physical system conditions, and this principle should not be compromised to accommodate
advocacy for specific technologies or resources.
B. VPPs Can Contribute to Interconnection, But Only Through Rigorous Locational Analysis
This comment should not be interpreted as opposition to VPP participation in interconnection solutions.
Distributed Energy Resources, including properly structured VPP programs, can provide genuine value for
managing local distribution constraints and potentially specific transmission constraints under carefully defined
conditions.
However, such contributions must be demonstrated through the same rigorous interconnection study
methodology applied to all other resources:
Locational specificity: The VPP portfolio must be geographically concentrated electrically upstream of the
identified constraint, with power flow modeling demonstrating actual constraint relief.
Temporal certainty: VPP availability must be guaranteed during the specific local peak hours that drive the
constraint, not just regional system peaks.
Operational firmness: VPP performance must be backed by binding contractual commitments with financial
penalties for non-performance sufficient to procure physical backup capacity.
Verification and measurement: VPP performance must be subject to metering and verification protocols
equivalent to generation interconnections, with real-time telemetry and after-the-fact settlement.
Such requirements would likely necessitate commercial and industrial VPP resources with firm contractual
commitments rather than voluntary residential programs. The resulting Attribution Gap might approach 40-60%
rather than the 5-15% typical for residential programs, potentially justifying meaningful interconnection credit.
C. The Commission Should Establish Clear Boundaries Between Market Mechanisms
More broadly, this docket presents an opportunity for the Commission to clarify the appropriate boundaries
between different grid reliability mechanisms:
Resource Adequacy markets should continue to value resources based on regional statistical contribution,
using ELCC or similar methodologies. These markets provide appropriate compensation for VPPs' genuine
reliability value.
Interconnection studies should continue to apply deterministic engineering analysis based on worst-case local
conditions. Resources seeking interconnection offset credit must demonstrate physical constraint relief through
power flow modeling.

--- PAGE 12 ---
Operating reserves and ancillary services provide a third category where VPPs may contribute, subject to
appropriate telemetry, response time, and verification requirements.
Maintaining clear analytical boundaries between these mechanisms protects both reliability and market
efficiency. Blurring these boundaries to expedite specific interconnections would ultimately undermine both
goals.
VI. CONCLUSION
Virtual Power Plants represent valuable grid resources that contribute meaningfully to regional Resource
Adequacy. However, their statistical performance across multi-state footprints cannot be translated into firm
capacity relief at specific transmission nodes without violating basic engineering principles and the
Commission's own precedent regarding interconnection study requirements.
The temptation to apply Resource Adequacy metrics to interconnection challenges is understandable given the
urgency of data center queue backlogs. But the Commission has consistently held that reliability shortcuts in
interconnection create long-term risks that outweigh short-term expediency. Equipment thermal limits, voltage
constraints, and protection coordination requirements reflect physical realities that cannot be compromised
through creative regulatory interpretation.
The Monte Carlo analysis presented in Section IV demonstrates that the Attribution Gap between regional
ELCC credit and local interconnection value is not a minor correction factor—it represents order-of-magnitude
overestimation in virtually all scenarios. A VPP portfolio with 690 MW of regional capacity credit provides
median interconnection value of only 33 MW, with 93% probability of less than 100 MW actual contribution.
This is not speculation or conservative assumption—it is the mathematical consequence of applying three
empirically grounded filters that reflect actual VPP program characteristics and power flow physics.
VPPs should continue to receive appropriate compensation through capacity markets for their genuine Resource
Adequacy contributions. Data centers should continue to fund interconnection upgrades necessary to maintain
physical system reliability. And the Commission should resist pressure to conflate these distinct reliability
functions, regardless of how sophisticated the advocacy or how compelling the policy objectives.
The grid's physical reliability depends on maintaining this distinction.
