On behalf of the Texas Blockchain Council (TBC), a 501(c)(6) trade association representing the nation’s leading bitcoin mining and AI compute operators and grid-supportive data center developers, we appreciate the Department of Energy’s leadership in seeking input on how the United States can efficiently integrate critical large-load infrastructure into the electric grid.

High-performance compute loads, including artificial intelligence, bitcoin mining, and advanced research processing are rapidly becoming essential to economic competitiveness, national security, and the modernization of industrial supply chains. Reliable and timely interconnection of these resources is crucial not only for the Texas economy, but for the United States’ strategic technological leadership.

A New Pathway: Non-Firm Interconnection for Flexible Loads

The largest friction point in the deployment of high-density data center infrastructure is the slow and uncertain nature of interconnection processes designed for traditional, always-on, critical load. However, not all large loads behave the same way. Digital-compute data centers, particularly bitcoin mining and, in the future, some emerging AI compute installations, can operate as large, flexible, interruptible loads with the capability to modulate power consumption within seconds in response to grid demands.

To unlock this flexibility for the benefit of the grid, we recommend that DOE support the creation and adoption of a non-firm load interconnection category across U.S. markets.

A model already exists: Southwest Power Pool’s (SPP) High-Impact Large Loads (HILLs) framework enables “fast-track” interconnection for large loads that agree to curtail when needed to preserve reliability. Texas and ERCOT face similar pressures and would benefit greatly from a standardized pathway like this.

Why Non-Firm Load Status Works

Adopting a non-firm interconnection category would:

Cut interconnection delays dramatically

Flexible load does not require the same level of transmission upgrades as other businesses and often locates at or near existing power sources. This reduces required studies, transmission upgrades, and queue congestion.

Improve reliability through fast-acting demand response

Bitcoin mining facilities in Texas have already demonstrated real-time load shed performance ahead of grid emergencies, contributing meaningful stability during both extreme heat and winter reliability events.

Increase transmission and distribution and generator revenue

In instances where utilities do not have to build more transmission to connect flexible loads, they can increase revenue on the same infrastructure, wires, and substations. Prior to TDSP rate adjustments, this increased revenue can, in turn, help pay for additional transmission projects assuming guidelines have been developed outlining what transmission charges apply to these loads when they are co-located, at the same POI.

Lower consumer costs

When these loads are exposed to real-time prices and congestion signals, they shift consumption away from peak periods using a variety of market-based tools, reducing uplift charges and lessening the need for emergency procurement.

Encourage co-location with new generation and grid-enhancing technologies

Flexible loads can serve as anchor tenants for new generation projects and unlock financing for
transmission-constrained generation pockets.

Recommendations to DOE

We respectfully request that the Department of Energy:

● Encourage FERC-jurisdictional ISOs and RTOs as well as ERCOT to establish a uniform,
non-firm interconnection category for qualifying flexible loads.
● Expand coordination with NERC and reliability entities to ensure transparent, achievable
performance criteria for fast-acting interruptible load. Standards should focus on both
strengthening grid resilience on the supply side and market-based approaches to
encourage demand-side resources.
● Highlight private-sector case studies, including Texas, demonstrating how bitcoin
compute is already providing measurable grid-balancing services.

DOE leadership in this direction will accelerate the deployment of critical digital infrastructure
while strengthening reliability and affordability, which is a strategic win for the United States.

Conclusion

The U.S. is in the early stages of a generational buildout of energy-intensive, next generation compute. We can choose to treat this growth as a strain on the grid, or as the tool that helps modernize it. Flexible data center loads, enabled by non-firm interconnection pathways, offer a once-in-a-generation opportunity to future-proof the American power system.

We appreciate the Department’s willingness to engage stakeholders and would welcome the
opportunity to provide additional technical details through workshops or testimony.
