COMMENTS OF NORTHEASTERN REMC  
 
Northeastern Rural Electric Membership Corporation (“NREMC”) respectfully submits 
these comments in response to the Commission's Notice Inviting Comments on the Advanced 
Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (“ANOPR”) proposed by the Secretary of Energy on October 
23, 2025.  
NREMC appreciates the opportunity to comment on the important issues highlighted in the 
proposed ANOPR and commends the Commission’s efforts to navigate the impacts of large load 
additions at this critical point in time. It is essential that the integration of large loads occur in a 
manner that does not discriminate against the size and timing of load showing up on the grid, while 
also not jeopardizing system reliability.  
 
I. 
INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND 
NREMC provides these comments from the perspective of a non-profit, member-owned 
electric distribution cooperative organized and existing pursuant to the laws of the State of Indiana 
within the PJM region. NREMC serves a peak load of 350 MW, supplying electric power to more 
than 35,000 residential, commercial, and industrial member-consumers in northeast Indiana. 
NREMC supplies power through a significant behind-the-meter generation fleet consisting of

battery energy storage systems, solar, and natural gas facilities, as well as participation in the PJM 
energy, capacity, and ancillary services markets as a Load Serving Entity (“LSE”).  
 
II. 
COMMENTS 
NREMC appreciates the Secretary’s attention to developing a framework for the timely 
interconnection of large loads while ensuring grid reliability. The ANOPR asserts that the 
Commission has jurisdiction over large load interconnections because, like generator 
interconnections, they are “a critical component of open access transmission service.”1 The 
ANOPR argues that similar to exercising its authority to remedy undue discrimination by 
requiring public utilities to adopt standardized interconnection procedures and agreements for 
generators, standardized procedures are necessary for large loads, including hybrid facilities, to 
ensure open and non-discriminatory access to the transmission system.2  
Commission jurisdiction over generator interconnections as a component of interstate 
transmission service does not, by itself, support an inherent grant of jurisdiction over large-load 
interconnections. Should the Commission determine otherwise and move forward with this 
rulemaking, it must do so within the express limitations of its statutory authority. Commission 
jurisdiction cannot override state authority over retail sales, confer any delivery rights under the 
Open Access Transmission Tariff (“OATT”), or create a federal right to interconnection that 
would allow large loads to bypass state-established service territories or retail-choice restrictions.  
Granting a federal right of interconnection to large loads could enable these loads to 
obtain de facto retail access to alternative suppliers across the transmission system, even in states

Under these considerations, NREMC provides the following initial comments on some 
selected principles from the ANOPR:  
A. Principle 2 – Large Load Definition  
The second principle of ANOPR states that the Commission’s reforms, like its pro forma 
LGIP and LGIA, “should only apply to new loads greater than 20 MW and, for hybrid facilities, 
where the load is greater than 20 MW.”3 
Establishing a minimum size threshold for which “large load” reforms apply is necessary, 
but a uniform, nationwide bright-line standard at 20 MW is far too low and would include many 
manufacturing and industrial loads essential for communities across nation. These types of loads 
bear little resemblance in scale, behavior, or system impact to the concern driving this 
proceeding: extraordinarily large, highly concentrated data-center demand measured in the 
hundreds of megawatts or even gigawatts, at a single point of interconnection. The minimum 
threshold should more closely reflect these loads of concern and should be set at no less than 100 
MW. Smaller or more distributed loads present fundamentally different characteristics and risk 
profiles. Their electrical demands, operational behavior, and location on the system do not 
produce the same system-level challenges that hyper-concentrated data-center growth creates.

Applying the same interconnection requirements to these very different categories of load would 
impose unnecessary barriers on beneficial local development. 
B. Principle 3 – Studying load and Hybrid Facilities with Generation 
NREMC agrees that, to the extent practicable, studying load and hybrid facilities together 
with new generating facilities can support more efficient siting decisions and reduce the need for 
costly network upgrades. Co-locating a large load front-of-meter with newly developed 
generation, not generation pulled from the existing supply stack, can meaningfully reduce the 
overall interconnection footprint and limit the transmission reinforcements that would otherwise 
be required if each facility were studied independently.  
All load and generation configurations, including hybrid arrangements, should 
interconnect on a front-of-the-meter basis, with full transparency regarding both the load and the 
generating components. Transparent, front-of-the-meter interconnection ensures that 
transmission providers can accurately study system impacts (including contingency scenarios at 
the hybrid facilities), appropriately allocate costs, and maintain clear operational visibility. This 
clarity is essential for maintaining reliability as the system evolves and as new types of load-
generation pairings emerge.  
C. Principle 7 – Prioritization for Interconnection of Curtailable Large Loads 
The principle that “the interconnection study of large loads that agree to be curtailable 
and hybrid facilities that agree to be curtailable and dispatchable should be expedited”4 is 
problematic. Expediting interconnection studies solely for large loads or hybrid facilities that 
agree to be curtailable and dispatchable would create a discriminatory two-tiered system, 
favoring certain customers over others based on operational preferences rather than system

needs. Data centers often claim “national security” imperatives that prevent curtailment, making 
such distinctions impractical.  
A “fast-track” interconnection process should instead focus on facilities paired with new, 
front-of-meter generation, where operational flexibility and system benefits can be clearly 
demonstrated. This approach maintains fairness, supports reliable integration, and avoids 
preferential treatment that could distort the interconnection queue. 
D. Principle 9 – Equal Opportunity for Interconnection Customers  
Interconnection customers who are not transmission owners should absolutely be 
afforded the same or equivalent option to build as generator interconnection customers. 
Transmission owners should not be permitted to delay or impede the interconnection process if 
the customer has the capability to construct the necessary facilities themselves. Allowing 
interconnection customers to exercise this option ensures timely project development, maintains 
a level playing field, and prevents unnecessary bottlenecks in the interconnection process. 
E. Principle 12 – Ancillary Services 
NREMC agrees that utilities serving large loads should be responsible for ancillary 
services based on the gross peak load, without netting against any co-located generation. Even 
where generation is co-located, the system must still be prepared to serve the full load at any 
time, maintaining the ability to provide black start, regulation, reactive power, and other essential 
services. All grid users benefit from having these services available to ensure stability, reliability, 
and balanced operation, irrespective of whether co-located generation is present or utilized.  
Co-location does not reduce the system’s obligations or the risk the load imposes, and 
charges for ancillary services should reflect the full potential need to deploy these capabilities.

This approach ensures that costs are appropriately aligned with the actual drivers of ancillary 
service requirements and supports consistent, reliable system operation. 
F. Principle 13 – Implementation Plan 
Implementation of any proposed reforms must be supported by a well-defined plan and 
transition framework. The ANOPR appropriately recognizes that any set of reforms adopted in 
this proceeding will require a defined and coordinated implementation plan that occurs over a 
reasonable transition period. Any proposed changes will need to be aligned with existing 
generator-interconnection procedures, transmission-service request processes, and ongoing 
regional transmission-planning activities. Absent a clear transition framework, transmission 
providers and customers could face conflicting requirements, duplicative studies, or delays that 
undermine the very efficiencies these reforms are intended to achieve. 
RTOs and ISOs, transmission providers, and load serving entities must have sufficient 
time to update their procedures, software, staffing, and coordination protocols before any new 
requirements take effect. Moreover, regional flexibility is necessary to account for differences in 
queue structures, study methodologies, and the status of current large-load interconnection 
requests. A well-designed, regionally informed transition plan will be absolutely essential to 
preserve momentum on active studies and maintain system reliability throughout the transition 
period. 
 
III. 
CONCLUSION 
NREMC respectfully requests that any Commission action in this proceeding reflect the 
considerations and recommendations outlined in these comments.
